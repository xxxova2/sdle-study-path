A. White & red lesions

A1. Why this section pays

  • Banks test one hinge: wipes off or not, then red vs white risk, then next step (reassure / antifungal / biopsy / refer). You do not need a path residency — you need discriminators.
  • Clinical white patches are a family, not one disease. Labeling every white patch “leukoplakia and watch” is how people miss erythroplakia and thrush traps.
  • Study mode: each bullet is a stem hinge. If you cannot restate the discriminator without looking, the block is not done.

A2. Leukoplakia (clinical term)

  • Leukoplakia (clinical): a white patch that cannot be wiped off and is not explained by another defined disease (not candidiasis, not frictional keratosis with clear cause you accept, not lichen planus with classic pattern, etc.). It is a clinical label, not a histologic diagnosis.
  • Histology after biopsy may show hyperkeratosis only, dysplasia, carcinoma in situ, or invasive SCC. You cannot know from color alone.
  • Risk rises with: thickness / homogeneous vs non-homogeneous pattern, red component, tobacco and alcohol, high-risk sites (floor of mouth, ventral/lateral tongue, soft palate / oropharynx risk zones), long duration, older age, male smoker pattern classic but anyone can present.
  • Homogeneous flat white is lower risk than non-homogeneous / speckled / nodular / verrucous patterns — still may need biopsy based on site and risk.
  • Management concept: remove risk factors + biopsy when suspicious or when diagnosis is needed; do not “reassure forever” a high-risk white/red lesion.

A3. Candidiasis vs non-wipe whites

  • Pseudomembranous candidiasis (thrush): creamy white plaques that wipe off leaving a red or bleeding base. Classic after broad-spectrum antibiotics, inhaled steroids (no rinse after), immunosuppression, xerostomia, denture wear, uncontrolled diabetes, infancy/elderly frailty.
  • Exam hinge: wipes off → thrush pathway (antifungal + risk factor fix). Does not wipe → not thrush as the wipe-off diagnosis.
  • Erythematous candidiasis may be red without thick white plaques (denture stomatitis palate, median rhomboid tongue pattern). Still Candida-related thinking + denture hygiene.
  • Angular cheilitis often Candida ± staph; fix vertical dimension / denture height / moisture when contributing.

A4. Erythroplakia and speckled patterns (higher risk)

  • Erythroplakia: velvety red patch that cannot be attributed to another disease. Very high rate of severe dysplasia or carcinoma compared with homogeneous leukoplakia. Exam gold: erythroplakia is higher risk than leukoplakia.
  • Do not watch forever. Biopsy / specialist pathway. Photograph and refer urgently when classic.
  • Speckled leukoplakia (erythroleukoplakia): mixed red-white — higher risk than pure homogeneous white. Biopsy the worst (often red) area.
  • Any white/red lesion with induration, ulceration, fixed nodes, unexplained numbness, or rapid growth → cancer workup priority, not “try nystatin first for weeks.”

A5. Not every white patch is leukoplakia

  • Frictional / factitial keratosis: white at site of chronic trauma (sharp tooth, cheek biting). Fix cause; may resolve. If persists after trauma removed or looks suspicious → reassess/biopsy.
  • Linea alba: horizontal white line on buccal mucosa at occlusal plane — common, benign pattern.
  • Leukoedema: diffuse gray-white buccal mucosa that fades/disappears on stretching — classic bank discriminator. Reassurance.
  • Hairy leukoplakia: usually lateral tongue, corrugated white, does not wipe; EBV-associated; immunocompromise (HIV classic). Not thrush. Medical workup context.
  • Geographic tongue (benign migratory glossitis): migrating red patches with white borders; burning sometimes; usually reassure + avoid triggers; not cancer workup first-line when classic migrating pattern.
  • White sponge nevus / hereditary whites: rare stems; lifelong, familial, not progressive cancer pathway like erythroplakia.

A6. Exam stem patterns (white/red)

  • “Creamy plaques wipe off leaving red base after antibiotics” → pseudomembranous candidiasis.
  • “White patch cannot wipe, smoker, floor of mouth” → clinical leukoplakia pathway → risk + biopsy consideration.
  • “Velvety red patch floor of mouth” → erythroplakia → high risk → biopsy/refer, not endless watch.
  • “Gray-white buccal mucosa fades when stretched” → leukoedema.
  • “Lateral tongue white corrugated plaques, HIV, does not wipe” → hairy leukoplakia (EBV), not thrush.
  • “Mixed red-white speckled patch” → higher risk than pure white → biopsy worst area.

B. Lichen planus & blistering flags

B1. Oral lichen planus (OLP)

  • Classic reticular OLP: bilateral white Wickham striae on buccal mucosa (lacy network). Often asymptomatic; diagnosis often clinical when classic bilateral pattern.
  • Erosive / atrophic / ulcerative OLP: pain, burning, red raw areas, desquamative gingivitis pattern possible. Needs active management (topical steroids common teaching) + follow-up.
  • OLP is chronic; erosive forms need surveillance awareness because of small malignant transformation risk discussion in exams (not every LP is cancer, but do not ignore changing lesions).
  • Bilateral classic striae ≠ unilateral white next to a new amalgam/gold/composite only — that may be lichenoid contact reaction.

B2. Lichenoid reactions

  • Contact lichenoid: often unilateral, adjacent to restorative material (amalgam classic). Consider material replacement after diagnosis pathway if indicated.
  • Drug lichenoid: antihypertensives (some), antimalarials, NSAIDs, others appear in lists — correlate timing with meds; physician coordination to change drug if needed.
  • Unilateral “LP” next to restoration → think lichenoid reaction before calling idiopathic bilateral disease.

B3. Pemphigus vulgaris flags

  • Autoimmune against desmogleins (desmosomes) → intraepithelial split → flaccid bullae that rupture easily → painful erosions.
  • Oral lesions often first; may precede skin. Nikolsky concept (gentle pressure extends/sloughs) appears in stems.
  • Exam action: recognize as urgent oral medicine / dermatology pathway — not “mouthwash and review in 6 months.” Biopsy ± DIF as directed by specialist protocols.

B4. Mucous membrane pemphigoid (MMP) flags

  • Autoimmune against basement membrane zone → subepithelial split → tense bullae more than flaccid, then erosions; desquamative gingivitis classic.
  • Scarring risk especially ocular mucosa — if MMP suspected, eye referral awareness is a free point. Do not ignore red eyes / symblepharon history.
  • DIF / biopsy special handling (perilesional tissue, correct medium) — coordinate; do not destroy sample with wrong fixative for DIF needs.

B5. Desquamative gingivitis differential

  • Clinical description: peeling, erythematous, painful gingiva — not a single disease name.
  • DDx: erosive LP, MMP, pemphigus, plasma cell gingivitis / allergy, rarely other. History of skin/eye lesions matters.
  • When autoimmune blistering suspected → specialist + biopsy ± immunofluorescence rather than endless polishing alone.

B6. Exam stem patterns (LP / blistering)

  • “Bilateral buccal lacy white striae, mild” → reticular lichen planus pattern.
  • “Unilateral white-red next to large amalgam” → lichenoid contact reaction pathway.
  • “Painful oral erosions first, flaccid bullae, positive Nikolsky” → pemphigus flags → urgent specialist.
  • “Desquamative gingivitis + ocular scarring concern” → MMP flags → include eye referral concept.
  • “Raw gingiva only, peeling, chronic” → desquamative gingivitis DDx, not always “just plaque” if classic autoimmune story.

STOP 40 sec: “LP striae bilateral. Unilateral next to filling → lichenoid. Flaccid/Nikolsky → pemphigus. Tense/scar/eye → MMP. Desquamative gingivitis = DDx not one disease.”

C. Biopsy logic

C1. Choose the right sample, not random cutting

  • Goal: give the pathologist a specimen that answers the clinical question without destroying the ability to do definitive surgery later when cancer is suspected.
  • Label site, orientation if needed, clinical photo helps. Do not freeze or desiccate a soft-tissue specimen intended for routine histopathology — place in appropriate fixative (formalin classic for H&E) promptly.
  • For suspected autoimmune blistering needing DIF, special transport medium (not always routine formalin) — know that DIF has special handling; when unsure, call the lab/oral med before you cut.

C2. Excisional vs incisional

  • Excisional biopsy: remove the entire lesion. Best for small, accessible, clinically benign-appearing lesions you can remove completely without mutilation (e.g., small fibroma, small mucocele in selected cases).
  • Incisional biopsy: remove a representative portion of a large lesion or any lesion suspicious for malignancy when complete removal would be extensive surgery that should wait for diagnosis. Include the worst-looking area (red zone, indurated edge, non-healing ulcer edge — not only necrotic center).
  • Classic trap: huge suspicious tongue ulcer → do not try “total glossectomy-style excisional” as first move; incisional then definitive plan after path.
  • Classic trap: tiny pedunculated fibroma on buccal mucosa → excisional is appropriate, not “tiny bite that leaves most of it.”

C3. FNA and cytology limits

  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA): useful first step for many salivary swellings, neck nodes, some deep masses — especially when open excision would be premature (e.g., bilateral parotid enlargement patterns, node workup).
  • FNA is not a substitute for scalpel biopsy of a high-risk mucosal white/red patch when surface dysplasia/SCC is the question.
  • Exfoliative / brush cytology: samples superficial cells only. Adjunct at best. Negative cytology does not rule out malignancy. Never use a negative brush as permission to ignore a suspicious erythroplakia.

C4. What to send and what not to destroy

  • Include adequate depth for suspected invasion questions when safe; shallow scrape of a deep lesion fails the patient.
  • Avoid crushing with forceps on the diagnostic edge. Avoid electrocautery char on the entire diagnostic surface.
  • Multiple sites if multifocal high-risk disease — document each pot separately.
  • Refer early when lesion is in dangerous anatomic zones, patient medically complex, or you lack biopsy comfort — delaying cancer diagnosis is an ethics + clinical failure.

C5. Exam stem patterns (biopsy)

  • “5 mm pedunculated soft nodule buccal, years, no growth” → often excisional.
  • “3 cm indurated ulcer lateral tongue, smoker” → incisional of representative/worst area, not brush only.
  • “Bilateral painless parotid swellings” → often FNA / medical workup first, not radical bilateral excision day one.
  • “Brush cytology negative, still velvety red patch” → still needs proper biopsy; cytology ≠ clearance.
  • “Suspected pemphigoid, need immunofluorescence” → special medium / correct technique, not wrong fixative for DIF.

STOP 35 sec: “Small simple complete remove = excisional. Large/suspicious = incisional worst area. FNA glands/nodes. Brush not cancer clearance. Label + correct medium.”

WRITE GATE: small fibroma → ___; large suspicious tongue ulcer → ___; parotid swelling first step often → ___; negative brush on erythroplakia → ___; DIF needs → ___.

D. Common cysts

D1. Radicular (periapical) cyst

  • Most common odontogenic cyst. Linked to a non-vital tooth (pulp necrosis pathway) with apical inflammatory stimulus.
  • Radiograph: apical radiolucency continuous with lamina dura loss pattern of the source tooth. Tooth tests non-vital (with multi-test caveats).
  • Treatment core: treat the source — endodontic therapy or extraction ± enucleation of residual lesion as indicated. You do not “marsupialize forever” a classic small radicular without source control.
  • Vitality discriminates vs many other apical lucencies next to vital teeth (COD, etc.).

D2. Dentigerous (follicular) cyst

  • Forms around the crown of an unerupted tooth (classic mandibular third molar or maxillary canine).
  • Radiograph: radiolucency attached at CEJ region enclosing crown. Can displace teeth, expand bone, rarely associate with other pathology — treat and submit tissue.
  • Management: enucleation with removal of associated tooth often; large lesions may be marsupialized first to decompress then definitive care — exam loves marsupialization for large dentigerous concept.

D3. Odontogenic keratocyst (OKC / KCOT)

  • Behavior historically aggressive with higher recurrence after simple enucleation than many cysts; may be multilocular; posterior mandible / ramus classic.
  • Multiple OKCs → think nevoid basal cell carcinoma syndrome (Gorlin / NBCCS) pathway — exam association free point.
  • Management often specialist: careful enucleation ± adjuncts, long follow-up. Do not treat as “harmless radicular” on a vital tooth with multilocular ramus lesion.

D4. Residual, nasopalatine, and quick DDx

  • Residual cyst: inflammatory cyst left after extraction of the offending tooth — history of prior XO at site + residual lucency.
  • Nasopalatine duct cyst: midline anterior maxilla (incisive canal region); teeth often vital (vs radicular of central). Heart-shaped classic description sometimes. Treat surgically when indicated; vitality saves you from wrong endo on both centrals.
  • Lateral periodontal cyst: along lateral root of vital tooth, often mandibular canine/premolar region — vitality helps vs radicular.
  • Always couple cyst thinking with vitality + location + unerupted tooth presence.

D5. Exam stem patterns (cysts)

  • “Apical lucency, non-vital molar” → radicular pathway → source control (endo/XO).
  • “Lucency around crown of unerupted 8” → dentigerous → enucleation ± tooth; large → marsupialize concept.
  • “Multilocular posterior mandible, high recurrence history in stem” → OKC thinking; multiple → NBCCS.
  • “Midline anterior maxilla lucency, vital centrals” → nasopalatine, not dual RCT automatically.
  • “Lucency in old extraction site, history of non-vital tooth removed” → residual cyst.

STOP 40 sec: “Radicular = nonvital apex. Dentigerous = unerupted crown. OKC = recurrence ± multilocular ± NBCCS if multiple. Nasopalatine = midline maxilla, teeth vital. Residual = left after XO.”

E. Bone pattern recognition

E1. Paget disease of bone (osteitis deformans) — jaws

  • Classic radiograph word: cotton-wool mixed radiopaque patches. May enlarge jaws, spacing of teeth, hypercementosis, ill-fitting dentures over time.
  • Lab teaching point: elevated alkaline phosphatase with other context; polyostotic possible.
  • Surgical caution: increased vascularity / bleeding risk in active phases; healing considerations. Many patients may also have antiresorptive history for treatment → layer MRONJ awareness when extractions planned.
  • Exam photo/stem: cotton-wool skull/jaws → Paget until proven otherwise in bank language.

E2. Fibrous dysplasia

  • Classic radiograph word: ground-glass (orange-peel) pattern, poorly defined blending with normal bone; expansion of jaw; young patients often; monostotic jaws common.
  • Teeth may displace; occlusion change; facial asymmetry. Histology/clinical correlation; avoid outdated “radiation for FD” thinking (malignancy risk historically).
  • Management often contour / observation depending on growth and function — not the same cotton-wool label as Paget.

E3. Cemento-osseous dysplasia (COD)

  • Often middle-aged women; periapical regions of mandibular anteriors (periapical COD) or florid multi-quadrant patterns.
  • Teeth typically vital — do not RCT every lucent apex that is early COD. Stages move from lucent → mixed → radiopaque.
  • Dense mature lesions have poor vascularity → avoid unnecessary extraction/biopsy/surgery when radiographic diagnosis is classic; risk of non-healing and infection/sequestrum.
  • Exam trap: asymptomatic mixed radiopacities at vital lower incisors in a 45-year-old woman → COD, not automatic endo on all four.

E4. Malignancy and other red flags

  • Rapid expansion, paresthesia, tooth mobility out of proportion, irregular PDL widening, sunburst / onion-skin periosteal reactions (age-dependent tumors/infection DDx), non-healing extraction socket → urgent imaging + specialist, not 6-month recall polish.
  • Metastasis to jaws possible in adults with cancer history — ask systemic history.
  • Do not force every mixed opacity into Paget if the patient is a young monostotic ground-glass case (FD) or vital anterior COD.

E5. Exam stem patterns (bone)

  • “Cotton-wool radiopacities, enlarged jaw, high ALP” → Paget.
  • “Teen, ground-glass expansion maxilla” → fibrous dysplasia.
  • “Middle-aged woman, vital lower incisors, mixed periapical opacities” → COD — avoid unnecessary XO/endo.
  • “Rapid numb lip + irregular bone destruction” → malignancy pathway / urgent referral.

STOP 30 sec: “Paget = cotton-wool. FD = ground glass. COD = vital teeth, don’t cut needlessly. Numb + destroy = bad.”

F. MRONJ vs osteoradionecrosis

F1. Shared clinical picture, different cause

  • Both can present as non-healing exposed jaw bone. The exam splits them by history: drugs vs radiation. Mixing labels loses the point.

F2. MRONJ (medication-related osteonecrosis of the jaw)

  • Associated with antiresorptives: bisphosphonates, denosumab, and sometimes antiangiogenic agents in cancer care.
  • High-level definition pattern: exposed bone (or fistula probing to bone) in the jaw lasting >8 weeks, current/prior antiresorptive or antiangiogenic therapy, and no history of radiation to the jaws (and not metastatic disease explaining the exposure).
  • Risk higher with IV oncology dosing, longer duration, steroids, poor OH, invasive dental surgery (extractions).
  • Prevention > cure: optimize dentistry before starting therapy when possible; prefer non-surgical options; atraumatic technique; antimicrobial rinses; specialist for high-risk extractions. Never freestyle-stop cancer drugs without oncology agreement.
  • Stage-based management: CHX, antibiotics if infection, smooth sharp bone, debridement/resection advanced — coordinate oncology/OMS.

F3. Osteoradionecrosis (ORN)

  • History of therapeutic radiation to the jaws/head-neck field. Hypovascular, hypocellular bone heals poorly.
  • Risk with post-RT extractions, trauma, poor OH, high dose, mandible often more than maxilla.
  • Pre-RT dental clearance is the free-point prevention: extract hopeless teeth, optimize OH, fluoride trays concepts for xerostomia caries — before radiation when possible.
  • After RT: avoid extractions if possible; endo/restorative salvage preferred; if XO unavoidable → specialist protocols (hyperbaric oxygen appears in some older/variable protocols — know concept exists, do not invent freestyle).

F4. Exam split table (memorize)

  • Drug history (bisphosphonate/denosumab) + exposed bone + no RT → MRONJ.
  • Radiation history + exposed bone → ORN.
  • Both need prevention, OH, cautious surgery, specialist when advanced. Labels still differ.

F5. Exam stem patterns

  • “IV zoledronate for cancer, exposed mandible bone 3 months, never radiated” → MRONJ.
  • “History of H&N RT, nonhealing socket after XO” → ORN pathway.
  • “About to start IV bisphosphonate, many hopeless teeth” → clear dental disease before therapy when possible.
  • “Post-RT patient needs extraction” → avoid if possible; specialist planning — not casual XO.

STOP 30 sec: “Drugs → MRONJ. Radiation → ORN. Prevention first. Don’t stop cancer drugs alone. Pre-RT dental clearance.”

WRITE GATE: denosumab + exposed bone no RT → ___; H&N RT + exposed bone → ___; before IV antiresorptive → ___; post-RT prefer → ___ over casual XO.

G. Pregnancy lesion + medical free points

G1. Pyogenic granuloma (pregnancy tumor / granuloma gravidarum)

  • Reactive vascular nodule, often gingiva, bleeds easily, grows with plaque + hormonal influence in pregnancy.
  • If asymptomatic / late pregnancy / not interfering severely → often observe + OH + debridement; many regress postpartum.
  • Excise if severe bleeding, function/esthetics demand, or persists after delivery. Histopathology confirms when excised.
  • Exam trap: pregnant patient with bleeding gingival nodule → not automatic emergency excision every time; observe + OH is classic when mild.

G2. Pregnancy dentistry timing free points

  • Many electives deferred in first trimester when possible; second trimester often preferred for necessary care; third trimester positioning: left lateral tilt / avoid prolonged flat supine (IVC compression concept).
  • Emergency care for infection/pain is appropriate any trimester with physician coordination as needed — untreated odontogenic infection harms mother and fetus more than thoughtful dental care.
  • Radiographs: justify necessity, use shielding, minimize but do not refuse essential diagnostic imaging when benefit outweighs risk.
  • Drug categories: know principle of avoiding unnecessary drugs; coordinate for systemic meds. Local anesthesia with careful technique remains mainstay when treatment needed (exam-level: treat infection, do not invent freestyle high-dose sedatives).

G3. Drug-induced gingival enlargement triad

  • Memorize three: nifedipine (and other CCBs), phenytoin, cyclosporine.
  • Plaque worsens hyperplasia. Treatment: OH, professional debridement, physician may switch drug, surgery if fibrous residual.
  • Stems: “seizure med + enlarged gingiva” → phenytoin; “transplant + enlarged gingiva” → cyclosporine; “HTN CCB + enlarged gingiva” → nifedipine pathway.

G4. Other medical free points that sneak into path day

  • Uncontrolled diabetes → infection risk, poor healing, thrush risk, defer elective complex care until optimized when possible.
  • Asthma analgesia → prefer paracetamol; caution NSAIDs (AERD subset). Carry inhaler awareness.
  • Hyperthyroid / unstable cardiac → caution epinephrine in LA (link Day 7).
  • Recent MI / unstable angina / recent transplant → defer elective (ethics section expands transplant).

G5. Exam stem patterns

  • “Second trimester, bleeding pedunculated gingival mass, otherwise OK” → pregnancy pyogenic granuloma → OH ± observe often.
  • “Phenytoin + diffuse gingival overgrowth” → drug enlargement triad member.
  • “Late pregnancy, dizzy supine in chair” → left lateral tilt / position change.
  • “Asthma needs analgesic after extraction” → paracetamol preferred teaching.

STOP 35 sec: “Pregnancy epulis often observe + OH. Electives often 2nd trimester. Left lateral late pregnancy. Nifedipine/phenytoin/cyclosporine enlarge gingiva. Defer elective if unstable/transplant.”

G2. Path/med free-point expansion (Block B depth)

  • Paget of bone: cotton-wool skull, expanded bone, high alkaline phosphatase classic lab hook; cotton-wool on jaws too in stems.
  • Fibrous dysplasia: ground-glass bone, young patients, monostotic common in jaws — do not treat like infection alone.
  • Cemento-osseous dysplasia: often vital teeth, mixed radio-opaque/lucent periapical (esp. lower anteriors in classic demos) — do not excavate “for endo” on vital COD.
  • Malignancy red flags: solitary expanding radiolucency with ragged borders, paresthesia (numb lip), loose teeth without perio reason, non-healing extraction socket.
  • MRONJ = antiresorptive/antiangiogenic drug history + exposed bone ≥8 weeks concepts + no RT. ORN = radiation field history.
  • Stage thinking: exposed bone, infection, fistula, pathologic fracture — severity drives refer/surgery pathway.
  • Pregnancy epulis (pyogenic granuloma): often observe if stable; definitive after delivery if regresses; good OH; position left lateral tilt for supine hypotensive syndrome in late pregnancy.

H. Infection control & Spaulding

H1. Order is non-negotiable

  • Always: Cleaning → Disinfection → Sterilization.
  • Organic debris and bioburden block sterilant penetration. Putting visibly dirty instruments straight into an autoclave is wrong even if the cycle runs — banks hammer this.
  • Cleaning = physical/chemical removal of soil (ultrasonic, washer-disinfector, scrubbing per protocol with PPE). Then disinfect as indicated. Then sterilize critical/semi-critical items that require it.
  • Single-use items: do not reprocess unless the manufacturer and regulations allow a specific device — exam usually wants single-use discarded.

H2. Spaulding classification (must automatic)

  • Critical: enters sterile tissue, bloodstream, or bone / contacts sterile body areas — e.g., scalpels, elevators, surgical burs, periodontal scalers that penetrate, implant instruments. Require sterilization (heat sterilize preferred) or sterile single-use.
  • Semi-critical: contacts mucous membranes or non-intact skin without penetrating sterile tissue — e.g., mouth mirrors, amalgam condensers, impression trays, handpiece exteriors concepts, radiographic film holders. Sterilize preferred; high-level disinfection (HLD) if heat-sensitive and sterilization not possible per device IFU.
  • Non-critical: contacts intact skin only — e.g., BP cuff, pulse oximeter, some x-ray tube heads with barriers. Low- to intermediate-level disinfection; clean first if soiled.
  • Exam drills: “elevator after surgical extraction” → critical → sterilize. “Mirror used intraorally” → semi-critical. “Blood pressure cuff” → non-critical.

H3. Barriers, surfaces, hand hygiene

  • Clinical contact surfaces: light handles, curing light handles/tips with barriers, chair switches, air/water syringe handles — barrier and/or intermediate-level disinfect every patient.
  • Hand hygiene before donning gloves, after removing gloves, before/after patient contact — gloves never replace hand hygiene. Never wash and reuse disposable gloves.
  • Housekeeping surfaces (floors) vs clinical contact surfaces — different intensity; do not claim mopping sterilizes elevators.

H4. Sharps, PPE, waterlines, handpieces

  • Sharps: no two-handed recap (scoop technique one-handed if recap unavoidable); puncture-resistant containers; never overfill. Post-exposure: wash, report, occupational protocol, Hep B status matters.
  • PPE: gloves, mask, eye protection; gown when splash/spatter likely. PPE protects DHCP and patients as part of standard precautions — does not sterilize instruments.
  • DUWL: biofilm control, flush protocols, sterile water/irrigant for surgical sites. Do not use untreated waterline water for surgical osteotomies as best practice teaching.
  • Handpieces: clean, lubricate per IFU, heat sterilize between patients when indicated by guidelines — “wipe only” is insufficient for devices that require sterilization.

I. Sterilization monitoring

I1. Three monitoring pillars

  • Mechanical (physical) monitoring: record time, temperature, pressure for each load (printout/log). Detects equipment failure patterns but does not prove biological kill alone.
  • Chemical indicators: color-change strips/tape that respond to sterilant exposure conditions. Show the item/process was exposed to some sterilizing conditions — do not alone prove sterility. Internal chemical indicators in packs help process monitoring.
  • Biological indicators (BI / spore tests): gold standard. Use resistant spores (Geobacillus stearothermophilus for steam; Bacillus atrophaeus concepts for dry heat/EO teaching). Grow/detect failure. Run on a regular schedule (weekly often cited in dental teaching) and after sterilizer repair/relocation, and with implantables per stricter protocols when applicable.

I2. Failed BI and pack management

  • Failed spore test → take sterilizer out of service, do not use instruments from suspect loads, follow recall protocol for potentially unsterile packs, retest after repair/maintenance, document.
  • Do not ignore a failed BI because “chemical tape turned brown.”
  • Wet packs after cycle are not stored as sterile — reprocess; moisture breaks barrier assumptions.
  • Date packs, store closed/dry, first-in first-out, inspect packaging integrity before use. Torn pack = not sterile.

I3. Bowie-Dick and autoclave concepts

  • Pre-vacuum steam sterilizers: Bowie-Dick-type test assesses air removal / steam penetration in empty chamber test packs where used — process challenge, not a substitute for BI schedule.
  • Overloading, sealed pouches wrong, heavy instrument nesting → cold spots / wet packs / failures.
  • Exam: “which proves sterilization best?” → biological indicator / spore test — not chemical tape alone.

I4. Exam stem patterns (monitoring)

  • “Gold standard sterilizer monitor” → biological indicator (spore test).
  • “Tape changed color — is load proven sterile?” → no, chemical = process only.
  • “Spore test positive/failed” → out of service + recall protocol.
  • “Wet pack on shelf” → do not use as sterile; reprocess.
  • “How often BI commonly taught” → weekly (+ after repair) pattern in many dental IC stems.

J. Ethics free points

J1. Informed consent

  • Elements: diagnosis/problem in understandable language, proposed treatment, material risks and benefits, alternatives including no treatment, who will treat, opportunity for questions, voluntary decision, capacity to decide, documentation.
  • Consent is a process, not only a signature. Coercion, hiding risks, or treating without capacity/consent is unethical and often illegal.
  • Emergency life-saving exceptions exist in broader medicine; routine elective dentistry still needs consent. For minors: parent/guardian consent + child assent when appropriate per local law.

J2. Confidentiality and records

  • Do not discuss patients in elevators, social media, or waiting rooms. Share only with care-team need-to-know or with consent / legal duty (reportable diseases, court orders, child protection duties as applicable).
  • Secure charts, encrypted systems where required, no casual photo posting of identifiable faces/teeth without consent.
  • Honest records: do not fabricate notes; late entries should be honest about timing. Advertising must not be false or misleading.

J3. Core principles

  • Autonomy: patient decides after adequate information.
  • Beneficence: act in patient’s best interest.
  • Non-maleficence: do no harm — includes not doing procedures beyond competence without training/referral.
  • Justice: fairness in access and non-discrimination.
  • When principles conflict (e.g., patient refuses beneficial care), autonomy usually prevails in competent adults after information — document refusal.

J4. Transplant, unstable medical, referral duties

  • Recent organ transplant / unstable systemic disease: defer elective dental care. Coordinate with transplant team/physician. High infection risk, drug interactions (cyclosporine, steroids, immunosuppression), altered healing.
  • Urgent infection still needs management — with medical input, antibiotics as indicated, possible hospital setting — not “ignore abscess because transplant.”
  • Refer when beyond competence: suspected oral cancer, complex OMS, uncontrolled medical, advanced sedation needs. Holding a cancer patient for months of “try this gel” is harm.
  • Child / vulnerable adult protection: duty to act per local law when abuse/neglect suspected — know principle even if exact form number is not memorized.

J5. Exam stem patterns (ethics)

  • “Patient wants full mouth veneers, you list risks/benefits/alternatives including none” → informed consent process.
  • “Colleague posts patient smile selfie without consent” → confidentiality breach.
  • “Liver transplant 2 months ago wants elective whitening + multiple crowns now” → defer elective, coordinate medicine.
  • “Suspicious tongue ulcer, you are unsure” → refer / biopsy pathway, do not delay.
  • “Competent adult refuses X-ray after explanation” → respect autonomy, document, discuss limits of treatment without imaging.
