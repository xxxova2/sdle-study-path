B. 2017 classification — stage vs grade (must automatic)

  • Staging = severity + complexity of management (how bad the disease is / how hard it is to treat). Staging is not “how fast it is progressing.” Speed belongs to grade.
  • Stage drivers (severity): interdental CAL, radiographic bone loss (RBL), tooth loss due to periodontitis. Complexity modifiers push stage up: deep PD, furcation II/III, vertical/angular defects, ridge defects, bite collapse/drifting, <20 remaining teeth, masticatory dysfunction, secondary occlusal trauma patterns.
  • Stage I: interdental CAL 1–2 mm; RBL mostly coronal third <15%; no tooth loss due to periodontitis; max PD usually ≤4 mm; mostly horizontal bone loss. Early/ mild management complexity.
  • Stage II: interdental CAL 3–4 mm; RBL 15–33%; no tooth loss due to periodontitis; max PD usually ≤5 mm; mostly horizontal. Moderate severity still without perio tooth loss.
  • Stage III: interdental CAL ≥5 mm; RBL extending to mid‑root and beyond; ≤4 teeth lost to periodontitis; complexity includes PD ≥6 mm, vertical bone loss ≥3 mm, furcation II or III, moderate ridge defects. Severe with potential for tooth loss but not the full functional wreck of IV.
  • Stage IV: same severity territory as III plus functional complexity needing complex rehabilitation: secondary occlusal trauma, severe ridge defects, bite collapse, drifting, flaring, <20 teeth, masticatory dysfunction, need for complex multi‑disciplinary rehab thinking. Stage IV is “severe + broken bite/function story.”
  • Extent descriptors (added to stage): localized (<30% teeth), generalized (≥30%), or molar‑incisor pattern. Extent is not a substitute for stage or grade.
  • Grading = rate of progression + risk profile (how fast / how risky the host is). Modifiers that banks love: smoking dose, diabetes control (HbA1c cutoffs in teaching tables).
  • Grade A (slow): no loss over 5 years when longitudinal data exist; heavy biofilm with low destruction; non‑smoker; normoglycemic / no diabetes diagnosis impacting risk in the teaching table.
  • Grade B (moderate — often default start): <2 mm loss over 5 years; destruction commensurate with biofilm; smoking <10 cigarettes/day; diabetes with HbA1c <7% in classic bank cutoffs.
  • Grade C (rapid): ≥2 mm loss over 5 years; destruction exceeds biofilm deposits; smoking ≥10 cigarettes/day; diabetes HbA1c ≥7%; early onset / rapid patterns (old “aggressive” often lives here). Grade C is not a stage — a young patient can be Stage II Grade C or Stage III Grade C depending on severity.
  • How banks trap you: stem describes rapid progression + heavy smoking → students pick “Stage C” (invalid). Correct framing: grade C, stage by CAL/bone/tooth loss/complexity separately. Another trap: deep PD alone automatically “Stage IV” without functional collapse — complexity can raise stage, but IV needs the rehab/function package in teaching tables.
  • Memory sentence: Stage = Severity + Complexity. Grade = Rate + Risk. Never swap. Never invent “Stage C.”
  • Stem patterns — stage/grade:
    • “Which best describes severity and complexity of management?” → staging
    • “Which best describes rate of progression?” → grading
    • “Patient smokes 15 cig/day, rapid CAL increase” → grade C modifier territory
    • “Bite collapse, drifting, <20 teeth, severe CAL” → Stage IV pattern
    • “CAL 3 mm, <15–33% bone loss, no teeth lost to perio” → Stage I/II range — read exact numbers

C. CAL vs PD vs recession (measurement literacy)

  • Probing depth (PD) = gingival margin → base of the pocket/sulcus. PD changes with swelling, hyperplasia, and where the margin sits. Deep PD does not always equal severe cumulative attachment loss (pseudopockets in enlargement).
  • Clinical attachment level/loss (CAL) = CEJ (or fixed reference) → base of pocket. Best clinical measure of cumulative attachment loss used in staging. Staging tables are built on interdental CAL, not PD alone.
  • Recession = CEJ → gingival margin (when margin is apical to CEJ). When recession is present (margin apical to CEJ): CAL ≈ PD + recession (same units, careful probing technique).
  • Pseudopocket / gingival enlargement: margin coronal to CEJ → deep PD can exist with little true CAL. Exam stem: drug enlargement with deep pseudopockets — do not automatically stage as severe periodontitis without CAL/bone loss evidence.
  • BOP (bleeding on probing): marker of inflammation/activity. Important caveat: smokers may bleed less despite significant disease — pink ≠ healthy when pockets and bone loss exist.
  • Mobility and fremitus are not the same as PD. Mobility can reflect inflammation, trauma, or reduced support (secondary occlusal trauma when support already lost).
  • Radiographs: RBL pattern (horizontal vs vertical), furcation arrows, calculus ledges, overhangs. Radiograph alone does not replace CAL charting, but banks use RBL % ranges in stage tables.
  • Stem patterns — measurements:
    • “Best indicator of cumulative destruction” → CAL (not PD alone)
    • “Margin apical to CEJ, PD 4, recession 3” → think CAL ~7 mm territory
    • “Deep pockets, swollen gingiva, no CAL” → pseudopocket / gingivitis or enlargement story
    • “Smoker, little bleeding, deep PD, bone loss” → still diseased; smoking masks BOP
  • Practical exam tip: if options mix PD and CAL definitions, match the reference point: margin→base = PD; CEJ→base = CAL.

STOP 30 sec: PD = margin→base · CAL = CEJ→base · recession = CEJ→margin · CAL ≈ PD + recession when recessed · smokers bleed less.

D. Furcation (Glickman) + Miller recession (brief)

  • Glickman I — incipient: probe catches the concavity of the furcation entrance; horizontal component not yet a real tunnel into the furcation. Early involvement.
  • Glickman II — cul‑de‑sac: probe goes horizontally into the furcation but not through‑and‑through to the other side. Blind pouch. Common complexity raiser for staging and hygiene access problems.
  • Glickman III — through‑and‑through: probe communicates through the furcation; soft tissue still covers so you may not see daylight clinically. Soft tissue roof present.
  • Glickman IV — through‑and‑through + visible: same communication but recession makes the furcation clinically visible. Exam favorite discriminator: IV = you can see it.
  • Furcation II/III (and IV) increase management complexity (stage upward pressure), worsen SRP access, and change prognosis options (root resection, tunneling, extraction, regenerative select cases — specialty judgment; exam cares that access and prognosis worsen).
  • Which teeth: multirooted — maxillary molars (3 furcations possible conceptually: buccal, mesial, distal), mandibular molars (buccal and lingual). Premolars less classic for big furcation teaching but possible when multi‑rooted.
  • Miller recession classification (brief — coverage predictability):
    • I: recession not to MGJ; no interdental soft/hard tissue loss — full coverage predictable in teaching
    • II: to or beyond MGJ; still no interdental loss — coverage still often predictable
    • III: interdental loss or malposition limits full coverage
    • IV: severe interdental loss — root coverage not predictable
  • Memory: Miller predictability dies when interdental bone/soft tissue is lost (III–IV). Glickman visibility dies/opens at IV with recession showing the through‑and‑through.
  • Stem patterns — furcation/Miller:
    • “Probe enters furcation but not through” → II
    • “Through‑and‑through, soft tissue covers” → III
    • “Through‑and‑through and clinically visible” → IV
    • “Recession past MGJ with papilla loss, coverage?” → Miller III/IV limited predictability

STOP 30 sec: Furcation I catch · II cul‑de‑sac · III through · IV through + see it · Miller III/IV = interdental loss kills full coverage predictability.

E. Biofilm, calculus, host response

  • Dental plaque biofilm is the primary etiology of plaque‑induced gingivitis and the necessary microbial driver of periodontitis in susceptible hosts. Biofilm is a structured community — it needs mechanical disruption (brushing, interdental cleaning, professional debridement). Mouthwash alone is not a cure for periodontitis.
  • Calculus is mineralized plaque that acts as a retention niche for living biofilm — it is not a chemical acid burn that “eats bone by itself” in the oversimplified sense. Subgingival calculus blocks healing until SRP removes it; leaving calculus = leaving the apartment for bacteria.
  • Host response drives most connective tissue and bone destruction. Same plaque burden ≠ same CAL across patients. Genetics, smoking, diabetes, immune status, local plaque traps change outcomes. This is why grading exists (risk/rate) separate from stage (severity).
  • Gingivitis: inflammation of gingiva, reversible, no progressive CAL of periodontitis definition. Still important — untreated gingivitis can progress in susceptible patients; also masks/overlays other conditions.
  • Periodontitis: inflammation with CAL and usually pocketing and/or radiographic bone loss pattern — irreversible attachment loss history (though therapy can gain some attachment clinically, destroyed attachment is disease).
  • Local contributing factors (banks love): overhanging restorations, open contacts with food impaction, overcontoured crowns, ill‑fitting clasps, calculus ledges, anatomic grooves, crowded teeth hard to clean. Fixing local traps is part of cause‑related therapy, not optional cosmetics.
  • Supra‑ vs subgingival: subgingival biofilm/calculus access is why SRP exists; coronal polishing alone does not treat periodontitis.
  • Stem patterns — etiology:
    • “Primary etiology of plaque‑induced gingival disease” → biofilm/plaque
    • “Role of calculus” → plaque retention / harbors biofilm
    • “Why two patients with similar plaque differ in bone loss” → host susceptibility / risk factors
    • “Open contact + papilla inflammation” → food impaction local factor (ties to Day 4 open contact rule)

STOP 30 sec: Biofilm primary · calculus retains · host destroys tissue · gingivitis reversible no CAL · periodontitis = CAL · fix overhangs/open contacts.

F. Smoking and diabetes (bidirectional, grade modifiers)

  • Smoking — major modifiable risk: more CAL and bone loss, poorer response to SRP and surgery, higher implant complication/failure risk, delayed healing, more residual deep sites after therapy in many studies/teaching summaries.
  • Smoking masks clinical redness/BOP: vasoconstriction and immune effects → tissues may look less inflamed while pockets and bone loss progress. Trap stem: “gingiva looks pink, little bleeding, but deep PD and RBL” in a smoker → still periodontitis, not “healthy because no BOP.”
  • Dose for grading tables: ≥10 cigarettes/day often pushes Grade C thinking in 2017 teaching cutoffs; <10 may sit in Grade B modifier zone (along with other factors). Know the exam table spirit even if real life is continuous risk.
  • Diabetes ↔ periodontitis bidirectional: poor glycemic control worsens periodontal destruction and healing; severe periodontitis is associated with harder glycemic control. Uncontrolled DM → poorer healing, more abscess risk, caution with elective surgery/implants (ties Day 2/4 medical free points).
  • HbA1c cutoffs in grade modifiers (classic teaching): <7% more Grade B territory; ≥7% more Grade C territory when diabetes is the modifier story — always combined with other grade evidence when available.
  • Counseling is clinical, not optional ethics fluff: smoking cessation and medical coordination for DM improve periodontal prognosis. Exam may ask most important risk factor modification.
  • Stem patterns — smoking/DM:
    • “Least bleeding despite deep pockets, smokes” → smoking masks BOP
    • “Which raises grade toward C?” → heavy smoking or poorly controlled DM
    • “Bidirectional relationship” → diabetes and periodontitis
    • “Uncontrolled DM wants implants” → control disease / safer temporary removable (restorative crossover)

STOP 30 sec: Smoking masks BOP + kills healing · ≥10 cig → Grade C thinking · DM ↔ perio both ways · HbA1c ≥7% high‑risk modifier spirit.

G. SRP, re‑eval, surgery indications

  • Scaling and root planing (SRP) = foundation of cause‑related / non‑surgical therapy: remove plaque and calculus, disrupt biofilm, smooth roots as indicated by teaching, create environment for healing and hygiene. Goals: reduce PD, reduce BOP, possible clinical attachment gain, improve tissue tone, make maintenance possible.
  • Oral hygiene instruction is not a side quest: without daily plaque control, SRP and surgery fail. Banks test “what is essential before surgery” → plaque control / cause‑related therapy first.
  • Re‑evaluation timing: classically about 4–8 weeks after SRP (healing and tissue response window). Do not jump to elective pocket reduction surgery the same day as first SRP in standard teaching. Re‑eval assesses residual PD, BOP, calculus remnants, furcation access, OH adequacy.
  • After re‑eval pathways:
    • Sites improved, OH good, residual shallow → supportive periodontal therapy / maintenance
    • Residual deep PD, persistent BOP, poor access (furcation, deep infrabony), good OH → consider surgical access / pocket reduction / regenerative where indicated
    • OH still poor → more motivation, re‑instrumentation, delay elective surgery
  • Pocket reduction / resective surgery: reduce PD for maintainability when residual deep pockets remain after cause‑related therapy + adequate OH. Apically positioned flap / osseous resective concepts in appropriate anatomy — exam level: know indication spirit (residual deep PD after SRP), not full residency technique steps.
  • Regenerative approaches: selected infrabony defects (favorable walls, e.g. three‑wall defects classic teaching), GTR, bone grafts, biologics — not magic for every flat horizontal loss pattern or every hopeless Grade III furcation. Case selection is the exam point.
  • Extraction of hopeless teeth: sometimes correct perio therapy (Stage III/IV plans). Do not keep a hopeless mobile abscess factory forever because “perio should save all teeth.”
  • Maintenance (SPT): lifelong for periodontitis patients. Interval often 3–4 months initially teaching range; risk‑based. Missing maintenance → recurrence.
  • Stem patterns — therapy sequence:
    • “Next step after initial SRP” → re‑eval in weeks (not immediate flap)
    • “Residual 6–7 mm PD after SRP, excellent OH” → consider surgical therapy options
    • “Residual deep PD, plaque score still terrible” → OH reinforcement first
    • “Best initial therapy for most periodontitis” → cause‑related SRP + OH, not antibiotics alone

STOP 30 sec: SRP first · re‑eval ~4–8 weeks · residual deep PD + good OH → surgery options · bad OH → no elective flap · maintenance lifelong.

H. Old aggressive vs chronic → 2017 map

  • Older classification used chronic periodontitis (common adult, usually biofilm‑related pace) and aggressive periodontitis (earlier onset, rapid, often molar‑incisor pattern, familial aggregation teaching, destruction may seem out of proportion to deposits).
  • In 2017 framework: there is no separate “aggressive stage.” You describe periodontitis by stage + grade + extent (localized/generalized/molar‑incisor pattern).
  • Old chronic ≈ often Stage I–III depending on severity, Grade A or B depending on rate/risk — but can be Grade C if rapid or heavy risk factors.
  • Old aggressive (early rapid, molar‑incisor, family history stories) ≈ often Grade C; stage still set by CAL/bone/tooth loss/complexity; extent may be molar‑incisor pattern.
  • Exam move when you see “aggressive” in a stem: translate to rapid progression / Grade C thinking + stage by severity numbers given. Do not freeze because the word is old.
  • Necrotizing diseases (NUG/NUP) remain special clinical entities (pain, necrosis, pseudomembrane, fetor, often stress/smoking/immunosuppression) — not the same as garden chronic pocketing. Manage acutely (debridement, OH, risk factors); HIV/systemic screen when indicated.
  • Periodontal abscess: localized pus in perio pocket, pain, swelling — drain/debride, manage occlusion if foreign body, consider systemic status; not the same as endo abscess without testing vitality when differential matters (endo‑perio later days too).
  • Stem patterns — old map:
    • “Young adult, rapid molar‑incisor bone loss, sparse deposits” → Grade C pattern / old aggressive map
    • “Adult, slow, deposits match destruction” → Grade A/B chronic‑like map
    • “How is aggressive classified in 2017?” → stage + grade + extent, not a separate stage name

STOP 30 sec: Old aggressive ≈ Grade C rapid · no separate aggressive stage · stage still by severity · chronic‑like often A/B · re‑eval before surgery still applies.

I. Drug‑induced gingival enlargement

  • Classic triad every SDLE candidate must fire cold: phenytoin (anticonvulsant), nifedipine (and other calcium channel blockers — amlodipine etc. may appear), cyclosporine (immunosuppressant, transplant patients).
  • Clinical picture: firm, enlarged gingiva, often starts in interdental papillae (anterior first common), can cover crowns, create pseudopockets, plaque traps, esthetic and speech issues. Not pure soft “edematous gingivitis only” when fibrotic drug effect dominates — though plaque amplifies severity hugely.
  • Management ladder:
    • Excellent OH + professional debridement (reduce inflammatory component)
    • Physician consult for drug substitution when medically feasible (e.g., different antihypertensives / anticonvulsants / immunosuppressants — never stop transplant drugs yourself)
    • Surgical gingivectomy / flap as needed for residual fibrotic enlargement after control phase
  • Differential: mouth breathing enlargement, pregnancy gingivitis/epulis, leukemia infiltration (systemic red flags), hereditary gingival fibromatosis — exam still returns to the triad most often when a drug history is planted.
  • Stem patterns:
    • “Hypertensive on nifedipine, swollen gums” → drug enlargement
    • “Seizure med phenytoin + overgrown papillae” → drug enlargement
    • “Transplant cyclosporine + gingival bulk” → drug enlargement
    • “First management” → OH + debridement ± MD for drug change; not only immediate gingivectomy ignoring plaque
  • Cross‑link Days 2/4 medical free points: same triad appears outside pure perio sections. Free points if automatic.

STOP 30 sec: Phenytoin · nifedipine (CCBs) · cyclosporine · OH + debridement + MD drug review · surgery if fibrotic residual.

J. Biologic width (supracrestal tissue attachment) and margins

  • Biologic width / supracrestal tissue attachment ≈ junctional epithelium + connective tissue attachment coronal to alveolar crest. Classic teaching combined dimension ≈ 2 mm (variable anatomically; sulcus is separate from the attachment figure in many tables).
  • Violation: restoration margin, overhang, or crown contour invading this zone too close to bone → chronic inflammation, BOP, discomfort, unpredictable attachment, localized bone loss around that restoration. Patient may present as “perio around one crowned tooth.”
  • Fix paths: redesign margins/contours (replace restoration), crown lengthening surgery to re‑establish space, or orthodontic extrusion in selected cases — not “more chlorhexidine forever” as sole fix.
  • Prevention: respect sulcus depth, avoid unnecessary deep subgingival margins, control finish lines, remove excess cement (implant cement especially), cleansable contours.
  • Stem patterns:
    • “Deep crown margin, chronic red swollen gingiva localized” → biologic width violation / excess cement / overhang DDx
    • “Best correction of margin too close to bone” → crown lengthening or margin relocation concepts
    • “Approximate classic biologic width value” → ~2 mm attachment teaching
  • Ties hard to Day 2 fixed prostho — Day 5 is where the perio mechanism locks in.

STOP 30 sec: Bio width ≈ JE + CT ~2 mm · deep margin → chronic inflammation · fix by redesign/lengthening not mouthwash alone.

K. Peri‑implant health / mucositis / peri‑implantitis

  • Peri‑implant health: no signs of inflammation that concern (no excessive BOP/erythema), stable probing relative to baseline, no progressive bone loss beyond initial remodeling expectations.
  • Peri‑implant mucositis: soft‑tissue inflammation around implant (BOP, redness, swelling) without progressive crestal bone loss. Analogy: gingivitis around an implant. Often reversible with improved plaque control and professional debridement if caught early. Remove excess cement if present.
  • Peri‑implantitis: inflammation plus progressive loss of supporting bone beyond remodeling. Analogy: periodontitis around an implant. Harder to manage; may need surgical access, decontamination, resective or regenerative strategies case‑by‑case — exam discriminator is + progressive bone loss.
  • Risk factors: poor oral hygiene, residual cement, history of periodontitis, smoking, uncontrolled diabetes, lack of maintenance, poorly cleansable prosthetic design.
  • Probing implants: light probing with plastic/appropriate probes in clinical life; exam cares more about mucositis vs implantitis definitions than probe brand.
  • Maintenance non‑negotiable: implants are not “set and forget.” SPT and hygiene access design matter as much as surgical placement.
  • Distance numbers still Day‑2 free points: IAN ≈2 mm, tooth ≈1.5 mm, implant–implant ≈3 mm — peri‑implant disease questions may sit beside spacing stems.
  • Stem patterns:
    • “Red implant gingiva, BOP, no progressive bone loss” → mucositis
    • “Inflammation + progressive bone loss on serial radiographs” → peri‑implantitis
    • “Most important long‑term factor” → plaque control / maintenance / cleansable design
    • “Cemented crown, inflamed implant site” → check residual cement

STOP 30 sec: Mucositis = soft only · peri‑implantitis = + progressive bone loss · cement/smoking/DM/perio history risk · maintenance forever.

L. Antibiotics and occlusal trauma

  • Systemic antibiotics = adjuncts, not first‑line monotherapy that replaces mechanical debridement. Foundation remains biofilm removal (SRP / surgical access). Consider adjunctive systemic abx in select severe progressive / Grade C / aggressive‑pattern cases per teaching — not every routine Stage II with good SRP access.
  • Local antimicrobials (chips, gels): residual sites after debridement in some protocols — still not a calculus substitute. If calculus remains, chemistry will not magic it away.
  • Abscess / acute infection: drainage and debridement primary; systemic abx when systemic signs or spreading infection — same spirit as OMS common sense.
  • Primary occlusal trauma: excessive occlusal force on a tooth with normal periodontal support → may see mobility, widened PDL, fremitus, without prior periodontitis CAL. Manage forces; support is intact.
  • Secondary occlusal trauma: normal or excessive force on a tooth with reduced periodontal support → mobility, fremitus, drifting — common in Stage IV complexity stories. Reduce inflammation first; occlusal adjustment is adjunct, not a cure for untreated periodontitis.
  • Critical teaching: occlusal adjustment alone does not cure plaque‑induced periodontitis. Treat biofilm disease; manage trauma as adjunct when present.
  • Fremitus: palpable vibration on teeth in function — clue to occlusal trauma component.
  • Stem patterns:
    • “Antibiotic as only treatment for chronic periodontitis” → wrong; mechanical therapy foundation
    • “When systemic abx considered” → select severe/rapid cases as adjunct after/with mechanical therapy teaching
    • “Mobility with reduced bone support” → secondary occlusal trauma
    • “High forces, normal bone height, widened PDL” → primary occlusal trauma pattern

STOP 30 sec: Abx adjunct not solo · primary trauma = excess force + normal support · secondary = force on reduced support · adjust occlusion adjunct after biofilm control.

M. Exam traps — make these automatic

  1. Stage ≠ grade — severity/complexity vs rate/risk. There is no “Stage C.”
  2. PD ≠ CAL — CEJ reference for CAL; margin reference for PD.
  3. Furcation IV = through‑and‑through and visible; III through but soft tissue covers.
  4. Smoking: less bleeding ≠ healthy when pockets/bone loss present.
  5. Diabetes bidirectional — control affects grade modifiers and healing.
  6. Enlargement drugs: phenytoin, nifedipine (CCBs), cyclosporine — every time.
  7. SRP → re‑eval weeks later before elective pocket surgery.
  8. Biologic width violation → chronic red margins around deep restorations; fix structurally.
  9. Mucositis soft only; peri‑implantitis + progressive bone loss.
  10. Antibiotics adjunct, not solo first line for routine perio.
  11. Primary trauma = excess force + normal support; secondary = reduced support.
  12. Old aggressive ≈ Grade C rapid pattern + stage by severity; molar‑incisor extent possible.
  13. Pseudopockets in drug enlargement ≠ automatic severe stage without CAL/RBL.
  14. Open contact / overhang = local perio risk factor (Day 4 crossover free points).
  15. Surgery without plaque control → predictable failure; OH is not optional.
  16. Maintenance after active therapy is part of treatment, not an afterthought.

Appendix — dense stage/grade decision examples (exam logic)

  • Example 1: Interdental CAL max 2 mm, RBL <15% coronal, PD max 4 mm, no teeth lost to perio, non‑smoker, no DM, slow history. → often Stage I, Grade A/B depending on progression evidence. Management mostly non‑surgical + maintenance.
  • Example 2: CAL 4 mm, RBL ~25%, PD 5 mm, no perio tooth loss, smokes 5 cig/day, HbA1c 6.5%. → severity often Stage II; grade often B (moderate risk cutoffs) unless rapid loss documented.
  • Example 3: CAL ≥5 mm, PD 7 mm, furcation II, vertical defects, two teeth already lost to perio, OH improvable. → Stage III territory; grade from rate/smoking/DM separately.
  • Example 4: Same severity as III but bite collapse, drifting anteriors, <20 teeth, secondary occlusal trauma, needs complex rehab thinking. → Stage IV.
  • Example 5: Young adult, molar‑incisor pattern rapid bone loss, sparse plaque relative to destruction, family history of early tooth loss, smokes 12/day. → extent molar‑incisor; grade often C; stage from measured CAL/RBL/tooth loss — old “aggressive” map.
  • Example 6: Phenytoin enlargement, PD 6 mm papillae, but CAL minimal and bone crest intact. → treat as enlargement/pseudopocket problem first; do not auto‑label Stage III periodontitis without true attachment/bone loss evidence.
  • Example 7: After SRP + excellent OH, residual PD 6–7 mm with BOP at molars, furcation II access limited. → re‑eval already done; consider surgical access / further therapy options — not endless SRP only if access truly fails.
  • Example 8: After SRP, residual PD 6 mm but plaque index still terrible and calculus left. → OH + re‑instrumentation first; surgery will fail on dirty field.

Appendix — prognosis & local factors (high‑yield extras)

  • Favorable prognosis clues: adequate bone support remaining, good OH potential, non‑smoking or cessation, controlled systemic disease, cleansable anatomy, patient commitment to maintenance.
  • Guarded / poor clues: advanced CAL, furcation III/IV, severe mobility with reduced support, non‑compliance, heavy ongoing smoking, uncontrolled DM, strategic tooth with no restorable plan.
  • Local plaque traps to name on exam: overhanging amalgam/composite, open contacts, poorly contoured crowns, subgingival calculus ledges, ill‑fitting RPD clasps, crowded arches, enamel pearls / grooves in furcation regions.
  • Tooth mobility Miller (brief if asked): I slight, II moderate ≤1 mm horizontal, III severe >1 mm or vertical depressibility — interpret with inflammation and support, not alone.
  • Fremitus: tooth vibrates in function — trauma component flag alongside perio charting.
  • Endo‑perio note (border for Day 6): true combined lesions need both pulp and perio logic; narrow deep isolated defect may be VRF or endo sinus — vitality testing matters before heroic perio only.

Appendix — full 2017 quick table (memorize skeleton)

  • Stage I: CAL 1–2 mm · RBL <15% · no tooth loss from perio · PD ≤4 typical · horizontal mostly.
  • Stage II: CAL 3–4 mm · RBL 15–33% · no tooth loss from perio · PD ≤5 typical · horizontal mostly.
  • Stage III: CAL ≥5 mm · RBL to mid‑root+ · ≤4 teeth lost to perio · complexity: PD≥6, vertical≥3, furcation II/III, moderate ridge defect.
  • Stage IV: III‑level severity plus functional wreck: secondary trauma, bite collapse, drifting, <20 teeth, severe ridge defect, complex rehab need.
  • Grade A: slow · no loss/5y when known · heavy biofilm low destruction · non‑smoker · no/low DM impact.
  • Grade B: moderate default · <2 mm/5y · destruction fits biofilm · <10 cig/day · HbA1c <7% teaching cutoff.
  • Grade C: rapid · ≥2 mm/5y · destruction exceeds biofilm · ≥10 cig/day · HbA1c ≥7% · early/rapid patterns.
  • Extent: localized <30% teeth · generalized ≥30% · molar‑incisor pattern available.
  • Say it: Stage numbers from severity/complexity. Grade letters from rate/risk. Extent from distribution. Three labels can stack: e.g., generalized Stage III Grade C periodontitis.

Appendix — SRP visit structure (what banks imply)

  • Before instruments: diagnosis, charting (PD, recession, CAL, BOP, furcation, mobility), radiographs as needed, risk factor interview (smoke, DM), OH baseline, informed plan.
  • During SRP: supra then sub debridement, quadrant or full‑mouth protocols per teaching/case, local anesthesia as needed, patient comfort, no heroic flap “while we are here” without plan.
  • Immediate after: OH reinforcement, sensitivity management, risk factor advice, schedule re‑eval window.
  • Re‑eval visit: re‑chart, compare, residual calculus check, decide maintenance vs further non‑surgical vs surgery pathway.
  • Maintenance visits: risk‑based interval, debridement of residual pockets, OH coaching, monitor implants if present, update medical history (new CCB? new smoking?).
  • When surgery enters the story: only after cause‑related therapy + re‑eval logic, with defined residual problem (deep PD, access, regenerative candidate defect) — not as first click for every deep number on day one charting.

Appendix — peri‑implant vs periodontal language (do not mix)

  • Natural tooth: gingivitis / periodontitis with CAL on CEJ‑referenced attachment.
  • Implant: mucositis / peri‑implantitis with progressive bone loss criterion for implantitis.
  • You can have a patient with periodontitis history who later develops peri‑implantitis — history of perio is a risk factor, not the same diagnosis word on the implant site.
  • Cement residue is a classic implant‑specific local factor; natural teeth more often overhangs/open contacts — related ideas, different prostheses.
  • Exam stem with “implant” in it almost always wants mucositis vs implantitis discrimination or spacing numbers — lock both.
