A. Extraction indications & contraindications

A1. Common indications

  • Non‑restorable caries or fracture below salvage (no ferrule possible, vertical root fracture, roots split).
  • Hopeless periodontitis with mobility, bone loss, and non‑strategic value after perio therapy discussion.
  • Orthodontic needs (premolars, supernumeraries, selected third molars) when part of a planned scheme — not random “all wisdom teeth always.”
  • Trauma beyond salvage, pathology linked to the tooth (cysts, tumors — often with OMS plan), severe pain from an unrestorable tooth, selected teeth in a fracture line when indicated by trauma protocol.
  • Failed endodontics with non‑restorable or non‑strategic tooth; patient preference after true informed options (RCT vs extract vs nothing when appropriate).

A2. Systemic defer / caution (exam gold)

  • Unstable systemic disease: unstable angina, hypertensive crisis, uncontrolled diabetic crisis, acute severe asthma attack — stabilize first; elective extraction is not a hero move.
  • Recent MI / unstable cardiac / recent transplant: defer elective care; coordinate medicine. “Unstable / recent major medical event → defer elective” is a free‑point pattern across SDLE medical stems.
  • Airway‑threatening infection: secure airway and start medical/surgical infection pathway before leisurely chairside extraction of a hopeless tooth in a crashing Ludwig patient.
  • Radiation to jaws / antiresorptive history: plan for ORN and MRONJ risk (Section G) — not casual multi‑extraction day without strategy.
  • Bleeding diathesis, severe thrombocytopenia, INR massively supra‑therapeutic — fix the systemic risk with medicine, then operate with a plan (Section D).

A3. Local cautions

  • Severe pericoronitis with trismus: control infection/inflammation first when access and anesthesia are unsafe; extract when conditions allow or refer.
  • Vascular lesions in the field, limited opening with aspiration risk without proper setup, proximity to IAN/lingual/sinus without imaging plan.
  • Acute cellulitis with poor localization — sometimes drainage/antibiotics first, then definitive extraction when indicated (case‑dependent; airway always first).

A4. Consent and pre‑op checklist

  • Consent must cover: pain, swelling, infection, dry socket, bleeding, damage to adjacent teeth/restorations, nerve injury (IAN/lingual especially for lower 3rds), sinus communication (upper molars), jaw fracture rare but real on atrophic mandible, need for further care/referral.
  • Pre‑op: full med list (anticoagulants, antiplatelets, antiresorptives, steroids, immunosuppressants), allergies (LA, latex, antibiotics), pregnancy status when relevant, radiographs adequate for root form / IAN / sinus proximity / pathology.
  • Anxiety and NPO rules matter for sedation cases; for LA only still control anxiety to reduce syncope (Section J).

A5. Exam stem patterns (extraction ind/contraind)

  • “Unstable angina, elective asymptomatic third molar” → defer + medical clearance.
  • “History of IV bisphosphonate for cancer, asymptomatic fractured root tip” → MRONJ risk planning, not casual surgical heroics.
  • “Consent omission for lower 3rd molar” → nerve injury risk must be discussed.
  • “Upper 1st molar roots in sinus on PA” → warn oroantral communication; surgical plan.

STOP 35 sec: “Non‑restorable / hopeless / ortho / trauma · unstable systemic → defer elective · consent nerves + dry socket + sinus · meds + radiograph first.”

B. Impacted third molars — classification

B1. Why classification exists on exams

  • Banks use Winter and Pell & Gregory to ask “which is hardest?” and to link difficulty with nerve injury risk and surgical time.
  • You do not need to draw artistic OPGs — you need the axes: angulation (Winter) and space + depth (Pell & Gregory).

B2. Winter classification (angulation of 3rd relative to 2nd)

  • Mesioangular: crown tipped toward 2nd molar — most common lower impaction pattern; often more accessible than horizontal/distoangular but still can hug IAN.
  • Vertical: long axis parallel to 2nd.
  • Horizontal: long axis perpendicular — more bone removal/sectioning often needed.
  • Distoangular: crown tipped toward ramus — often harder access, path of withdrawal blocked by ramus.
  • Also: buccoversion, linguoversion, inverted — know they exist; angulation still drives difficulty.

B3. Pell & Gregory classification

  • Class 1 / 2 / 3 — space available distal to 2nd molar relative to anterior border of ramus:
    • Class 1: enough space for the 3rd crown (easier access conceptually).
    • Class 2: reduced space (partial coverage by ramus).
    • Class 3: little/no space — tooth mostly within ramus (hardest access on this axis).
  • Position A / B / C — depth relative to the occlusal plane of the 2nd molar:
    • A: 3rd occlusal surface at or above 2nd occlusal plane (most superficial).
    • B: between occlusal plane and cervical line of 2nd.
    • C: below cervical line of 2nd (deepest).
  • Exam synthesis: Class 3 + Position C + horizontal/distoangular = high difficulty + higher complication/nerve risk language.

B4. IAN risk clues and coronectomy concept

  • OPG/CBCT clues of intimate IAN relation: darkening of root, interruption of canal white line, diversion of canal, narrowing of root/canal — when present, refer / CBCT / discuss high nerve risk.
  • Coronectomy: intentional retention of roots when full removal risks IAN injury and roots are otherwise suitable — specialist decision, not a random GP experiment without training.
  • Lingual nerve risk rises with distal/lingual flap trauma and deep lingual bone work — careful technique and warning in consent.

B5. Exam stem patterns (3rd molars)

  • “Winter classification describes” → angulation of 3rd vs long axis of 2nd.
  • “Pell & Gregory Class 3” → insufficient space in ramus region.
  • “Position C” → deepest (below cervical of 2nd).
  • “Hardest combination often tested” → Class 3 + C (+ unfavorable angulation).
  • “Dark band over IAN canal on OPG” → intimate relation → risk discussion/imaging.

STOP 40 sec: “Winter = angulation · Pell & Gregory = ramus space (1–3) + depth (A–C) · Class 3 + C = hard · IAN signs on OPG → caution/refer · coronectomy = leave roots to save nerve when indicated.”

C. Dry socket (alveolar osteitis)

C1. Timing and clinical picture

  • Timing: severe pain typically 2–4 days after extraction — not the evening of surgery (day 0–1 pain is expected postop pain).
  • Picture: empty socket (lost/disintegrated clot), exposed bone, foul odor/taste, pain often radiating to ear; usually little facial swelling compared with spreading infection.
  • Patient often says “it was getting better then day 3 it exploded.”

C2. Risk factors

  • Mandibular molars, especially lower third molars; traumatic/difficult extraction; poor oral hygiene; premature vigorous rinsing/spitting/straws in some teaching; prior dry socket history.
  • Smoking is a classic high‑yield risk (suction + chemistry + poor healing).
  • Oral contraceptive use appears in many textbooks as a risk modifier for alveolar osteitis.

C3. Management (and what not to do first)

  • Gentle irrigation with saline; remove debris gently; place a sedative/medicated dressing (eugenol‑containing dressings historically common — follow product teaching); analgesia; review and repack as needed.
  • Avoid aggressive curettage of the entire bony wall as first‑line “to start bleeding” in classic dry socket — the issue is the lost clot/alveolar osteitis pattern, not a deep abscess requiring full debridement of every socket always.
  • Stop smoking advice; soft diet; follow‑up until granulation covers bone.
  • Antibiotics are not automatic for pure dry socket without spreading infection signs.

C4. Differential — do not miss real infection

  • Normal day‑1 pain improving ≠ dry socket.
  • Spreading cellulitis/abscess: fever, progressive swelling, trismus worsening, floor elevation, dysphagia → emergency infection pathway, not only a socket dressing.
  • Retained root, sequestrum, or adjacent tooth issues can mimic — examine and radiograph when unclear.

C5. Exam stem patterns (dry socket)

  • “Severe pain day 3, empty socket, smoker” → dry socket / alveolar osteitis.
  • “Pain evening of extraction day” → expected postop pain, not classic dry socket timing.
  • “Management first line” → irrigate + medicated dressing + analgesia, not “start IV antibiotics for every case.”

STOP 35 sec: “Dry socket = day 2–4 · empty socket · smoking · irrigate + dressing · not day‑0 pain · not Ludwig.”

D. Bleeding control & anticoagulants

D1. Local hemostasis first

  • Most dental bleeding is controlled with local measures: firm gauze pressure 20–30 minutes with correct bite, inspect for soft‑tissue bleeders, sutures, LA with vasoconstrictor when appropriate, oxidized cellulose / gelatin sponge, bone wax for osseous bleeders, tranexamic acid mouthwash concepts in some protocols.
  • Remove granulation carefully; pack socket; avoid endless unnecessary trauma that restarts bleeding.
  • Patient instructions: no spit/suck/straw/smoking, soft diet, pressure if oozes, when to return/ER.

D2. Primary, reactionary, secondary bleeding

  • Primary: during the procedure.
  • Reactionary: hours later — classic story when epinephrine wears off and/or blood pressure rises (anxiety, exercise, missed meds).
  • Secondary: days later — often infection‑related oozing.
  • Timing helps the stem: “started bleeding again 4 hours later at home” → reactionary pattern → local control + check systemic factors.

D3. Warfarin and INR — exam logic

  • For many simple extractions in a patient with stable therapeutic INR, exam teaching often favors: continue warfarin + meticulous local hemostasis rather than unilaterally stopping warfarin (stroke/thrombosis risk).
  • Know the recent INR; coordinate with physician when values are high, procedure is extensive, or medical history is complex. Do not freestyle stop warfarin or invent vitamin K/bridging without medical guidance.
  • “INR concepts”: higher INR → more bleeding risk; very high INR → defer elective surgery and refer to medicine to correct. Exact numeric cutoffs vary by guideline era and procedure invasiveness — banks still love continue + local measures for stable simple cases more than “always stop 5 days.”

D4. DOACs, antiplatelets, dual therapy

  • DOACs (e.g. apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) and dual antiplatelet therapy: case‑by‑case with physician/cardiology — exam still rewards local hemostasis and “don’t stop without advice” for stable simple oral surgery when that is the safe teaching path.
  • Aspirin monotherapy often continued for simple procedures with local control in many modern dental protocols — know the principle of balancing thrombosis vs bleed.

D5. When to refer / ER

  • Bleeding not controlled by proper local measures, airway compromise from hematoma, hypotensive shock, known severe coagulopathy without plan, post‑extraction bleeding with expanding floor‑of‑mouth hematoma → ER/OMS.

D6. Exam stem patterns (bleeding/anticoagulation)

  • “Warfarin, stable INR, simple extraction” → continue warfarin + local hemostasis (classic bank preference).
  • “Stopped warfarin himself 1 week ago for cleaning” → wrong; thrombosis risk + poor self‑management story.
  • “Bleeding 5 hours later, pressure not tried” → reactionary; teach pressure first.
  • “Expanding floor swelling after lower XO” → airway threat emergency.

STOP 45 sec: “Local first · primary / reactionary / secondary · warfarin stable simple XO = continue + local hemostasis · don’t stop anticoagulants alone · high INR/complex → coordinate medicine.”

E. Odontogenic space infections

E1. Microbiology and management pillars

  • Odontogenic infections are usually mixed aerobic + anaerobic flora from plaque/caries/periapical sources.
  • Pillars: airway first · source control (extract/RCT/I&D) · antibiotics when indicated (systemic signs, spreading cellulitis, immunocompromise, fascial spaces) · never antibiotics alone as permanent cure of an undrained abscess.

E2. Canine space

  • Source often upper canine (sometimes first premolar). Swelling lateral to the nose, obliterated nasolabial fold, may involve lower eyelid.
  • Severe cases can track toward orbital/cavernous pathways — orbital signs, severe headache, cranial nerve findings, high fever → emergency (not “more amoxicillin at home”).

E3. Buccal space

  • Cheek swelling outside the buccinator from upper or lower molars/premolars. Dramatic facial contour change but usually less immediate airway risk than deep neck/floor infections — still treat source and assess trismus/systemic status.

E4. Submandibular, sublingual, submental

  • Submandibular: lower molars classic; swelling below mandible border, trismus, pain; can connect to neighboring spaces.
  • Sublingual: floor of mouth elevation, tongue displacement — airway concern rises quickly.
  • Submental: midline under chin; may join bilateral processes.

E5. Ludwig angina (must be automatic)

  • Ludwig angina: rapidly progressive bilateral infection of submandibular + sublingual + submental spaces (cellulitis of the floor of mouth region).
  • Signs: floor of mouth elevated, tongue elevated/posterior, drooling, dysphagia, dysphonia, stridor, toxic appearance — airway emergency.
  • Management: hospital/OMS, secure airway early, IV antibiotics, surgical drainage as needed, source control when possible. Do not send home with oral antibiotics alone.

E6. Red flags for referral / ER

  • Progressive trismus, dysphagia, drooling, voice change, floor elevation, periorbital spread, high fever/toxicity, inability to swallow saliva, SpO₂ drop, neck rigidity/severe headache with cranial signs.

E7. Exam stem patterns (spaces)

  • “Bilateral floor elevation, tongue up, drooling” → Ludwig → airway.
  • “Nasolabial fold lost, upper canine source” → canine space.
  • “Cheek balloon, molar source” → buccal space.
  • “Lower molar, swelling under mandible” → submandibular.

STOP 40 sec: “Mixed flora · airway + source control · canine / buccal / submandibular · Ludwig = bilateral floor elevation = airway emergency.”

F. Facial fractures — exam hooks

F1. ABC trauma order

  • Facial injury stems still start with ABC: airway (blood, swelling, bilateral mandible), breathing, circulation/bleeding control, C‑spine precautions when mechanism warrants — then dental occlusion and OMS workup.
  • Do not obsess about a chipped tooth while the patient cannot protect their airway.

F2. Zygomaticomaxillary complex (ZMC)

  • ZMC fracture classic bank symptom: diplopia (orbital floor/wall involvement, muscle/entrapment, orbital volume change).
  • Other signs: periorbital ecchymosis, flattened malar eminence, step deformity at infraorbital rim/zygomatic arch, infraorbital nerve paresthesia, limited mouth opening if depressed arch hits coronoid, epistaxis sometimes.
  • Stem hinge is often diplopia + cheek trauma history → think ZMC/orbital complex, not “only soft tissue bruise.”

F3. Mandible fractures

  • Common sites: condyle, angle, body, symphysis/parasymphysis, ramus, coronoid. Multiple sites possible (e.g. angle + contralateral condyle patterns).
  • Clinical: malocclusion, step in occlusion, segment mobility, pain, IAN paresthesia (body/angle), floor hematoma, limited opening.
  • Airway risk if bilateral body/condyle patterns allow tongue fall back — evaluate carefully.

F4. Le Fort (high‑level recognition)

  • Le Fort I: floating maxilla (horizontal above teeth roots) — maxilla moves separate from upper face.
  • Le Fort II: pyramidal — maxilla + nasal complex involvement.
  • Le Fort III: craniofacial disjunction — face separates at cranial base level conceptually.
  • Exam usually wants recognition language, not full ORIF technique.

F5. Exam stem patterns (fractures)

  • “Blow to cheek, double vision” → ZMC / orbital involvement → diplopia hook.
  • “Floating maxilla” → Le Fort I.
  • “Malocclusion after punch, step in lower border” → mandible fracture pathway.
  • “First priority in facial trauma with bleeding in mouth” → airway.

STOP 35 sec: “ABC first · ZMC → diplopia · mandible = malocclusion/step · Le Fort I = floating maxilla · II pyramidal · III craniofacial disjunction.”

G. MRONJ

G1. Drugs and risk

  • MRONJ (medication‑related osteonecrosis of the jaw): associated with antiresorptives — bisphosphonates, denosumab, and sometimes antiangiogenic cancer drugs.
  • Higher risk: IV oncology dosing, longer duration, concomitant steroids, poor oral hygiene, invasive dental procedures (extractions), pre‑existing inflammatory disease.
  • Osteoporosis oral bisphosphonate risk is lower than oncology IV dosing but not zero — still plan carefully for extractions.

G2. Definition high‑level (exam contrast with ORN)

  • Classic elements: exposed bone (or fistula probing to bone) in the jaws persisting >8 weeks, current/previous antiresorptive or antiangiogenic therapy, no history of jaw radiation.
  • If radiation history explains the necrosis → think ORN (osteoradionecrosis), not MRONJ. Different label, overlapping careful surgery principles.

G3. Prevention and management concepts

  • Prevention: optimize OH and complete necessary extractions before high‑risk drug therapy when possible; prefer non‑surgical options; atraumatic technique; antimicrobial rinses; avoid elective surgery on high‑risk patients without specialist input.
  • If a patient is already on IV oncology antiresorptives, treat dental infections aggressively early, minimize surgical trauma, and involve the oncology team before planned extractions.
  • Management is stage‑based: CHX rinses, antibiotics if infection, smooth sharp bone edges that traumatize soft tissue, conservative debridement vs resection in advanced disease — always coordinate oncology/physician.
  • Drug holiday: only with treating physician/oncologist agreement — never freestyle‑stop cancer antiresorptives yourself on a hunch. Osteoporosis dosing discussions also belong with the prescribing physician, not solo dental cancellation of systemic therapy.
  • Document drug name, dose route (oral vs IV), duration, and dental findings every visit — stems often hide the risk in the medication list rather than the chief complaint.

G3b. Clinical communication traps

  • Patient says “I take a bone strengthener” → identify exact agent (alendronate vs denosumab vs IV zoledronate) before surgical consent language.
  • Asymptomatic radiographic sequestra or non‑healing socket after extraction in antiresorptive patients → evaluate for MRONJ staging, not only “dry socket dressing forever.”
  • ORN vs MRONJ: radiation history is the hinge; management philosophies overlap (conservative first, surgery selected) but labels differ on exams.

G4. Exam stem patterns (MRONJ)

  • “IV zoledronate for metastasis, exposed bone 3 months after extraction” → MRONJ pattern.
  • “Radiation history, exposed bone after XO” → ORN pathway.
  • “Prevention before denosumab” → dental clearance / OH optimize.
  • “Stop denosumab yourself for 6 months without MD” → wrong unsupervised plan.

STOP 35 sec: “Bisphosphonate/denosumab · exposed bone >8 wk · no radiation (else ORN) · prevent before therapy · don’t freestyle stop cancer drugs.”

H. Local anesthesia — amide/ester & max dose

H1. Amides vs esters

  • Amides (two “i”s in the name often helps: lidocaine, mepivacaine, articaine, bupivacaine, prilocaine): primarily hepatic metabolism. Most modern injectable dental LAs are amides.
  • Esters (procaine, benzocaine topical, tetracaine): hydrolyzed by plasma esterases; PABA metabolites historically linked to more allergic reactions. True allergy to amides is less common than patients claim — but always take allergy history seriously and do not challenge casually.
  • Articaine is an amide with an ester side chain (hybrid structure trivia sometimes tested) and good tissue diffusion.

H2. Max dose concepts (high‑yield numbers)

  • Lidocaine with epinephrine — classic teaching maximum about 7 mg/kg (absolute ceilings also exist in tables; weight‑based is the exam concept).
  • Lidocaine without vasoconstrictor often listed lower (~4.4–5 mg/kg in many teaching tables) because systemic absorption is faster without epi holding the drug locally.
  • Know cartridge math: 2% solution = 20 mg/mL. A 1.8 mL cartridge of 2% lidocaine contains about 36 mg lidocaine. Calculate total mg before stacking carpules in small adults/children.
  • Mepivacaine, articaine, bupivacaine each have their own max tables — exam most often hammers lidocaine+epi ~7 mg/kg and “do not exceed max / weight‑dose children.”
  • Bupivacaine: long duration; more cardiotoxic in overdose — respect max carefully.
  • Prilocaine: methemoglobinemia risk in overdose / certain patients (benzocaine topical also classic methemoglobinemia culprit in stems).

H3. Safe injection practice

  • Always: aspirate, inject slowly, smallest effective dose, never exceed max, dose children by weight, reverse if blood on aspiration (reposition).
  • Vasoconstrictor improves depth/duration and reduces peak plasma levels of LA for many procedures — but brings its own cautions (Section I).
  • Document total cartridges and concentrations when multiple techniques are used (IANB + long buccal + infiltration) so you do not accidentally exceed max across “a few more carpules.”
  • Pregnancy and significant hepatic disease change risk calculus — lowest effective dose, avoid unnecessary multi‑quadrant stacking, medical context matters.
  • Topical benzocaine still counts toward systemic risk stories (especially methemoglobinemia stems in children) — not “free and unlimited because topical.”

H3b. Cartridge math drill (exam skill)

  • Work in milligrams, not “number of carpules feels fine.” Example thinking: 2% lidocaine = 20 mg/mL; 1.8 mL carpule ≈ 36 mg lidocaine.
  • Adult 70 kg with lidocaine+epi ~7 mg/kg → theoretical ~490 mg lidocaine ceiling before absolute table caps — still use far less clinically; the point is weight‑based discipline, especially in small adults and children.
  • Child 20 kg: calculate max first, then decide how many carpules are even possible — banks love this trap.

H4. Exam stem patterns (LA dose chemistry)

  • “Amide vs ester metabolism” → liver vs plasma esterases.
  • “Max lidocaine with epi” → ~7 mg/kg concept.
  • “2% means” → 20 mg/mL.
  • “Child 20 kg, how many carpules roughly?” → calculate mg first, don’t guess adult carpule counts.

I. IANB failures, toxicity, epi & asthma

I1. Why IANB fails

  • Wrong height/depth relative to lingula/mandibular foramen; injection too anterior; insufficient volume; not waiting for onset; inflamed tissue (lower pH) reducing free base; accessory innervation (mylohyoid, cervical plexus contributions, bifid IAN, contralateral crossover anteriors).
  • Anatomic variation is real — repeating the exact same failed injection without reassessing landmarks is not a plan.

I2. Rescue strategies (concept level)

  • Reassess landmarks; repeat carefully; consider Gow‑Gates or Akinosi (Vazirani‑Akinosi) when taught for difficult lower anesthesia; PDL / intraosseous / buccal infiltration with articaine as adjuncts for residual soft tissue or “hot” teeth; wait adequate onset time.
  • For irreversible pulpitis “hot tooth,” supplemental techniques are classic — not endless lidocaine into the same wrong spot only.

I3. Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) sequence

  • Early CNS excitatory signs: metallic taste, tinnitus, circumoral numbness, agitation, visual/auditory changes, muscle twitches → seizures → CNS depression.
  • Cardiovascular: hypertension/tachycardia early possible → later bradycardia, conduction blocks, cardiovascular collapse (worse with bupivacaine stories).
  • Management: stop injection, call for help/EMS as needed, ABC, oxygen, airway support, manage seizures per emergency protocol, lipid emulsion therapy is advanced hospital care for severe LAST — know that severe cases leave the dental chair pathway.
  • Prevention: aspirate, slow inject, respect max dose, caution in small/elderly/hepatic impairment patients.

I4. Epinephrine cautions

  • Caution / minimize epi: uncontrolled hyperthyroidism, unstable ischemic heart disease, severe uncontrolled hypertension, certain refractory arrhythmias, recent cocaine use (catecholamine surge), some pheochromocytoma contexts.
  • Use lowest effective epi concentration/volume; plain LA when medically indicated; monitor vitals; medical consult for complex cardiac patients before elective surgery.
  • Intravascular epi → pounding heart, anxiety, BP rise — stop, reassure if self‑limited, support ABCs if severe.

I5. Asthma and analgesia cautions

  • For analgesia after dental work in asthmatics: prefer paracetamol (acetaminophen) when NSAID sensitivity is a concern.
  • NSAIDs can trigger bronchospasm in susceptible asthmatics (AERD / Samter’s triad pattern with nasal polyps + aspirin sensitivity — know the caution).
  • Steroid‑dependent asthmatics undergoing major procedures: discuss stress‑dose steroids with physician when indicated; control anxiety; avoid known triggers; have rescue inhaler available.
  • Sulfite sensitivity concerns appear in some epi‑containing cartridge discussions for severe asthmatics — use clinical judgment and history.

I6. Exam stem patterns (IANB/toxicity/epi/asthma)

  • “IANB failed twice, same height” → reassess landmarks / accessory innervation / alternate technique.
  • “Metallic taste, tinnitus after injection” → early LA toxicity → stop, O₂, support.
  • “Uncontrolled hyperthyroid needs extraction” → minimize epi / medical control.
  • “Asthmatic postop painkiller” → paracetamol preferred over NSAID if risk.

J. Medical emergencies (basics)

J1. Vasovagal syncope (most common)

  • Most common dental office emergency. Triggers: anxiety, pain, sight of blood/needle, fasting, upright position.
  • Signs: pallor, sweating, nausea, yawning, bradycardia, loss of consciousness if not treated early.
  • Management: supine with legs elevated, airway open, oxygen if needed, cool compress, monitor. Prevent with anxiety control, don’t treat extremely anxious patients bolt upright without prep, eat guidelines as appropriate.

J2. Anaphylaxis

  • Triggers: drugs (antibiotics, LA rare true allergy), latex, chlorhexidine rarely, etc.
  • Signs: urticaria, angioedema, wheeze/stridor, hypotension, GI symptoms, sense of doom — multi‑system.
  • IM epinephrine first (anterolateral thigh concept), call EMS, airway/oxygen, do not delay epi for oral antihistamine or “wait and see.” Antihistamines/steroids are adjuncts, not the first life‑saving drug.

J3. Hypoglycemia

  • Diabetic (or fasting anxious patient): sweating, tremor, confusion, irritability, tachycardia; can progress to seizure/coma.
  • Conscious cooperative: oral glucose / sugar gel / juice.
  • Unconscious: nothing forced orally (aspiration); EMS; glucagon IM/IN or IV glucose per training and protocols.

J4. Angina / MI and other quick hits

  • Chest pain: stop procedure, sit/semi‑upright as preferred, oxygen as indicated, patient’s GTN if prescribed for angina, aspirin if MI protocol allows and not contraindicated, EMS early if unrelieved or first‑time severe pain.
  • Hyperventilation: anxiety, tingling fingers, carpopedal spasm — coach slow breathing; do not give oxygen as first thought when pure hyperventilation without hypoxia (reassurance + rebreathing coaching per training).
  • Seizure: protect from injury, time the event, airway after convulsive phase, EMS if prolonged/first/unknown diabetic/LA toxicity context.
  • Aspiration/airway obstruction: stop, encourage cough if partial; EMS/advanced maneuvers if complete obstruction — another reason for throat packs/dam/ligatures on clamps.

J5. Exam stem patterns (emergencies)

  • “Pale sweaty anxious before injection, slow pulse” → vasovagal → supine legs up.
  • “Wheeze + hives + low BP after drug” → anaphylaxis → IM epi.
  • “Known DM, confused sweaty after skipping breakfast” → hypoglycemia → oral sugar if conscious.
  • “Crushing chest pain not relieved by rest” → EMS / MI pathway.

M. Rapid retrieval cards (speak once — 5 min)

M1. Exodontia and third molars

  • Extract when non‑restorable, hopeless perio, planned ortho, trauma beyond salvage, or pathology demands — after medical safety and consent.
  • Defer elective work for unstable cardiac, crisis hypertension/diabetes, recent major medical events, uncontrolled airway infection, unplanned high‑risk antiresorptive/radiation situations without a plan.
  • Winter = angulation of third molar relative to second (mesioangular common; distoangular/horizontal often harder).
  • Pell & Gregory Class 1–3 = space to ramus; Position A–C = depth; Class 3 + C = hardest access pattern banks love.
  • IAN risk on imaging: darkening, interrupted white line, diversion, narrowing → caution, imaging, specialist pathways, coronectomy concept when intimate roots.
  • Consent must name nerve injury for lower thirds and sinus communication for upper molars, plus dry socket and bleeding.

M2. Dry socket, bleeding, anticoagulants

  • Dry socket = severe pain 2–4 days later, empty socket, exposed bone, foul taste/odor; smoking raises risk; irrigate + medicated dressing + analgesia; not the same as day‑0 expected pain or Ludwig.
  • Local hemostasis is first: pressure 20–30 min, sutures, hemostatic agents, correct bite, patient instructions against spit/suck/smoke.
  • Primary bleeding during procedure; reactionary hours later when epi wears or BP rises; secondary days later often with infection.
  • Warfarin + stable therapeutic INR + simple extraction → classic exam answer: continue warfarin + meticulous local hemostasis; coordinate medicine when INR high or surgery extensive; never freestyle stop or invent bridging.
  • DOACs/dual antiplatelets: case‑by‑case with physician; exam still rewards local control + no unilateral cessation in stable simple scenarios.
  • Expanding floor hematoma or uncontrolled bleed → airway/ER pathway immediately.

M3. Spaces, fractures, MRONJ

  • Infections: mixed flora; airway + source control; antibiotics adjunct when spreading/systemic — not sole cure of undrained pus.
  • Canine space (nasolabial fold, upper canine); buccal space (cheek); submandibular (lower molars under border); sublingual elevates floor.
  • Ludwig angina = bilateral submandibular + sublingual + submental cellulitis with floor elevation and tongue displacement → hospital airway emergency.
  • Facial trauma ABC first; ZMC classic hook = diplopia plus cheek flattening/step/infraorbital numbness; mandible = malocclusion/step/mobility; Le Fort I floating maxilla, II pyramidal, III craniofacial disjunction language.
  • MRONJ: bisphosphonates, denosumab (± antiangiogenics); exposed bone >8 weeks; no jaw radiation (else ORN); prevent with dental clearance; do not freestyle‑stop oncology drugs.

M4. LA, toxicity, emergencies

  • Amides mainly hepatic metabolism; esters plasma esterases; most modern injectables are amides.
  • Lidocaine with epi classic max ≈ 7 mg/kg; without epi often lower; 2% = 20 mg/mL; ~36 mg per 1.8 mL carpule of 2% lidocaine; children by weight; aspirate and inject slowly.
  • IANB fails from landmarks, accessory innervation, inflamed tissue, haste — reassess and use supplemental/alternate techniques rather than only repeating the same miss.
  • Toxicity early: metallic taste, tinnitus, circumoral numbness → seizures → CV collapse; stop, O₂, ABC, EMS as needed.
  • Epi caution: uncontrolled hyperthyroid, unstable ischemia, severe uncontrolled HTN, some arrhythmias, recent cocaine.
  • Asthma analgesia: prefer paracetamol; caution NSAIDs/AERD.
  • Syncope most common: pale, sweaty, bradycardic → supine legs up. Anaphylaxis: IM epinephrine first. Hypoglycemia: oral sugar if conscious. Chest pain: stop, support, EMS pathway.

M5. Elimination habits for OMS stems

  • If the stem says day 3 severe pain and empty socket, do not pick “normal healing” or “start only IV antibiotics without local socket care.”
  • If the stem says stable warfarin simple XO, do not pick “stop warfarin five days without physician” as the best first plan.
  • If the stem says bilateral floor elevation and drooling, do not pick “home oral antibiotics and review next week.”
  • If the stem says cheek trauma and double vision, lock ZMC/orbital pathway before soft‑tissue‑only comfort answers.
  • If the stem lists metallic taste and tinnitus during injection, stop and treat toxicity — do not finish the carpule.
  • If the stem is asthma pain control, prefer paracetamol over casual NSAID when sensitivity risk is in play.
