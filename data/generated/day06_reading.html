A. Why Day 6 pays free points

A1. Blueprint and bank reality

  • Endodontics is roughly 17% of SDLE — large enough that weak diagnosis language costs many items even if your restorative is strong.
  • Banks prefer clinical language over gadget names: lingering cold, sinus tract, percussion tenderness, empty socket is Day 7 — here it is pulp/apex pairing, irrigant chemistry, and trauma tables.
  • Most free points come from: (1) pulp diagnosis words, (2) apical diagnosis words, (3) which test measures what, (4) irrigant jobs, (5) immature tooth strategy, (6) avulsion rules, (7) mishap recognition (MB2, file, ledge, perf, VRF).
  • Technique brand names rarely win. Mechanism + sequence wins: diagnose → access → WL at constriction → clean with chemistry → shape safely → obturate to terminus → coronal seal.

A2. Study rules that match the exam

  • Diagnose before treat. A stem that never tells you vitality or lingering vs brief pain is not asking for “best file system.”
  • Pair pulp status + apical status every time (AAE‑style thinking). Example: necrotic pulp + sinus = chronic apical abscess pathway language, not “reversible pulpitis.”
  • Trauma questions are table memory: displacement type, storage medium, extraoral dry time, splint type/duration, when RCT is likely.
  • Mishap stems reward mechanism: tip locked → torsion; continuous flex in curve → cyclic; strip on thin distal root → perforation risk; failed upper molar → hunt MB2.

A3. Day‑6 success picture

  • By tonight you should answer in under 10 seconds: reversible vs irreversible vs necrosis; SAP vs AAP vs acute vs chronic abscess; NaOCl vs EDTA vs CHX; apexogenesis vs apexification; avulsion milk/HBSS + dry time; VRF clues.
  • If you cannot, the problem is almost always skimmed diagnosis sections A–D, not missing a rare rotary brand fact.

B. Pulp diagnosis (exam language)

B1. Normal pulp

  • Normal pulp: asymptomatic; mild brief cold response that resolves almost instantly when stimulus is removed; no spontaneous pain; radiograph without pathosis attributed to that pulp.
  • Exam trap: “slight cold sensitivity after new filling with high occlusion” may be occlusion/PDL first — adjust bite before labeling irreversible disease.

B2. Reversible pulpitis

  • Reversible pulpitis: provoked pain (cold/sweet) that is brief and stops when the stimulus is removed. No spontaneous lingering night pain as the main story.
  • Cause is often caries, exposed dentin, recent restoration, cracked enamel with dentin involvement — the pulp is inflamed but expected to recover once the cause is removed and sealed.
  • Treatment logic: remove cause + restore/protect · monitor. Not RCT first‑line when history and tests match reversible pattern.
  • Stem pattern: “pain only when drinking cold, stops immediately, no night pain, caries in dentin” → reverse cause, not extract/RCT by default.

B3. Symptomatic irreversible pulpitis

  • Symptomatic irreversible pulpitis: lingering thermal pain (often cold; heat can also feature), spontaneous pain, night pain, pain that is hard to localize sometimes, analgesics may help only partly.
  • Pulp is still vital (responds, often exaggerated) but beyond reliable recovery with simple restoration alone → RCT or extraction depending on restorability/strategic value.
  • Classic bank hinge: lingering after cold is removed. Brief = reversible language; linger = irreversible language.
  • Do not treat irreversible pulpitis with “bigger composite only” and hope — that is a trap.

B4. Asymptomatic irreversible pulpitis

  • Vital pulp with inflammation not expected to heal (deep caries into pulp, hyperplastic pulpitis/pulp polyp in some young teeth, carious exposure patterns) even when the patient is not screaming with spontaneous pain.
  • Exam less dramatic than symptomatic irreversible, but still → definitive pulp therapy when criteria for irreversibility are met, not endless monitoring of exposed vital pulp under leakage.

B5. Pulp necrosis

  • Pulp necrosis: non‑vital pulp tissue. No reliable cold response; EPT usually no response (with caveats below).
  • May be quiet until the apex/periodontium reacts (SAP, abscess, sinus). Do not require severe pain for necrosis to be true.
  • Multi‑rooted teeth can give mixed responses if some canals vital and some necrotic — interpret carefully; one cold‑positive canal does not prove all canals healthy.

B6. Previously treated / previously initiated

  • Previously treated: obturated canals already present. Symptoms and apical status are interpreted separately from “virgin pulp” labels.
  • Previously initiated: partial endo started (pulpotomy/pulpectomy incomplete). Still diagnose current pulp/periapical condition with tests + imaging + history.
  • Persistent lesion after “completed” RCT → quality of previous treatment, missed canal (MB2!), vertical root fracture, non‑odontogenic pain — not automatically “more antibiotics.”

B7. Exam stem patterns (pulp)

  • “Cold hurts 2 seconds then gone” → reversible pattern until proven otherwise.
  • “Cold lingers minutes + wakes at night” → symptomatic irreversible.
  • “No cold/EPT response, asymptomatic, apical RL” → necrosis + apical disease (see Section C).
  • “Pain to sweets only, shallow lesion, brief” → reversible / dentin hypersensitivity management, not RCT.
  • “History of lingering pain last month, now silent, dark tooth, sinus” → necrosis evolved; do not call reversible because pain stopped.

STOP 45 sec: “Brief provoked = reversible · linger/spontaneous = irreversible · no response = necrosis · previously treated = separate pulp label · treat cause first only when reversible.”

C. Apical diagnoses (pair with pulp)

C1. Why apical labels exist

  • Apical diagnoses describe the periapical tissues, not the pulp itself. You can have SAP with vital pulp (occlusal trauma, recent prep) or with necrotic pulp.
  • Exam wants the pair: e.g. symptomatic irreversible pulpitis with SAP; pulp necrosis with chronic apical abscess.

C2. Normal apical tissues

  • Not tender to percussion or palpation; radiograph shows intact lamina dura and normal PDL width for that tooth.

C3. Symptomatic apical periodontitis (SAP)

  • SAP: pain on biting and/or percussion tenderness; ± widened PDL; patient may point to one tooth.
  • Can accompany vital inflamed pulp or necrotic pulp. Percussion does not prove vitality either way.
  • Management depends on pulp status: high restoration → adjust; irreversible/necrotic → endodontic pathway; pure occlusal trauma on vital pulp → occlusion first.

C4. Asymptomatic apical periodontitis (AAP)

  • AAP: apical radiolucency with little or no percussion pain. Classic silent chronic lesion under necrotic or previously treated tooth.
  • Do not ignore “because it doesn’t hurt.” Source control (RCT retreatment/extraction) is still the disease pathway conversation.

C5. Acute apical abscess

  • Acute apical abscess: rapid onset swelling, severe pain, purulence, possible systemic signs (fever, malaise), trismus if spread.
  • Needs drainage + source control (endo access/incision when indicated) — antibiotics alone are not the cure of a drainable odontogenic abscess.
  • Differentiate space infections that threaten airway (Day 7) from localized vestibular swelling.

C6. Chronic apical abscess

  • Chronic apical abscess: gradual process; often a draining sinus tract; less pain than acute abscess; necrotic pathway almost always.
  • Trace sinus with gutta‑percha cone + radiograph when the source tooth is unclear.
  • MCQ gold: sinus tract → chronic apical abscess language / necrotic infection pathway — not reversible pulpitis.

C7. Condensing osteitis and other PA patterns

  • Condensing osteitis (focal sclerosing osteomyelitis pattern): localized apical radiopacity from low‑grade long‑standing pulp inflammation; tooth often has large restoration/caries history; may be vital or need endo depending on pulp tests — know the radiopaque apical story.
  • Phoenix abscess language (flare of chronic lesion) appears in some banks as acute exacerbation of chronic apical disease.

C8. Exam stem patterns (apical)

  • “Tender to percussion, no swelling, vital lingering cold” → irreversible + SAP type pairing.
  • “Sinus tract, nonvital, little pain” → chronic apical abscess.
  • “Rapid facial swelling, fever, nonvital molar” → acute apical abscess / spreading infection pathway.
  • “Asymptomatic apical RL on routine PA” → AAP until proven otherwise; still needs diagnosis of pulp/previous treatment.
  • “Pus or draining sinus” never equals reversible pulpitis.

D. Tests — vitality, percussion, palpation

D1. Cold testing (mainstay)

  • Cold is the everyday vitality workhorse. Dry tooth, isolate, compare to controls (adjacent and contralateral).
  • Brief sharp response that vanishes quickly → consistent with normal or reversible patterns depending on history.
  • Lingering pain after cold removed → irreversible pulpitis language.
  • No response → suspect necrosis, but confirm with second test and radiograph/history (calcified canals, immature apex, recent trauma “shock,” large restorations, multi‑canal confusion).

D2. EPT (electric pulp test)

  • EPT mainly answers presence vs absence of responsive pulp tissue — not a precise “health score.” Magic number chasing is weak exam thinking.
  • False negatives: immature apex, recent trauma, heavy calcifications, patient anxiety, poor contact/conductor, large restorations blocking current.
  • False positives: conduction to gingiva/adjacent tooth if isolation poor; partial necrosis in multi‑rooted teeth.
  • Never use EPT as the only decision tool when cold + radiograph + history already clear.

D3. Percussion and palpation (periodontium, not vitality)

  • Percussion flags PDL inflammation (apical periodontitis, acute trauma, high occlusion). It is not a vitality test.
  • Palpation over the apex detects swelling, pointing, cortical expansion — abscess/pathology signs.
  • Stem trap: “positive percussion so the pulp is dead” — false. Vital teeth with SAP exist.

D4. Bite tests, probing, mobility, radiograph

  • Bite / Tooth Slooth: crack or SAP; pain on release classically suggests cracked tooth syndrome patterns.
  • Probing: generalized deep pockets → perio story. Isolated deep narrow pocket on a root → think VRF or draining sinus along periodontal ligament — not routine horizontal periodontitis alone.
  • Mobility: trauma, perio, root fracture, acute abscess elevation — integrate, don’t single‑factor.
  • Radiograph: caries depth, prior RCT quality, apical RL/RO, root morphology, resorption, J‑shaped lesions, missed canals hints. Two angles help when anatomy confuses.

D5. Putting the exam algorithm together

  • History (spontaneous? linger? trauma? sinus?) → cold + control → percussion/palpation → bite/probe as needed → PA radiograph → pulp + apical labels → treatment tree.
  • Do not extract a vital restorable tooth for one vague pain episode without tests.
  • Do not start RCT on a clear reversible pattern just because the patient is anxious.

D6. Exam stem patterns (tests)

  • “Which test assesses pulp vitality?” → cold/EPT — not percussion.
  • “Tender to percussion only finding after high crown” → SAP/occlusion path; check vitality before endo.
  • “No cold response after blow to tooth yesterday” → may be temporary; retest, don’t automatic RCT the same day in every immature trauma case (follow trauma protocols).
  • “Isolated 9 mm pocket mid‑buccal on RCT tooth + J lesion” → VRF until proven otherwise.

STOP 45 sec: “Cold + controls · EPT = yes/no · percussion = PDL not vitality · palpation = swell · isolated deep pocket = VRF flag · history + tests + radiograph.”

E. Access, working length, apical constriction

E1. Access goals

  • Access objectives: straight‑line path to canal orifices, complete pulp chamber roof removal, conserve peri‑cervical dentin, find all canals, avoid gouging furcation or stripping walls.
  • Inadequate access → missed canals (MB2!), ledges, instrument stress, poor irrigation exchange.
  • Over‑aggressive access → weakened tooth, perforation risk (especially furcal floor in molars when depth misjudged).

E2. Working length concept

  • Working length (WL) = distance from a stable coronal reference point to the apical preparation terminus.
  • Classic biologic target: the apical constriction (minor apical foramen / minor diameter) — the narrowest canal diameter near the end of the canal system.
  • Constriction is often roughly 0.5–1 mm short of the radiographic apex, but anatomy varies (resorption, open apex, apical deltas). Do not worship a single millimeter number without confirmation tools.

E3. How WL is established and confirmed

  • Electronic apex locator + radiographic confirmation is modern standard thinking. Wet/dry conditions and metal restorations can affect locators — interpret with radiograph.
  • Reference point must be reproducible (cusp tip, incisal edge) — if you change the reference mid‑case, WL is meaningless.
  • After coronal flaring and during shaping, re‑check WL — canal straightening can effectively shorten measured length.

E4. Over‑instrumentation and long WL

  • Preparing long (past apex) inflames periapical tissues, seeds debris/irrigant extrude risk, and sets up overfill.
  • Bank concept: WL grossly long (e.g. ~2 mm past radiographic apex thinking) is a serious error — recalibrate before obturation.
  • Open apex / immature teeth need different apical strategies (Section I) — do not force a “standard constriction” story when constriction is absent.

E5. Exam stem patterns (access/WL)

  • “Ideal apical terminus of instrumentation historically” → apical constriction.
  • “File 2 mm beyond radiographic apex on WL film” → too long; adjust.
  • “Persistent disease after RCT, untreated second canal in MB root” → missed MB2 / inadequate access.
  • “Furcal radiolucency after access on molar, bleeding from floor” → furcal perforation until proven otherwise.

STOP 40 sec: “Straight‑line access · find all canals · WL at apical constriction · often ~0.5–1 mm short of radiographic apex · don’t obturate past terminus casually.”

F. Irrigants — NaOCl, EDTA, CHX (+ precipitate trap)

F1. NaOCl (sodium hypochlorite) — primary irrigant

  • NaOCl is the primary endodontic irrigant in almost every modern protocol stem.
  • Jobs: dissolves organic tissue (pulp remnants), broad antimicrobial action, helps clean where files cannot mechanically reach.
  • Concentration and volume/exchange matter more than brand of syringe. Activate/replenish — stagnant fluid does less work.
  • Hypochlorite accident: extrusion beyond apex → sudden severe pain, swelling, ecchymosis. Stop irrigation under pressure, emergency supportive care pathway — prevention: needle not locked in canal, side‑vented needles, slow delivery, correct WL.

F2. EDTA — inorganic smear layer

  • EDTA chelates calcium — removes the inorganic component of the smear layer created by instrumentation, opens dentinal tubules, improves sealer adaptation/penetration concepts.
  • EDTA does not replace NaOCl for tissue dissolution. Different jobs: organic vs inorganic.
  • Typical exam phrasing: “remove smear layer” → EDTA (or chelator family). “Dissolve pulp tissue / kill microbes as main irrigant” → NaOCl.

F3. Chlorhexidine (CHX)

  • CHX: antimicrobial with substantivity (binds dentin, prolonged effect). Used in some final‑rinse or retreatment protocols.
  • CHX does not dissolve necrotic tissue the way NaOCl does — do not treat CHX as a full NaOCl substitute for organic dissolution.

F4. CRITICAL trap — NaOCl + CHX precipitate

  • Never mix NaOCl and CHX in the canal. Interaction forms a brown/orange precipitate (para‑chloroaniline concern in literature) that is hard to remove, can stain, and may occlude tubules / interfere with seal.
  • If a protocol uses both in sequence: intermediate flush with sterile water or saline (and dry/aspirate) so they do not meet in the canal.
  • This is one of the highest‑yield irrigant MCQ traps in SDLE‑style banks.

F5. Chemistry vs files — who cleans?

  • Files shape and create space; irrigants clean (chemistry + volume + exchange ± activation).
  • Over‑reliance on large files without irrigation is a failed philosophy; so is flooding NaOCl without shaping a path for exchange.
  • Activation concepts (sonic/ultrasonic/manual dynamic irrigation) appear in modern stems as ways to improve exchange — still subordinate to correct chemistry choice and safe delivery.
  • Final rinse sequences vary by school; what does not vary on exams: NaOCl’s organic role, EDTA’s smear role, and the NaOCl–CHX incompatibility.

F5b. Clinical irrigation safety checklist

  • Confirm WL before aggressive irrigation deep in the canal.
  • Never bind the needle; prefer side‑vented needles; express slowly; keep needle moving out slightly during delivery.
  • If sudden severe pain and rapid swelling mid‑irrigation → stop, treat as hypochlorite accident pathway (supportive care, cold packs as taught, analgesics, steroids/antibiotics per protocol, close follow‑up, document, inform).
  • Rubber dam isolation protects soft tissues and improves control — endo without isolation is a process failure on most exam philosophies.

F6. Exam stem patterns (irrigants)

  • “Best tissue solvent among common irrigants” → NaOCl.
  • “Removes smear layer / chelates mineral” → EDTA.
  • “Antimicrobial with substantivity, poor tissue dissolve” → CHX.
  • “Brown precipitate after two irrigants” → NaOCl mixed with CHX.
  • “Sudden severe pain + swelling during irrigation” → NaOCl extrusion accident.

STOP 45 sec: “NaOCl = organic + kill · EDTA = smear mineral · CHX = kill, no dissolve · NEVER NaOCl+CHX · intermediate saline/water if both used · files shape, irrigants clean.”

G. Obturation basics and overfill

G1. Goals of obturation

  • Obturate only after adequate cleaning and shaping. Obturation seals the prepared system; it does not sterilize a dirty canal by magic.
  • Common core: gutta‑percha + sealer. Bioceramic sealers are modern high‑yield options in many protocols.
  • Sealer fills irregularities, tubules, and accessory anatomy; GP is the core mass. Aim for a thin sealer film — not a sealer‑only fill with voids of soft cement.

G2. Length of fill

  • Target: fill to the prepared terminus near the constriction / ideal radiographic terminus without gross overextension into periapical tissues.
  • Overfill / overextension risks: post‑op pain, foreign‑body reaction, delayed healing, poorer outcomes; near IAN/mental foramen can threaten neurosensory structures.
  • Underfill with residual bacteria and empty apical canal also fails. Both extremes are wrong; clean length + dense fill + coronal seal is the triad.

G3. Coronal seal is not optional

  • Long‑term success depends as much on coronal seal (timely definitive restoration) as on pretty apical GP.
  • Delayed temporary washout → coronal leakage → failure even if “WL looked perfect.”
  • Restorability check before heroic endo: no ferrule, vertical root fracture, non‑restorable crown — extraction may be the correct “endo decision.”

G4. Exam stem patterns (obturation)

  • “GP beyond apex, severe postop pain” → overfill/overextension complication.
  • “Short fill 3 mm from apex + residual symptoms” → inadequate length/cleaning story.
  • “Perfect RCT radiograph, temporary lost for months, new RL” → coronal leakage.
  • “Sealer puffs small vs gross overfill” — know gross overfill is the bank villain; tiny sealer puff is not always catastrophic, but stems usually punish obvious overextension.

STOP 35 sec: “Clean then fill · GP + sealer · length to constriction · overfill hurts · underfill fails · coronal seal = long‑term success.”

H. File separation, ledge, perforation, MB2

H1. Torsional failure (twist‑off)

  • Torsional failure: the file tip locks in the canal while the shank keeps rotating → elastic limit exceeded → fracture.
  • Classic setup: forcing rotary into a canal without a glide path, packing debris, or engaging too aggressively in a narrow apical third.
  • Prevention: create glide path with small hand files, light pecking, do not push, proper torque/speed, don’t skip sizes recklessly, keep canals patent and irrigated.

H2. Cyclic (flexural) fatigue

  • Cyclic fatigue: continuous flexing in a curvature causes crack propagation until the file snaps — even without a locked tip.
  • Risk rises with sharp curves, large taper/large diameter instruments, prolonged continuous rotation in the curve, and overused files.
  • Prevention: limit reuses of rotary files, reduce stress with coronal flaring, light pressure, consider reciprocating systems where taught, inspect files for unwind/defects.

H3. Separated instrument management philosophy

  • Inform the patient; document. Not every fragment must be removed if bypass and disinfection are achievable and risk of removal is higher (especially deep apical fragments in thin roots).
  • Retrieval tools: magnification/microscope, ultrasonics, loop kits — when fragment is accessible and tooth structure allows. Gouging a trough that perforates is worse than a bypassed fragment.
  • Exam often tests mechanism (torsion vs cyclic) more than brand of remover.

H4. Ledge formation

  • Ledge: artificial irregularity short of the true terminus, often from straight stiff files forced in curved canals, skipping sizes, or loss of patency.
  • Recognition: file no longer reaches prior WL; radiograph shows file tip away from canal path.
  • Management concept: pre‑curve small stainless hand files, gentle negotiation to bypass, restore patency; do not force larger NiTi into a false path and worsen the ledge or create a perforation.

H5. Perforation

  • Perforation = iatrogenic communication from canal/chamber to PDL/bone (furcal, strip, apical, lateral).
  • Strip perforation: classic on thin distal wall of mesial roots of mandibular molars when over‑flaring the danger zone.
  • Prognosis better when perforation is small, fresh, sealed immediately, and coronal rather than neglected infected.
  • Repair material high‑yield: MTA / calcium silicate bioceramics for many perforation repairs. Hemostasis + clean field + seal.

H6. MB2 — the canal that fails “perfect” upper molars

  • Maxillary molars frequently have a second mesiobuccal canal (MB2). Prevalence is high enough that “I only found three canals” is a risk statement, not a victory lap.
  • Location concept: MB2 is usually palatal to MB1 orifice under a dentin shelf — needs troughing under magnification, not random deep drilling.
  • Classic stem: persistent MB‑root apical lesion after RCT that shows only one MB fill → hunt MB2 / retreatment, not endless antibiotics.

H7. Exam stem patterns (mishaps/MB2)

  • “Tip locked, shank rotated, snap” → torsional failure.
  • “File breaks in sharp curve after long use, tip was free” → cyclic fatigue.
  • “Sudden bleeding from furcation during access” → perforation.
  • “Best material to seal perforation” → MTA/bioceramic concept.
  • “Upper molar still symptomatic, PA shows untreated second line in MB root” → MB2.

I. Immature apex — apexogenesis vs apexification

I1. Why immature teeth are different

  • Immature permanent teeth have thin walls and open apices. Goals shift toward allowing continued root development when pulp vitality can be preserved.
  • A thin short root that loses vitality is structurally weak even after obturation — that is why vital pulp strategies matter.

I2. Apexogenesis (vital path — keep growing)

  • Apexogenesis: vital pulp therapy in an immature tooth so radicular pulp remains vital → continued root lengthening, wall thickening, and natural apical closure.
  • Examples of procedures in this family: partial pulpotomy, full pulpotomy (case‑dependent), direct pulp cap in highly selected cases — material often MTA/bioceramic.
  • Requires: vital radicular pulp, controllable bleeding, no established necrosis/abscess pathway as the main story.
  • Memory: genesis = growth continues because pulp lives.

I3. Apexification (non‑vital path — build a barrier)

  • Apexification: non‑vital immature tooth needs an apical stop so you can obturate. Modern approach: place an MTA/bioceramic apical barrier then backfill the canal.
  • Classic multi‑visit calcium hydroxide apexification still appears in older stems (induce hard tissue barrier over months) — know it historically, but MTA barrier is the modern high‑yield answer.
  • Memory: fication = make an apex barrier because pulp is dead and natural closure will not reliably finish via vital growth.

I4. Regenerative endodontics (concept level)

  • Regenerative/revitalization protocols for selected necrotic immature teeth aim for continued root development via biologic disinfection + scaffold/blood clot + coronal seal — exam may mention as alternative to classic apexification in young patients.
  • For SDLE free points, still master the binary: vital immature → apexogenesis family; necrotic immature → apexification/MTA barrier (or regen when taught).

I5. Exam stem patterns (immature apex)

  • “Open apex, vital pulp after trauma, want root to mature” → apexogenesis / vital pulp therapy.
  • “Open apex, necrotic, need to obturate” → apexification with MTA apical barrier.
  • “Material for apical barrier in immature necrotic tooth” → MTA.
  • “Why not conventional WL constriction story?” → constriction may not exist yet.

STOP 40 sec: “Apexogenesis = vital grow · apexification = dead immature + MTA barrier · regen = selected necrotic immature alternative · thin walls need growth when possible.”

J. Dental trauma table (high‑yield)

J1. Concussion and subluxation

  • Concussion: injury to PDL without increased mobility or displacement; tooth tender to percussion. Usually no splint. Monitor vitality serially.
  • Subluxation: increased mobility without displacement; ± sulcular bleeding. Flexible splint short term if mobility significant; vitality follow‑up essential.

J2. Luxation injuries (displaced but still in socket)

  • Extrusive luxation: tooth displaced axially outward, appears elongated, mobile. Reposition into socket, flexible splint, monitor vitality; mature teeth often need RCT if pulp becomes necrotic.
  • Lateral luxation: displaced buccally/lingually/mesially/distally, often locked in bone (metallic sound, high percussion). Reposition (may need to free from bony lock), flexible splint; high endodontic follow‑up yield in mature teeth.
  • Intrusive luxation: tooth driven into bone; highest risk of pulp necrosis and root resorption among luxations (especially mature closed apex). Management may allow spontaneous re‑eruption (immature) vs orthodontic/surgical reposition (mature) per guideline patterns — close follow‑up is non‑negotiable.

J3. Avulsion — time, medium, splint (must be automatic)

  • Avulsion = complete displacement out of socket. Extraoral dry time and storage medium dominate prognosis (PDL cell survival).
  • If the tooth is a permanent tooth suitable for replantation and clean: immediate replant at site when possible (rinse gently, do not scrub PDL, seat into socket).
  • If cannot replant immediately, store in: Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS) ideal when available · cold milk excellent practical choice · saliva (buccal vestibule) acceptable short term · not dry gauze · plain water is poor (hypotonic damage to PDL cells).
  • Extraoral dry time critical: prolonged dry time (>60 min classically discussed) severely worsens PDL survival → higher ankylosis/replacement resorption risk. Exact protocol branches (delayed replant preparation, etc.) follow IADT‑style teaching — banks still key on “dry time + medium.”
  • After replant: flexible splint often about 2 weeks for uncomplicated permanent avulsion (root fracture and alveolar fracture need longer stabilization — see below).
  • Tetanus status; systemic antibiotics when indicated by guidelines/local teaching; soft diet; CHX rinses often advised; urgent dental follow‑up.
  • Mature closed‑apex avulsed teeth commonly require RCT within roughly the first 7–10 days (IADT teaching patterns) because pulp revascularization is unlikely — do not wait years for a necrotic infection/resorption spiral.
  • Immature open‑apex avulsion: revascularization possible in some cases — monitor carefully; different endo timing than mature teeth.
  • Primary tooth avulsion: generally do not replant (risk to permanent successor) — classic pedo/trauma free point (also Day 9).

J4. Root fracture and alveolar fracture

  • Root fracture: reposition coronal fragment; flexible splint often longer than simple avulsion (mid‑root frequently cited around ~4 weeks in teaching tables — know “longer than 2 weeks avulsion” if exact week not recalled). Apical fragment often remains vital.
  • Alveolar fracture: bone segment + teeth move as a unit. Reposition the segment; stabilize longer (classically ~4 weeks); assess each tooth’s endodontic status over time — do not automatically RCT every tooth on day one without diagnosis.

J5. Crown fracture and pulp exposure (brief)

  • Enamel‑only / enamel‑dentin fractures: protect dentin, restore, vitality follow‑up.
  • With pulp exposure: immature vital → vital pulp therapy (partial pulpotomy/MTA) often preferred to keep apexogenesis path; mature with large exposure/time delay may need full endo — history and maturity drive the choice.

J6. Exam stem patterns (trauma)

  • “Tooth knocked out, stored in milk 20 min, closed apex” → replant, flexible splint ~2 wk, plan early RCT pathway.
  • “Avulsed tooth in dry napkin 3 hours” → poor PDL prognosis / high ankylosis risk language.
  • “Best storage medium listed” → HBSS > milk > saliva ≫ water/dry.
  • “Primary avulsed incisor” → do not replant.
  • “Intrusion mature tooth” → high necrosis/resorption risk; close follow‑up / repositioning strategy.

K. Resorption flags and VRF

K1. Internal root resorption

  • Internal resorption: starts in the pulp canal and expands outward. May present as a pink coronal spot (pink tooth of Mummery) when coronal.
  • Radiograph: canal appears ballooned / enlarged, often more symmetric; the canal outline may be continuous with the defect.
  • Progress requires vital pulp tissue (or residual vital tissue). Treatment when diagnosed: RCT to remove the tissue driving resorption (if restorable and not perforated hopelessly).

K2. External root resorption

  • External resorption: starts on the PDL/root surface side. Types conceptually: surface, inflammatory (often infection + damaged cementum after trauma), replacement/ankylosis (common after severe luxation/avulsion with PDL death).
  • Radiograph tricks: irregular root surface; canal walls often still traceable through the defect (classic teaching contrast vs internal).
  • Inflammatory external resorption linked to necrotic pulp after trauma needs timely pulp removal/infection control. Replacement resorption/ankylosis is a different beast (metallic sound, infraocclusion in growing patients).

K3. Vertical root fracture (VRF)

  • VRF clues: often post‑endo teeth (especially with posts), isolated deep narrow periodontal pocket, J‑shaped or teardrop lateral radiolucency, pain on biting, optional sinus tract near mid‑root level.
  • Prognosis for the fractured root is often poor → extraction or root resection in selected multi‑rooted cases. Endless non‑surgical re‑RCT of a classic VRF pattern wastes time and bone.
  • Differentiate: primary perio disease is usually broader pockets and different distribution; VRF is localized and dramatic next to a root‑filled tooth.

K4. Exam stem patterns (resorption/VRF)

  • “Pink spot, ballooned canal” → internal resorption → RCT.
  • “History of avulsion, metallic percussion, infraocclusion” → replacement resorption/ankylosis pattern.
  • “RCT + post, isolated deep pocket, J‑lesion” → VRF.
  • “Canal outline continuous through lateral defect” → think external; “canal expands into defect” → internal (classic contrast).

STOP 40 sec: “Internal = pulp out, balloon canal, RCT · external = PDL side, irregular, canal traceable · VRF = isolated deep pocket + J‑lesion on RCT tooth · don’t endless re‑RCT classic VRF.”

N. Rapid retrieval cards (speak once — 4 min)

N1. Diagnosis pairs you will see tomorrow

  • Lingering cold + spontaneous night pain + vital exaggerated response → symptomatic irreversible pulpitis (± SAP if percussion hurts).
  • No cold/EPT + sinus tract → necrosis + chronic apical abscess.
  • No cold/EPT + rapid swell/pus/fever → necrosis + acute apical abscess → drain + source control.
  • Asymptomatic apical RL under old RCT → AAP / previously treated pathway; quality of prior fill and missed canals matter.
  • Brief cold only after shallow caries → reversible → restore cause, not RCT first.

N2. Chemistry and length in one breath

  • NaOCl dissolves organic tissue and kills microbes; do not extrude; side‑vented slow delivery.
  • EDTA removes mineral smear so sealer can adapt; it is not a tissue solvent replacement for NaOCl.
  • CHX has antimicrobial substantivity but poor tissue dissolve; intermediate water/saline if sequenced after NaOCl so they never mix.
  • NaOCl + CHX mixture → brown/orange precipitate trap — automatic wrong answer to “mix freely.”
  • WL target = apical constriction; often ~0.5–1 mm short of radiographic apex; re‑check after flaring.
  • Obturate clean canals to terminus; overfill hurts; underfill fails; coronal seal decides the decade, not only the GP tip.

N3. Mishaps and immature roots

  • Tip locked while rotating → torsional separation; continuous flex in curve → cyclic fatigue.
  • Ledge → pre‑curve small hand files, restore patency; do not force larger rotary into a false path.
  • Perforation → clean, hemostasis, MTA/bioceramic repair when indicated; strip risk on thin distal of mesial lower molar roots.
  • Failed upper molar with one MB line → hunt MB2 under magnification.
  • Immature vital → apexogenesis (keep growth); immature necrotic → apexification with MTA barrier (or regen when taught).

N4. Trauma and resorption final drill

  • Avulsion permanent: replant if appropriate; store HBSS > milk > saliva; never dry napkin; dry time kills PDL; flexible splint ~2 weeks uncomplicated; mature teeth often need early RCT.
  • Primary avulsion: usually do not replant.
  • Intrusion mature: high necrosis and resorption risk — close follow‑up and guideline repositioning logic.
  • Internal resorption: canal balloons from inside; pink spot possible; RCT removes driving tissue.
  • External: PDL side irregularity; canal often traceable through defect; replacement/ankylosis after severe luxation/avulsion.
  • VRF: post‑endo tooth + isolated deep narrow pocket + J/teardrop lesion → poor root prognosis, not endless re‑RCT theater.
