A. Why Day 4 exists (integration, not more theory)

  • Days 1–3 built operative, fixed/implant, removable/materials. Day 4 forces mixed retrieval under time — what the real exam does. The exam does not hand you a pure “amalgam only” booklet; it shuffles open contact, dam hole spacing, Kennedy class, IAN 2 mm, and eugenol‑under‑resin in the same hour.
  • Score collapses when you “know it” slowly. Integration day builds automaticity: stem → category → hinge fact → rule → click. That pipeline must fit inside ~72 seconds average.
  • If you only re‑read, you feel productive and stay weak. Re‑reading produces recognition; timed MCQs produce retrieval. Timed volume + wrong book is the product of Day 4. Recognition without speed fails the Prometric clock.
  • Restorative remains the largest slice (~40%). A strong Day 4 multiplies Days 1–3; a lazy Day 4 wastes them. You are not “behind on theory” if Days 1–3 were done — you are behind if traps still take 3 minutes to recall.
  • What Day 4 is not: not Day 1 redo, not a new materials encyclopedia, not optional “if energy left.” It is the conversion day from notes into exam behavior.
  • ADHD trap of the day: opening Day‑1 video “just to refresh one thing” and disappearing for 90 minutes. Allowed exception: one named weak lecture after triage proves a bucket is <60%. Not before the first timed 100.
  • Emotional contract: feeling stupid on a timed set is data, not a verdict. Write the rule. Move. Feeling clever after only re‑reading is a false positive.

C. Exam pace strategy — 72 seconds per question

  • Math you must feel, not just recite: 200 questions / ~4 hours ≈ 72 seconds per question average. One section 100 Q / 120 min = same ~72 s. That is the average — not a law that every item gets exactly 72.
  • Budget by difficulty feel: easy <40 s · medium ~60–75 s · hard max ~90–100 s then flag. Never donate 3–4 minutes to one stem in the first half of a section. Early overspend creates late panic and random clicking.
  • Pass structure: First pass — answer all you can at speed. Second pass — flags only with fresh eyes. Third — remaining empties. Standard one‑best‑answer strategy with no negative marking teaching: answer everything; blank is worse than an educated guess.
  • Long clinical vignette method: read the last sentence / lead‑in question first, then scan the stem for the hinge (age, uncontrolled DM, “food packing,” “wrinkled dam,” distances, Kennedy clues, “what is the next step”). Do not narrate the whole story like a novel if the ask is one fact.
  • When to change an answer: only when you find a concrete rule you misread (“I missed open contact in the stem”) — not when anxiety says “maybe B feels safer.” Anxiety changes usually net loss.
  • Section fatigue: accuracy drops late. Stand during breaks. Caffeine plan practiced on Day 4, not invented on exam morning. If you crash at Q 80 in practice, that is a stamina training signal, not “I’m dumb.”
  • Flag discipline: flag means “return with remaining time,” not “this item owns my nervous system for 5 minutes now.” Hard item early: pick best guess, flag, move. Protect average pace.
  • Pace checkpoints (use in timed 100): at 30 min ≈ Q 25 done · at 60 min ≈ Q 50 · at 90 min ≈ Q 75 · at 110 min finish last items / start flag pass. If you are at Q 40 after 60 min, you are behind — tighten easy items harder, not harder items longer.
  • Stem patterns that steal time: multi‑paragraph medical history when the ask is pure contact/occlusion; photo descriptions that do not change the classic rule; two correct‑sounding options where one is “always‑comes” and one is rare specialty path — pick always‑comes under speed.

STOP 30 sec: 72 sec/Q average · flag hard · first pass volume · change only with a rule · answer every item · checkpoints at 30/60/90 min.

I. How to run the actual practice blocks today (this is the real work)

  • Warm‑up 50 (learn mode): accuracy, read explanations, fill wrong book. No shame in 70% — you are loading working memory and finding holes. Do not skip explanations on misses.
  • Timed 100 (exam posture): 72 s average, flags, no notes except optional scratch memory sheet at start if you simulate strictly. Same chair rules as exam if possible.
  • Review 45–60 min: only misses + lucky guesses. Convert to rules. Re‑answer without looking until correct. Review is where score growth happens; raw volume without review is treadmill theater.
  • Endurance / weak drill decision tree:
    • If timed ≥75% → mixed endurance 100–150 to build stamina
    • If timed 70–74% → mixed 50 + pure top weak bucket 50
    • If timed <70% → pure weakest bucket 50 then short mixed 50 (competence before interleaving)
  • Cards 20 min: always‑comes deck + any deck matching top weak bucket. Rapid fire, out loud if alone.
  • Stop rule: when rules are written and energy is garbage, finishing a sloppy 200th question without review teaches guessing. Quality review beats empty volume at the margin.
  • App hygiene: use restorative‑heavy filters when available; if forced mixed bank, still log only restorative misses into today’s primary wrong book and note non‑resto for later days.
  • Honest practice vs fake practice: fake = untimed with notes open, looking up every stem. Real = closed notes, timer on, review after. Day 4 fails if you only do fake practice then feel “busy.”
  • Stem patterns to expect in volume sets:

Appendix B — domain picker under 10 seconds (integration muscle)

  • When a stem lands, your first job is domain, not emotion. Domain wrong → rule wrong → miss. Practice saying the domain out loud during warm‑up 50.
  • Operative domain cues: cavity class, dam, composite type, amalgam, pulp cap, caries removal depth, matrix/wedge, C‑factor, eugenol under composite, GIC fluoride, infection control instrument order in a restorative clinic vignette.
  • Fixed domain cues: crown/bridge complaint after cementation, contact, occlusion, margin, porcelain fracture, provisional problem, finish line choice, ferrule, post/core, impression for crown, cement type, biologic width around a crown.
  • Implant domain cues: distances (IAN/tooth/implant), mucositis vs peri‑implantitis, uncontrolled DM wanting fixtures, residual cement at implant crown.
  • RPD domain cues: Kennedy class language, free‑end, clasp/RPI, major connector bar vs plate, indirect retention, lateral force destruction.
  • CD domain cues: CR record, VDO high/low signs, post dam, sore spots after insertion, reline vs rebase, upper drops on speech.
  • Materials domain cues: which impression material, pour time, gypsum type for dies, acrylic porosity from cure cycle.
  • Medical free‑point cues: asthma analgesic, enlargement drugs, uncontrolled systemic disease deferral of elective implants, transplant stories (defer elective — Day 8 deepens this).
