A. Angle classification (must automatic)

A1. Why Angle still wins free points

  • Photo and vignette questions need molar key first, then incisor pattern for Class II subdivisions. Do not invent skeletal cephalometric analysis on a 72-second item unless the stem gives clear skeletal cues.
  • Angle is a dental molar (and historically canine) relationship classification. A patient can be Class I molars with terrible crowding (Class I malocclusion) or Class II molars with different face types.
  • Study mode: hold up your hands — right hand upper 6 MB cusp, left hand lower 6 buccal groove — and say Class I / II / III out loud until automatic.

A2. Class I — neutrocclusion

  • Class I: mesiobuccal cusp of the maxillary first permanent molar occludes in the buccal groove of the mandibular first permanent molar.
  • Canines often: maxillary canine between lower canine and first premolar (Class I canine).
  • Malocclusion can still exist: crowding, open bite, crossbite, bimaxillary protrusion with Class I molars. “Class I” ≠ “perfect occlusion.”

A3. Class II — distocclusion + divisions

  • Class II: mandibular first molar is distal relative to the maxillary first molar (mandible “behind” / maxilla relatively ahead in dental terms). MB cusp of upper 6 is ahead of the lower buccal groove (often in embrasure ahead of lower 6).
  • Division 1: Class II molars + maxillary incisors proclined + increased overjet. Often lip trap, incompetent lips, narrow upper arch, traumatic overjet risk to upper incisors.
  • Division 2: Class II molars + maxillary central incisors retroclined + typically deep overbite; lateral incisors may be proclined/flared; overjet often normal or reduced — not the big OJ of Div1. Exam photo: retroclined centrals + deep bite + Class II molars = Div2.
  • Never call Div2 “proclined big OJ.” That is Div1. Mixing Div1/Div2 is the most expensive 10% mistake.

A4. Class III — mesiocclusion

  • Class III: mandibular first molar is mesial relative to maxillary first molar (mandible ahead or maxilla deficient dentally).
  • Anteriors may be edge-to-edge or reverse overjet (lower incisors ahead of uppers).
  • Pseudo-Class III: functional shift / premature contacts force mandible forward — different from true skeletal Class III. Check CR vs CO and early contacts when stems mention shift.
  • Adult skeletal Class III often needs ortho + orthognathic surgery pathways (BSSO / maxillary advancement concepts in high-level stems) — not only tip teeth forever.

A5. Canines, photos, and exam speed

  • Canine classification often parallels molars but mixed cases exist — if photo is unclear on molars, use canines + overjet as support.
  • Photo algorithm: (1) find 6s (2) Class I/II/III molars (3) if Class II, look at upper incisors: proclined+OJ↑ = Div1; retroclined centrals+deep bite = Div2.
  • Deciduous molars / flush terminal plane / mesial step history matter for prediction but exam still loves permanent Angle keys.

A6. Exam stem patterns (Angle)

  • “MB cusp of U6 in buccal groove of L6” → Class I.
  • “Lower molar distal, proclined uppers, large overjet” → Class II division 1.
  • “Lower molar distal, retroclined upper centrals, deep bite” → Class II division 2.
  • “Lower molar mesial, reverse overjet” → Class III.
  • “Edge-to-edge anterior with forward shift on closing” → consider pseudo-Class III / functional shift.

STOP 40 sec (say out loud): “I = MB in groove. II = lower molar distal. II/1 = proclined + ↑OJ. II/2 = retroclined centrals + deep bite. III = lower molar mesial.”

B. Overjet, overbite, crossbite, open bite

B1. Overjet (horizontal)

  • Overjet: horizontal overlap of incisors (upper ahead of lower normally a few mm). Increased in many Class II/1; reverse overjet in many Class III.
  • Large OJ → trauma risk to upper incisors (sports, falls) — soft tissue trauma history in stems.
  • Measure conceptually: edge of upper incisor to labial of lower incisor horizontally.

B2. Overbite (vertical)

  • Overbite: vertical overlap of incisors. Deep bite = excessive cover of lower incisors; may cause palatal trauma, wear, periodontal strain patterns.
  • Open bite: no vertical overlap / vertical gap when teeth in occlusion — anterior open bite classic with digit habits; posterior open bite less common stems.
  • Do not confuse open bite (vertical problem) with reverse overjet (horizontal Class III problem).

B3. Crossbite

  • Crossbite: buccolingual reverse relationship. Anterior crossbite: upper incisor(s) lingual to lower. Posterior crossbite: upper buccal cusps lingual to lower (uni- or bilateral).
  • Always check for functional shift (pseudo): premature contact forces mandible to slide — early correction of the interfering tooth can resolve shift; true skeletal constriction needs expansion/ortho plan.
  • Unilateral posterior crossbite with shift is a classic interceptive target; bilateral may reflect narrow maxilla.

B4. Midline and other quick descriptors

  • Midline discrepancy: dental (crowding, early loss, asymmetric extraction) vs skeletal. Note but do not over-diagnose without records.
  • Scissor bite / Brodie: rare extreme buccal upper vs lower — specialist.
  • Exam language: OJ horizontal · OB vertical · crossbite BL reverse · open bite no vertical overlap.

B5. Exam stem patterns (descriptors)

  • “Upper incisors 8 mm ahead of lower” → increased overjet.
  • “Lower incisors completely covered, bite into palate” → deep overbite.
  • “Anterior teeth do not meet vertically, gap with habits” → anterior open bite pathway.
  • “Upper left posterior teeth bite inside lower, mandible shifts on close” → posterior crossbite + functional shift thinking.

STOP 25 sec: “OJ horizontal · OB vertical · open = no vertical meet · crossbite = BL reverse · always check shift.”

C. Habits (high-level)

C1. Digit / thumb sucking

  • Prolonged intense digit habit → proclined upper incisors, increased OJ, anterior open bite, narrow maxilla / posterior crossbite risk, possibly lip incompetence.
  • Many children stop by school age; intervene when habit persists into mixed dentition with malocclusion developing, or intensity/duration high. Counseling, reminders, reward charts first; habit-breaking appliance when indicated and child motivated.
  • Stopping the habit often allows partial spontaneous improvement in open bite if skeletal pattern allows — timing matters.

C2. Tongue thrust and mouth breathing

  • Tongue thrust associated with anterior open bite and some lisp patterns; treat underlying habit/airway/ortho plan rather than only scolding the tongue.
  • Mouth breathing / adenoid facies pattern: long face, open-mouth posture, open-bite tendency, gummy smile patterns — if obstruction suspected → ENT evaluation (adenoids/tonsils) as part of interdisciplinary care.
  • Exam: do not promise that “only tongue exercises” fix skeletal open bite in adults.

C3. Other habits

  • Bruxism: attrition, muscle pain, fractured restorations — counsel; night guard in permanent dentition when indicated; in primary, often monitor + address stress/airway if relevant.
  • Nail biting / pen chewing: local trauma, not full malocclusion usually.
  • Pacifier: similar dental effects to digit if prolonged; counsel weaning timelines.

C4. Exam stem patterns (habits)

  • “8-year-old, intense thumb, anterior open bite, proclined uppers” → habit-related malocclusion → counsel ± habit appliance pathway.
  • “Long face, open mouth, snoring, open bite tendency” → airway/ENT consideration + orthodontics context.
  • “Wear facets primary molars, otherwise healthy” → bruxism common; usually observation/counseling high-level.

STOP 30 sec: “Digit → open bite + ↑OJ + narrow maxilla risk. Tongue/airway → open bite patterns. Habit stop early helps. II/1≠II/2 still from section A.”

D. Space maintainers (decision tree gold)

D1. Why space is lost

  • Premature loss of primary teeth (especially primary second molars = E) allows mesial drift/tipping of permanent first molars and loss of leeway/E-space — crowding or impaction of premolars later.
  • Goal of space maintainer: hold the space until the successor erupts enough, when the case and cooperation allow appliance use.
  • Not every early loss needs an appliance: if successor is about to erupt (root formation adequate, near eruption), space already collapsed needing comprehensive ortho, or medical/compliance barriers.

D2. Band and loop

  • Band & loop: unilateral space maintainer. Classic after early loss of a primary molar when the permanent first molar (6) is already erupted and can be banded (or band on primary second molar depending on which tooth was lost — know the concept: band on abutment, loop holds space for missing tooth).
  • Most tested: missing primary first or second molar with 6 present → band&loop (unilateral).
  • Needs monitoring: cement washout, soft tissue impingement, successor eruption path.

D3. Distal shoe

  • Distal shoe: used when the primary second molar (E) is lost before the permanent first molar erupts — the appliance has an intra-alveolar extension that guides the unerupted 6 into place and holds E space.
  • Exam hinge: E gone + 6 unerupted → distal shoe (concept). 6 already erupted → band&loop (not distal shoe).
  • Contraindications/cautions: certain medical conditions (blood dyscrasias, immunosuppression, poor OH, uncooperative), endocarditis risk discussions historically — know limits exist; refer when complex.
  • After 6 erupts, distal shoe often converted to band&loop or other maintainer until premolar erupts.

D4. Nance and LLHA (bilateral)

  • Nance appliance: maxillary bilateral space maintainer — molar bands + acrylic button on palatal rugae. Holds bilateral maxillary space / prevents mesial drift of upper molars.
  • Lower lingual holding arch (LLHA): mandibular bilateral — molar bands + lingual wire contacting permanent incisors. Ideally place when permanent incisors have erupted so wire can rest correctly; holds leeway space / prevents molar mesial drift.
  • Bilateral multiple losses → these rather than multiple random loops without plan.

D5. Diastema + frenum sequence

  • Midline diastema with thick high labial frenum: classic teaching sequence often orthodontic space closure first, then frenectomy so scar tissue does not hold the space open / reopen diastema.
  • Ugly-duckling stage (mixed dentition, flared laterals with canine pressure) often self-corrects — do not frenectomy every pediatric diastema early without diagnosis.

D6. Decision tree (say it)

  • Unilateral loss, 6 erupted → band & loop.
  • Primary E lost, 6 unerupted → distal shoe (if appropriate candidate).
  • Bilateral maxillary → consider Nance.
  • Bilateral mandibular (permanent incisors up) → LLHA.
  • Successor erupting soon / space gone + full ortho planned → may skip appliance.
  • Diastema + frenum after permanent canines considered → often ortho close then frenectomy.

D7. Exam stem patterns (space)

  • “Early loss primary lower E, permanent 6 erupted, unilateral” → band & loop.
  • “Primary upper E extracted, 6 not erupted yet” → distal shoe concept.
  • “Bilateral premature loss upper primary molars” → Nance-type thinking.
  • “Bilateral lower primary molar loss, permanent incisors present” → LLHA.
  • “Midline diastema, thick frenum, ortho planned” → close space then frenectomy often.

STOP 40 sec: “Band&loop = unilateral, 6 up. Distal shoe = E gone, 6 not up. Nance = upper bilateral. LLHA = lower bilateral. Frenum = ortho close then cut often.”

E. Expansion appliances

E1. Why expand

  • Maxillary constriction / posterior crossbite / arch length needs in growing patients. Skeletal midpalatal suture expansion works best before suture maturation (children / early adolescents). Adults often need surgical assistance for true skeletal expansion (SARPE concepts).

E2. Rapid palatal expanders

  • Hyrax: tooth-borne jackscrew RPE — common fixed expander. Parents turn screw per schedule; midline diastema may appear temporarily as suture opens.
  • Haas: RPE with acrylic palatal coverage (tissue-tooth borne) — also expansion of constricted maxilla; banks list Haas and Hyrax as expansion appliances.
  • Retention after expansion needed so relapse does not collapse the gain immediately.

E3. Slower / milder options

  • Quad helix / W-arch: fixed wire expanders, slower dental/alveolar expansion, useful in milder cases or mixed dentition selected cases.
  • Removable expanders / plates with screws: compliance-dependent.
  • Exam: match appliance family to maxillary constriction / crossbite in a child — Haas/Hyrax/quad helix appear as correct options depending on stem.

E4. Adult limits

  • Adult skeletal expansion often fails with tooth-only tipping if suture fused — surgical assist (SARPE) or other orthosurgical plans. Pure dental tipping has periodontal limits.

E5. Exam stem patterns (expansion)

  • “Child, bilateral posterior crossbite, narrow maxilla” → expansion (Hyrax/Haas/quad helix family).
  • “Haas appliance used for?” → maxillary expansion / constriction correction.
  • “Adult wants skeletal expansion only with removable plate” → limited; surgery may be needed for true skeletal change.

STOP 25 sec: “Kids expand suture. Hyrax/Haas = RPE. Quad helix milder/slower. Adults often need surgery for skeletal expand.”

F. Interceptive notes (exam depth only)

F1. What interceptive means on this exam

  • Early treatment of selected problems to reduce severity — not “braces for every 7-year-old.” Focus on crossbites with shift, habits, space maintenance, ectopic canines surveillance, some Class II growth modification while growing.

F2. Serial / guided extraction

  • Specialist-planned sequence for severe crowding in carefully selected cases — not random extraction of healthy premolars without space analysis and growth assessment. Exam: if stem says “serial extraction,” think planned specialty pathway, not freestyle XO.

F3. Functional appliances and growth

  • Examples: twin block, other functional appliances for growing Class II — aim to modify posture/growth expression while patient is growing. Timing = growth; little magic after growth complete for skeletal change.
  • Retention after any active ortho: fixed (bonded wire) or removable retainers. Relapse risks: lower incisor crowding, rotated teeth, midline diastema — retain long enough; sometimes indefinite nights.

F4. Ectopic canines

  • Maxillary canines ectopic/impacted common specialty problem. Early screening OPG roughly ~10–12 years if not palpable buccally. Extracting primary canine sometimes improves path of permanent canine if criteria met (early, favorable position) — specialist judgment.
  • Do not ignore missing canine bulge + family history of impaction.

F5. Exam stem patterns (interceptive)

  • “Growing Class II, compliant, wants growth modification” → functional appliance pathway concept.
  • “After braces, lower incisors crowd again” → retention failure / need retention.
  • “Age 11, canine not palpable, OPG shows ectopic path” → interceptive extraction of primary canine consideration ± refer.

STOP 25 sec: “Space tree first. Expand kids. Interceptive = selected early fixes. Growth appliances while growing. Retain or relapse. Canine OPG ~10–12.”

G. Pedo pulp therapy

G1. Decision framework

  • Same big questions as adult pulp care: vital or not? reversible or irreversible/necrosis? restorable tooth? successor timing? medical/behavior limits?
  • Primary teeth differ: resorbing roots, furcation radiolucency more ominous, materials must resorb if in canals for pulpectomy, full coverage often SSC after pulp therapy.

G2. Indirect pulp therapy (IPT)

  • IPT: deep caries approaching pulp, no irreversible pulpitis signs (no lingering spontaneous night pain classic), no necrosis signs, restorable tooth. Remove infected dentin; leave deepest firm affected dentin to avoid exposure; place biocompatible base/liner; excellent seal.
  • Preferred when criteria met — avoids pulp exposure. Seal is half the therapy (links Day 1/6 thinking).

G3. Direct pulp cap (limited in primary)

  • Small clean mechanical/traumatic exposure, vital pulp, controllable hemorrhage, dam — more accepted in permanent teeth with MTA/bioceramic.
  • In primary teeth, carious exposure often leads to pulpotomy rather than simple direct cap in many protocols — exam may still mention cap for pinpoint mechanical exposure in ideal cases, but know pulpotomy is the workhorse after carious primary exposure.

G4. Pulpotomy (primary)

  • Indications pattern: carious (or traumatic) exposure, coronal pulp inflamed but radicular pulp healthy, restorable tooth, no fistula, no excessive mobility, no furcation/apical pathosis of necrosis pattern, controllable hemorrhage after coronal pulp removal.
  • Technique concept: remove coronal pulp, place medicament (modern MTA / bioceramic; formocresol historical), restore — preferably SSC for seal and durability.
  • If hemorrhage from canal orifices cannot be controlled → radicular pulp not healthy → pulpectomy or extraction path.

G5. Pulpectomy (primary)

  • Indications: irreversible pulpitis or necrosis in a restorable primary tooth without hopeless root resorption / non-restorable crown / strategic extraction plan.
  • Clean canals, fill with resorbable paste suited to primary roots (ZOE-type or iodoform pastes per protocol — concept: must resorb with root). Permanent GP not for primary roots that need to exfoliate.
  • Restore with SSC often. Follow for infection, mobility, successor eruption.

G6. Extraction and immature permanent

  • Extract when non-restorable, advanced pathologic root resorption, severe infection with poor prognosis, ortho plan needs space, or medical/behavior precludes pulp therapy success — then space maintenance decision tree (Section D).
  • Immature permanent: reuse Day 6 — apexogenesis (vital, keep root growing), partial pulpotomy, regenerative approaches, MTA apexification barrier if necrotic immature. Do not treat immature permanent like primary formocresol pulpotomy blindly without diagnosis.

G7. Exam stem patterns (pedo pulp)

  • “Deep caries primary molar, brief cold only, no spontaneous pain” → IPT pathway possible.
  • “Carious exposure primary molar, bleeding controlled at orifices, roots healthy radiograph” → pulpotomy + SSC.
  • “Primary molar fistula, radiolucency furcation, restorable crown” → pulpectomy or extract depending on severity/restorability — necrosis path not IPT.
  • “Uncontrollable bleed from canals after coronal pulp removal” → not simple pulpotomy success → pulpectomy/XO.
  • “After primary pulpotomy best final restoration often” → SSC.

STOP 40 sec: “IPT = deep, no irreversible signs. Pulpotomy = coronal pulp out, roots OK + SSC. Pulpectomy = primary root canal with resorbable fill. Extract if hopeless. Immature permanent = Day 6 rules.”

H. SSC & pedo restorative

H1. Stainless steel crown indications

  • SSC indications (high yield): extensive multi-surface caries on primary molars; after pulp therapy (pulpotomy/pulpectomy); severe developmental defects (MIH, amelogenesis imperfecta patterns); fractured primary molars needing full coverage; high-caries-risk large lesions where multi-surface composite will fail; sometimes interim on permanent molars (e.g., young MIH) until definitive cast/ceramic later.
  • SSC wins longevity and seal over heroic multi-surface composite in wet, high-risk primary molars — exam favors SSC when stem screams “extensive primary molar after pulpotomy.”

H2. Prep and cement concepts

  • Occlusal reduction + circumferential reduction (often slight taper), select size, crimp margins for snap fit, cement (often GIC). Open margin / loose crown = failure.
  • Do not over-reduce into pulp unnecessarily; do not leave ledge that prevents seating.

H3. Other pedo restorative

  • Strip crowns / composite for primary anteriors when indicated (esthetics + behavior). Isolation matters.
  • Sealants on deep pits/fissures of permanent molars (and selected primary) in risk kids — isolation critical for retention.
  • SDF (silver diamine fluoride): arrests caries, blackens lesion permanently — always consent (esthetics). Excellent for uncooperative / special needs / multiple lesions as bridge to definitive SSC when possible. Not a secret bleach-able cosmetic fix.
  • ART / GIC in limited cooperation field settings — still know GIC chemical bond + fluoride release.

H4. Exam stem patterns (SSC / resto)

  • “Primary second molar after pulpotomy” → SSC restoration of choice often.
  • “Multi-surface caries primary molar, high risk” → SSC.
  • “SDF side effect parents must know” → permanent blackening — consent.
  • “Deep pits first permanent molar, high risk, dry isolation OK” → sealant.

STOP 25 sec: “SSC = extensive primary molar or post pulp. SDF blackens — consent. Sealants need isolation. Composite not always hero in wet high-risk primary molars.”

I. Fluoride & prevention

I1. Systemic supplements — classic trap

  • Systemic fluoride supplements: none if community water is optimally fluoridated and the child has adequate intake and is low risk — classic exam answer. Do not auto-prescribe tablets for every child.
  • Only consider supplements if water fluoride is deficient and caries risk justifies, with dose by age tables (know principle of age-based dosing; exact mg tables vary by guideline year — concept > memorizing outdated numbers wrongly).
  • Excess systemic F during enamel formation → fluorosis risk. Topical benefits still key after eruption.

I2. Topical fluoride

  • Toothpaste: smear (grain of rice) for infants/toddlers; pea-size once child can spit (age teaching ~3 years varies by guideline — know smear vs pea concept). Supervise to minimize swallow.
  • Professional varnish: high-risk and routine prevention visits — safe topical even when systemic supplement is not indicated.
  • High-fluoride toothpaste (e.g., 5000 ppm concepts) for high-risk older children/adolescents with supervision.
  • APF gels/foams historically — varnish often preferred in modern pedo for ease and reduced swallow.

I3. Toxicity awareness (high-level)

  • Acute fluoride toxicity from massive ingestion: GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain), in severe cases hypocalcemia, cardiac, neuro — emergency care. Prevention: supervise toothpaste, childproof supplements, professional products measured.
  • Probable toxic dose teaching exists in pedo texts — exam may ask first aid: reduce absorption concepts, milk/calcium containing fluids sometimes taught while seeking emergency care — know it is serious, not “sleep it off.”
  • Do not panic over normal pea-size brushing; panic over eating the tube + supplements together.

I4. Diet and ECC

  • Sugar frequency > total amount (Stephan curve — Day 1 link). Night bottles / sippy cups with sweet liquids → early childhood caries pattern (upper anteriors classically, lower laterals often spared by tongue).
  • Counsel: no sweet bottles in bed; water at night after eruption; wean bottles; clean gums/teeth.

I5. Exam stem patterns (fluoride / prevention)

  • “Optimal water fluoride, low-risk child — systemic supplement?” → none.
  • “High-risk child, non-fluoridated well water” → consider supplement per age + topical intensifies.
  • “Parents fear all fluoride — still recommend?” → topical toothpaste/varnish benefits with proper dosing still core prevention.
  • “Infant toothpaste amount” → smear / rice-grain, not adult strip.
  • “Upper front ECC, bottle in bed” → diet habit counseling + definitive care plan.

STOP 35 sec: “Optimal water → no systemic F supplement. Topical still yes. Smear vs pea. SDF/varnish for risk. Sugar frequency. ECC = sweet bottle pattern. Acute F OD = emergency.”

J. Behavior & sedation (high-level)

J1. Tell-Show-Do (first-line)

  • Tell-Show-Do: explain in age-appropriate words → show the instrument/procedure on finger or model → do the procedure. Cornerstone basic behavior guidance MCQ. First-line before jumping to advanced techniques.
  • Works with voice control, positive reinforcement (“catch them being good”), distraction, modeling, parent presence policies (age and clinic dependent).

J2. Frankl scale

  • Cooperation documentation: definitely negative (--) · negative (-) · positive (+) · definitely positive (++). Used in charts/research — exam may ask what scale grades pediatric behavior.

J3. Protective stabilization

  • Last-resort selected cases with informed consent, not punitive, not for clinician convenience alone, document indication and duration. Alternatives first. Local law and ethics apply.

J4. Nitrous oxide (high-level)

  • Mild–moderate anxiety, gagging reduction, if nasal hood tolerated. Titrate oxygen; recovery with oxygen. Not general anesthesia.
  • Cautions/contraindications patterns: severe COPD / some respiratory compromise, pregnancy (especially first trimester classic teaching), severe nasal obstruction / upper respiratory infection preventing nose breathing, some psychiatric issues, intoxication, inability to understand/tolerate hood, otitis media acute issues in some lists, bleomycin history rare specialty note.
  • Exam: nitrous is conscious sedation adjunct, not a substitute for local anesthesia when soft tissue pain expected.

J5. Moderate/deep sedation and GA

  • Indications pattern: extensive treatment needs, special healthcare needs, failed basic guidance, very young with urgent disease burden, extreme anxiety — medical assessment, NPO rules, trained team and facility, monitoring, emergency readiness.
  • Exam level: know indications exist and safety systems matter; do not freestyle deep sedation in unprepared offices.

J6. Exam stem patterns (behavior)

  • “First-line basic behavior guidance technique” → Tell-Show-Do.
  • “Document cooperation scale -- to ++” → Frankl.
  • “Mild anxiety, hood OK, needs Class I restorations” → nitrous consideration + LA.
  • “Pregnancy first trimester elective nitrous?” → caution/avoid classic teaching.
  • “Uncooperative special needs, full mouth caries, failed chairside” → GA/sedation pathway with proper setting.

K. Natal/neonatal, Pierre Robin, eruption

K1. Natal vs neonatal teeth

  • Natal teeth: present at birth.
  • Neonatal teeth: erupt within the first 30 days of life.
  • Most often mandibular primary central incisors — may be true primary teeth (majority) or supernumerary. Radiograph if management unclear.
  • Manage if highly mobile (aspiration risk), causing Riga-Fede ulcer on tongue, or severe breastfeeding trauma — smooth sharp edges or extract if indicated. If stable and not causing problems → observe.
  • Exam hinge: natal = birth; neonatal ≤30 days — definitions free points.

K2. Pierre Robin sequence

  • Triad: micrognathia + glossoptosis + cleft palate (U-shaped cleft classic). Neonatal airway is the priority — prone positioning, specialty airway management, feeding support.
  • Dental team later manages cleft/ortho/feeding appliances as part of multidisciplinary care. Exam wants the triad automatic.

K3. Primary eruption rough order

  • Lower primary centrals first (~6 months average) → upper centrals → laterals → first molars → canines → second molars. Primary dentition complete ~2.5–3 years.
  • Variation normal; delayed eruption extreme + other anomalies → investigate systemic/syndromic causes.

K4. Permanent landmarks

  • Permanent first molars and lower centrals ~6 years. Premolars and canines later mixed dentition. Second molars ~12 years. Third molars teens+.
  • Ugly-duckling stage: mid-mixed, upper laterals flared with canine crowns pressuring roots — often self-corrects as canines erupt. Do not overtreat every temporary diastema.
  • Leeway space / E-space: difference between primary molars+canine vs permanent successors — important for crowding management (specialty).

K5. Exam stem patterns

  • “Tooth present at birth” → natal.
  • “Tooth erupts day 20 of life” → neonatal.
  • “Newborn, small mandible, tongue back, cleft palate, breathing issues” → Pierre Robin triad + airway first.
  • “Age 8, flared upper laterals, diastema, canines unerupted” → ugly-duckling often observe.

STOP 30 sec: “Natal = birth. Neonatal ≤30 d. Pierre Robin = micrognathia + glossoptosis + cleft. Ugly-duckling often self-corrects. 6s at ~6 years.”

L. Trauma: primary vs permanent

L1. Primary trauma principles

  • Protect the permanent successor bud. Avoid aggressive rigid long-term splinting that harms developing teeth. Many luxation/intrusion cases in primary: observe if no airway risk and position acceptable; extract if tooth threatens successor, is non-restorable, or infection path requires.
  • Warn parents: possible permanent enamel hypoplasia, dilaceration, eruption path problems later — document counseling.
  • Intruded primary tooth: soft tissue, radiograph to assess permanent bud relationship — observation vs extraction based on direction/severity teaching.

L2. Primary avulsion — classic rule

  • Primary avulsed tooth: generally do NOT reimplant — risk of damage to permanent successor. Classic exam teaching. (Permanent avulsion is the opposite urgency.)

L3. Permanent trauma — IADT-type logic (high-level)

  • Concussion: sore, no mobility increase/displacement — monitor vitality.
  • Subluxation: mobility↑, no displacement — short flexible splint sometimes; follow vitality.
  • Luxations (extrusive, lateral, intrusive): reposition as indicated, flexible splint, high endo risk especially mature apex; intrusion severity + apex maturity guide spontaneous re-eruption vs ortho/surgical reposition.
  • Avulsion permanent: reimplant ASAP if indicated; storage milk, HBSS, saliva — not dry tissue; water poor. Extra-oral dry time critical. Flexible splint often ~2 weeks uncomplicated avulsion (root/alveolar fracture changes duration). Tetanus, antibiotics when indicated. Mature closed apex often RCT within ~7–10 days teaching patterns; immature apex may allow longer pulp survival / revasc attempts.
  • Root fracture: reposition + longer flexible splint classically; alveolar fracture: segment stabilization longer.

L4. Exam stem patterns (trauma)

  • “3-year-old avulsed primary central” → do not reimplant (usual teaching).
  • “12-year-old avulsed permanent central, 20 min in milk” → reimplant, flexible splint ~2 wk pattern, endo plan by apex maturity.
  • “Primary intrusion, permanent bud at risk on radiograph” → careful decision; may extract primary; counsel permanent sequelae.
  • “Permanent mature tooth avulsed 3 hours dry” → poor prognosis discussion; still urgent assessment; replacement resorption risk high.

STOP 35 sec: “Primary avulsion → usually do not reimplant. Permanent avulsion → milk/HBSS/saliva + time + flexible ~2 wk. Protect successor in primary trauma. Mature apex avulsion → early endo plan.”
