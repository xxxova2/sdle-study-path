A2. Stem → category in under 10 seconds

B. Crown prep principles (must know cold)

B1. Reduction — bulk without killing the pulp

  • Reduction must give the restorative material enough bulk for strength and esthetics without over‑reducing into pulp or weakening remaining tooth.
  • Under‑reduction → lab/clinic forces overcontoured crown (plaque, gingivitis, poor esthetics) or thin weak ceramic/metal that fractures or shows gray.
  • Over‑reduction → pulp insult, thermal sensitivity, need for endo, short weak walls if height is also lost, harder provisional retention.
  • Material dictates minimum thickness: metal can be thinner than many ceramics; zirconia and glass ceramics need enough circumferential and occlusal clearance — know the spirit (ceramic needs bulk) more than every brand number.
  • Functional cusp needs extra thought: thin porcelain on the working cusp is a classic fracture setup.

B2. Taper / TOC (total occlusal convergence)

  • Taper / TOC: walls must converge slightly toward the occlusal so the restoration can seat fully without binding.
  • Ideal teaching range often ~6–10° per wall (~10–20° total occlusal convergence) — not knife‑thin parallel walls that bind on try‑in, not a conical stump that slides off.
  • Excessive taper destroys retention. Short + fat taper = crown that cement cannot save forever.
  • Parallel walls sound nice in theory but clinically hard to seat and easy to leave undercuts relative to one path — slight taper is intentional.
  • When walls are already short, every extra degree of taper hurts more — gain height (ferrule strategies) rather than “taper more so it seats easy.”

B3. Path of insertion and undercuts

  • Path of insertion = single direction the crown seats. All axial surfaces must be free of undercuts relative to that path.
  • Undercuts trap the casting/milling seating or leave cement voids and open margins / rocking.
  • Survey mentally (or with a surveyor on diagnostic cast for FPD abutments): add fillings/cores to block out minor undercuts only when tooth structure allows; otherwise re‑prep the undercut wall carefully.
  • FPD abutments must share a common path — tilted abutments may need ortho, modified prep, or different design rather than heroic over‑taper of both teeth.

B4. Functional cusp bevel, line angles, coverage choice

  • Functional cusp bevel (classic: buccal of lower, palatal of upper) adds reduction where load hits so metal/ceramic has bulk on the working cusp.
  • Without FCB, the lab thins the material or the crown stays high / overcontoured at the cusp tip — fracture and occlusal chaos risk.
  • Rounded line angles reduce stress concentration in ceramic and help the restoration seat; sharp internal angles are crack starters in porcelain.
  • Full coverage removes more tooth and raises pulp risk versus partial coverage when partial is biomechanically enough (onlay, 3/4, etc.). Banks still love “undermined cusps → coverage,” which you met on Day 1.
  • Axial walls provide the main frictional retention surface area. Height and area matter more than hoping cement is magic.

B5. Height, surface area, and short clinical crowns

  • Short clinical crown = low axial wall height = low retention and resistance.
  • Options banks test: subgingival finish only when justified, crown lengthening, orthodontic extrusion, boxes/grooves/pin features, adhesive cementation with appropriate material, and honest “is this tooth restorable?”
  • Do not solve short crown only by making a steeper taper “so the temp stays on” — that worsens the final.
  • Occlusal reduction that steals height without gaining material space thoughtfully can worsen short‑wall problems — plan reduction against the opposing, not randomly.

B6. Material‑linked reduction thinking (exam spirit)

  • Full metal crowns need the least occlusal/axial bulk of common full coverage options — still need even reduction and a clear finish line.
  • PFM needs metal + opaque + porcelain stack room on facial/occlusal where porcelain is planned — under‑reduce and you get opaque show‑through or fracture.
  • Monolithic zirconia often needs less bulk than layered glass ceramic but still fails if paper‑thin at margins or cusps.
  • Glass ceramics (e.g., lithium disilicate teaching family) need reliable circumferential thickness — knife edges and “minimal prep veneer thinking” on a full crown stem is a trap.
  • Anterior esthetic preps: uniform facial reduction, correct incisal edge position, and not over‑thinning cervical enamel into a weak J‑margin.
  • Always cool the bur with water; heat is a silent pulp killer during aggressive reduction — stems may mention post‑prep sensitivity after dry cutting.

B7. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (prep)

  • “Crown keeps coming off; prep looks conical” → excessive TOC / inadequate retention form.
  • “Porcelain fractured on buccal lower molar cusp” → under‑reduction of functional cusp / missing bevel / heavy load / bruxism.
  • “Crown will not seat, binds on try‑in” → undercut relative to path, debris, or heavy proximal contact of the crown itself.
  • “Overcontoured bulky crown, red gingiva” → often under‑reduction of tooth + lab overbuilding.
  • “Vital tooth, huge prep, lingering thermal pain later” → pulp insult from aggressive reduction/heat — still check occlusion and seal first in the stem.
  • “Two abutments for bridge, different tilts” → common path problem — not freehand “more taper both until it drops.”
  • “Gray show‑through at facial PFM” → inadequate reduction for ceramic/opaque stack or metal show at thin margin.
  • “Casting seats on die but not in mouth” → soft tissue, residual temporary cement, or proximal of adjacent teeth tighter clinically than on model.

STOP 60 sec: “Reduce for bulk, not for pulp murder. TOC slight for seat — not a cone. Path free of undercuts. Functional cusp bevel. Round internals for ceramic. Short walls = low retention. Material dictates how much room you must create.”

C. Finish lines — chamfer, shoulder, knife

C1. Chamfer

  • Chamfer: smooth, well‑defined finish line classic for many metal and many modern zirconia / some all‑ceramic designs when reduction is adequate.
  • Easy to cut with a torpedo/chamfer bur; readable on die and scan if continuous and clear.
  • Too deep a chamfer that becomes a reverse lip or “J‑margin” is a prep error — the lip flexes, traps cement, and confuses the margin.
  • Light chamfer with inadequate depth for ceramic thickness is a knife edge in disguise — material will be thin at the margin.

C2. Shoulder and rounded shoulder

  • Shoulder / rounded shoulder: provides bulk at the margin for brittle ceramics (classic PFM facial porcelain margin concepts and many all‑ceramic teachings).
  • Squared sharp internal angles can concentrate stress in ceramic — rounded internal line preferred in modern teaching.
  • Shoulder needs enough axial reduction so you are not left with a thin radial lip of enamel at the edge.
  • PFM often historically mixed: metal collar options vs porcelain margin on facial for esthetics — exam cares that porcelain needs room.

C3. Knife / feather edge

  • Knife / feather edge: minimal tooth removal but creates a thin weak margin, hard to read on die, easy to overcontour the restoration to hide the indistinct finish line.
  • Limited modern default use for bulk ceramics. Not the answer when the stem wants all‑ceramic strength at the margin.
  • Sometimes discussed for periodontally involved teeth or metal margins where conservation is extreme — know it is not free bulk for porcelain.
  • CLASSIC: all‑ceramic needs adequate circumferential reduction + chamfer or rounded shoulder — not a knife edge “to save tooth” if ceramic will be thin and fracture.

C4. Placement rules that win points

  • Finish line must be on sound tooth structure, continuous 360°, visible for impression or scan, and compatible with material thickness.
  • Supragingival margins are easiest to isolate, impress, clean cement, and maintain — use when esthetics and caries allow.
  • Equigingival / slightly subgingival when needed for retention, old restoration removal, caries, or esthetics — still respect soft tissue.
  • Deep subgingival margins → harder isolation, more biologic width risk, poorer impressions, more residual cement risk — only when needed.
  • Margin on restorative material (old core) is weaker logic than margin on sound tooth when possible; rebuild and re‑prep to sound structure.

C5. Margin errors that destroy lab work

  • J‑margin / lip: bur creates a reverse bevel or unsupported enamel lip that flexes and confuses the true finish line — re‑prep to a clean chamfer/shoulder without the lip.
  • Beveled metal margins exist in classic gold techniques (beveled shoulder) for burnishability — do not confuse gold bevel rules with all‑ceramic knife edges.
  • Rough, stepped, or incomplete finish lines → poor adaptation, open margins, and “which line is real?” on the die.
  • Finish line must match the planned restoration: do not cut a knife edge and order a bulky glass ceramic and hope.
  • After old crown removal, remove residual cement and caries, then re‑establish a fresh continuous finish line on sound tooth — do not just “touch up” a rotten ragged edge.

C6. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (finish lines)

  • “All‑ceramic crown, knife edge prep” → inadequate bulk at margin — redesign to chamfer/rounded shoulder with enough reduction.
  • “PFM facial, want porcelain to the edge” → need shoulder‑type room for ceramic, not a metal‑only feather story.
  • “Margin disappears on die, overcontoured crown” → indistinct knife or J‑margin / poor prep definition.
  • “Chronic red tissue, margin 2+ mm sub‑G near bone” → think biologic width violation (section H), not only “patient does not floss.”
  • “Gold inlay/onlay bevel mentioned” → classic metal margin philosophy, not a free knife edge for porcelain crowns.
  • “Old crown off, dark soft dentin at previous margin” → excavate to sound tooth and place new finish line; do not cement on soft caries.

STOP 45 sec: “Chamfer = metal/many zirconia. Shoulder/rounded = ceramic bulk. Knife = thin weak margin. Sound continuous visible finish line. Deep sub‑G only when needed. No J‑lip. Match margin to material.”

D. Retention, resistance, ferrule, posts

D1. Retention vs resistance (definitions that banks test)

  • Retention form = resists forces along the path of withdrawal (crown pulls off axially / sticky candy path).
  • Resistance form = resists tipping and lateral dislodging forces (chewing, parafunction, lateral loads).
  • Both depend on geometry of remaining tooth more than brand of cement. Cement helps; geometry rules.
  • Long parallel‑ish walls help retention. Boxes, grooves, and pin ledges help resistance when walls are short or taper is large.

D2. How to improve retention and resistance

  • Increase wall height (crown lengthening or ortho extrusion for ferrule / clinical crown).
  • Reduce excessive taper (re‑prep conservatively if tooth allows).
  • Add auxiliary features: proximal boxes, vertical grooves, pins in selected cases — without shredding remaining dentin.
  • Adhesive cementation with appropriate ceramic/metal primer systems when indicated and isolatable — not a license for a 40° cone.
  • Opposing occlusion and parafunction control — heavy lateral bruxism loosens and breaks crowns that were “barely retentive.”
  • Core rebuild to recreate wall height and form when tooth is hollowed by caries/endo access — still need ferrule of tooth, not only core material.

D3. Ferrule — the non‑negotiable concept

  • Ferrule: circumferential band of sound tooth structure above the finish line / encircling the core, classic teaching ≈1.5–2 mm height of reasonably parallel walls.
  • Ferrule grips the tooth under the crown like a metal ring on a stick — protects the root and post‑core complex from fracture under load.
  • No ferrule + post only is a weak story. The post does not replace ferrule. Gain ferrule surgically (crown lengthening) or orthodontically (extrusion), or rethink restorability / extraction‑implant / RPD pathways.
  • Ferrule of core buildup material alone without tooth walls is not the classic ferrule effect banks want.
  • Partial ferrule (e.g., remaining buccal and lingual) is better than none but full circumferential is the teaching ideal.

D4. Posts — only when the core needs them

  • Posts: only when remaining tooth structure cannot retain a core — not “every RCT tooth gets a post.”
  • Prefer conservation of dentin. A post occupies space that was root dentin; oversized posts weaken roots.
  • Fiber posts with adhesive cores are common when indicated (modulus closer to dentin teaching story; more retrievable sometimes). Cast posts/cores in selected cases with little remaining structure and appropriate ferrule plan.
  • Post length concepts: adequate length for retention of the post/core, often discussed relative to root length, without violating the apical seal (leave adequate apical gutta‑percha, classic teaching often ~4–5 mm apical seal ideas) and without thinning walls dangerously.
  • Post diameter: enough for strength of the post, not so wide that root walls become eggshell.
  • Ferrule first in planning; post second. Exam loves the order.

D5. RCT tooth for crown — restorability algorithm

  • Decide restorability first: remaining walls, ferrule possible?, perio status, crown‑root ratio, occlusion/parafunction, strategic value of the tooth.
  • Crown of an unrestorable root is delayed failure — do not “save” with a pretty PFM and hope.
  • Endodontically treated posterior teeth often need cuspal coverage when large MOD loss — structural, not only “because RCT.”
  • Anterior RCT teeth with minimal structure loss may not need posts if a core or even bonded restoration retains adequately — indication‑driven.
  • If crown lengthening would invade furcation or leave hopeless perio, extraction may be kinder than heroic post‑core‑crown.

D6. Core materials and timing (high yield add‑ons)

  • Core rebuild materials (amalgam, composite, RMGI cores) must leave you enough sound tooth for ferrule after prep — building a huge core and prepping it all away leaves no ferrule.
  • Bonded composite cores need isolation and no eugenol contamination when resin chemistry is used — same Day‑1 rule.
  • If remaining walls after caries removal are paper thin and unsupported, remove weak enamel/dentin and plan coverage + ferrule strategy rather than glue‑and‑pray.
  • Pin‑retained cores are older teaching still in some banks: pins can crack dentin if overused; prefer adhesive cores and remaining tooth walls when possible.
  • Never place a post in a vital tooth for “retention of a crown” — posts are for endodontically treated teeth when the core cannot retain otherwise.

D7. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (retention / ferrule / posts)

  • “Crown dislodges repeatedly on short tapered prep” → improve retention/resistance form / ferrule — not endless temporary cement forever.
  • “Post placed, no ferrule, root fractures later” → missing ferrule / weak root / over‑post.
  • “RCT molar, large remaining walls, core holds — need post?” → often no if core retention is adequate.
  • “1 mm tooth above finish line all around” → inadequate ferrule — lengthen or extrude or rethink.
  • “Cast post too wide on thin root” → root fracture risk.
  • “Vital premolar, dentist wants post for crown retention” → wrong — no post in vital teeth for that reason; improve prep or coverage design.
  • “Apical GP removed entirely for long post” → violated apical seal — failure/leak risk.
  • “Crown lengthening would enter furcation on hopeless perio molar” → extraction / other plan, not endless lengthening.

STOP 60 sec: “Retention = pull‑off path. Resistance = tip/lateral. Ferrule 1.5–2 mm sound tooth. Post only if core needs it. Post ≠ ferrule. No post in vital teeth for crown retention. Restorability before heroics.”

E. Cementation concepts (RMGI, resin, zinc phosphate)

E1. Zinc phosphate

  • Zinc phosphate: classic luting cement, high compressive strength historically, no chemical bond to tooth — mechanical retention and good fit still king.
  • Technique‑sensitive mixing (powder‑liquid on cool glass historically), can be low film thickness when mixed well.
  • Acidic when fresh — pulp protection thinking on vital teeth in old teaching; still appears in comparison tables.
  • Use story on exam: conventional metal/PFM with adequate retention form when modern adhesives are not the hinge of the stem.

E2. RMGI cement

  • RMGI cement: resin‑modified glass ionomer family for luting — fluoride release concepts, often more moisture tolerant than pure resin systems, widely used for metal, PFM, and many zirconia cases when retention form is decent.
  • Follow product indications; some ceramics and some situations prefer dedicated resin cements.
  • Not a free pass for wet blood‑filled fields — isolation still matters.
  • Expansion/history quirks of early products appear less than basic “when to choose RMGI vs resin” logic on modern banks.

E3. Resin cement

  • Resin cement: highest bond potential when isolation + adhesive protocol are correct; preferred for many all‑ceramic / glass ceramic and low‑retention situations with proper primers/etch systems.
  • Glass ceramics often etched (HF in lab or carefully as indicated) + silane; zirconia often MDP‑containing primers (no HF etch of zirconia the way glass etches) — stem may test “primer/etch matches material.”
  • Moisture contamination and saliva on try‑in without re‑cleaning destroy bond.
  • Light‑cure, dual‑cure, self‑cure systems — opaque/metal understructure may need dual/self‑cure chemistry because light will not reach.
  • Excess resin cement subgingival is hard to remove and becomes a plaque/periopath trap — seat, clean carefully, floss, recheck tissue.

E4. Eugenol vs resin — high‑yield trap

  • Eugenol (ZOE temporary cements, some temporary materials) can inhibit free‑radical polymerization of resin.
  • If final cement will be resin (or bonded composite core), prefer non‑eugenol provisional cement, or clean meticulously and follow decontamination protocols the product allows.
  • This is the same eugenol logic as Day 1 under composite — banks recycle it under crowns.
  • IRM / ZOE temp under planned resin cement = classic failure setup if ignored.

E5. Clinical cementation sequence (exam story)

  • Confirm fit on die/model and clinically: margins, contacts, occlusion plan.
  • Try‑in: adjust proximal contacts so the crown can seat fully; heavy contacts can hold the crown high and leave open margins.
  • Clean prep: temporary cement residue, desensitizer products as indicated, oil, blood.
  • Isolate; apply cement per type; seat firmly along path; verify complete seating (explorer, radiograph when indicated for sub‑G).
  • Remove excess at correct stage (some cements clean easier at gel/tacky stage); floss contacts carefully without dragging crown off before set if protocol requires hold.
  • Recheck occlusion after set — cement layer and full seat can change contacts.
  • Open margin after cementation often = incomplete seating, debris, heavy proximal contacts before seat, or poor prep/die — not “magic cement failure only.”

E6. Try‑in, shade, and contamination (extra traps)

  • Try‑in pastes for ceramics help shade check; clean completely before final cement — residue can block bond.
  • Saliva on etched ceramic or primed zirconia after try‑in → re‑clean per protocol (often ultrasonic / alcohol / special cleaners depending on material) then re‑prime as indicated.
  • Do not sandblast glass ceramic the same way you treat zirconia intaglio in every protocol — material‑specific surface treatment is the exam idea.
  • Desensitizers and hemostatic agents can interfere with some bonds — read the stem for “what was applied before cement.”
  • If isolation is impossible for a resin‑dependent restoration, change the plan (different material, better retraction, cord, dam, or conventional retention design) rather than cementing into blood.

E7. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (cement)

  • “Glass ceramic veneer/crown, dry field, etched + silane” → resin cement pathway.
  • “Metal crown, good retention, conventional” → zinc phosphate or RMGI‑type luting often acceptable in stems.
  • “Provisional with ZOE, then resin cement final fails” → eugenol inhibition.
  • “Crown high after cement, open margin on radiograph” → incomplete seating / heavy contact / debris.
  • “Subgingival excess cement, inflamed papilla later” → residual cement / biologic insult — clean at cementation.
  • “Zirconia crown, MDP primer mentioned” → resin or primer‑compatible pathway; not HF etch like glass.
  • “Patient bites cotton while cement sets, crown later high on one cusp” → incomplete seating / patient bite displaced path — verify seat before full set.

STOP 45 sec: “Zinc phosphate = mechanical classic. RMGI = popular luting + F story. Resin = bond + isolation. Eugenol kills resin. Clean, seat fully, clean excess, recheck bite. Contamination after try‑in kills ceramic bonds.”

F. Provisionals — purpose and failures

F1. Why provisionals are not optional toys

  • Purpose: protect pulp/dentin from thermal and bacterial insult; maintain tooth position (no drift/supraeruption of opposing); stabilize occlusion and VDO; shape gingival contour; trial esthetics and phonetics; diagnose problems before the expensive final.
  • Good provisional = sealed margins, proper contacts, correct occlusion, cleansable contours, not irritating gingiva, retained safely.
  • Chairside acrylic/bis‑acryl vs lab processed long‑term provisionals — concept: quality scales with time in mouth and complexity (FPD, vertical changes).

F2. What “good” looks like (checklist stems)

  • Margins closed enough to protect pulp and reduce sensitivity/caries risk.
  • Proximal contacts present so papilla is healthy and food does not pack.
  • Occlusion not high (PDL trauma) and not completely out of contact if that causes supraeruption of opposing over long waits.
  • Emergence profile and contours cleanable — not a plaque ledge.
  • Color acceptable for anterior trials; phonetics checked when anterior teeth change length/position.
  • Retention adequate so patient does not swallow or lose the provisional and teeth migrate.

F3. Provisional failures / problems (always drill)

  • Open margin → sensitivity, caries, gingivitis, bad smell.
  • Open contact → food packing (same ALWAYS‑comes rule as finals).
  • High occlusion → pain, PDL trauma, fracture of provisional, patient avoids chewing.
  • Overcontoured → plaque, gingivitis, blunted papilla, bleeding on probing.
  • Undercontoured / open embrasure mess → food trap, phonetic issues sometimes.
  • Fracture → reinforce, remake, reduce load, check thickness and occlusion.
  • Poor retention → swallow/aspirate risk, tooth movement, emergency visits — recement with appropriate temp cement; fix form if chronic.
  • Long‑term provisional abuse without a final plan = recurrent caries and lost tooth position — provisional is a bridge to a final, not a lifestyle.

F4. Materials and eugenol again

  • Bis‑acryl and acrylic each have shrink/heat/handling quirks — exam cares more about function checklist than brand names.
  • Temporary cement choice: eugenol soothes some vital teeth historically but conflicts with planned resin finals — non‑eugenol when bonding is planned.
  • If provisional comes off often, do not only switch to “stickier forever” without fixing prep form or contact/occlusion problems.

F5. FPD provisionals and long‑span issues

  • Multi‑unit provisional must maintain abutment positions and pontic space — a broken connector lets teeth drift and the final bridge will not seat.
  • Reinforce long‑span temps (wire, fiber) when the stem describes repeated fractures.
  • Pontic of provisional shapes the papilla/ridge soft tissue for ovate designs — poorly shaped pontics leave ugly final tissue architecture.
  • If the provisional is high only on the pontic, the patient may still overload abutments — check full arch contacts carefully.

F6. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (provisionals)

  • “Temp crown, food packs mesially” → open contact of provisional.
  • “Sensitive to cold under temp, margin open” → remake/reseal provisional, protect pulp.
  • “Anterior temp for 4 months, teeth shifted, final will not fit” → long provisional without maintaining contacts/occlusion / delayed definitive care.
  • “Final resin cement planned, ZOE temp cement used” → eugenol problem for resin.
  • “Bridge temp connector broken, abutments feel tight space changed” → drift risk — repair/remake provisional urgently.
  • “Overcontoured temp, bleeding papilla for weeks” → recontour or remake; inflamed tissue ruins final impression quality.

STOP 45 sec: “Provisional protects pulp, position, occlusion, gingiva, esthetics. Open contact still = food packing. High temp = trauma. Eugenol temp vs resin final = conflict. Broken multi‑unit temp → teeth drift.”

G. Common crown failures table (ALWAYS drill)

G1. Open / light proximal contact

  • Open / light proximal contact → food packing, papilla pain, caries risk, perio insult, patient says “something always stuck.”
  • ALWAYS comes on banks for crowns and for Class II fillings. Same one‑line rule.
  • Fix: adjust/remake contact; do not first jump to perio surgery for a food trap created by the restoration.
  • Check floss resistance and visual; adjacent tooth mobility and open gingival embrasures can contribute but open contact remains the classic hinge.

G2. High occlusion

  • High occlusion → post‑cement pain, percussion sensitivity, fracture of porcelain or opposing enamel/restoration, muscle/TMJ complaints, tooth feels “tall.”
  • First action when the stem fits: adjust occlusion (articulating paper, excursive checks), not automatic RCT on day one.
  • After adjustment, reassess; irreversible pulp signs still get endo path — but high bite is the free point many miss.

G3. Open margin

  • Open margin → leakage, caries, cement washout, gingivitis, bad taste/smell.
  • Management depends on severity and access: remake is honest when open and carious risk is high; polishing a true open margin does not seal it.
  • Causes: incomplete seating, distorted impression, die error, debris, poor prep, heavy contacts holding crown up.

G4. Porcelain / ceramic fracture

  • Porcelain / ceramic fracture → heavy bite, bruxism, under‑supported ceramic, trauma, poor design, thin ceramic from under‑reduction, occlusal prematurity on ceramic.
  • Protect with nightguard when parafunction is the story; repair vs remake depends on size, location, and underlying metal/zirconia support.
  • Biting hard objects and opposing wear facets are clues in stems.

G5. Loss of retention

  • Loss of retention → short prep, over‑taper, poor cement choice/technique, occlusal trauma, recurrent caries under crown, washed‑out cement.
  • If crown and tooth are intact: recement only after diagnosing why it failed — otherwise it returns next month.
  • Recurrent caries under a loose crown can force remake and possible endo/extraction decisions.

G6. Gingival inflammation and pulp symptoms

  • Gingival inflammation → overhang/overcontour, subgingival excess cement, biologic width violation, poor OH access, open margin plaque trap.
  • Remove excess cement, recontour or remake, address bio width if deep chronic inflammation without plaque‑only explanation.
  • Pulp symptoms after prep/crown → deep insult, bacterial leakage, high bite, pre‑existing pulp disease unmasked — check occlusion first when fitting, then pulp tests and radiographs as indicated.
  • Not every post‑cement ache is endo; not every ache is “just occlusion.” Use the stem clues.

G7. One‑line failure table (memorize)

  • Open contact → food packing.
  • High occlusion → adjust bite.
  • Open margin → leak / caries / remake logic.
  • Porcelain crack → load / design / thickness / bruxism.
  • Comes off → retention form / cement / caries.
  • Red gums at margin → contour / cement / bio width / OH.
  • Pain on bite early → occlusion until proven otherwise.

G8. Time course clues in stems

  • Immediate post‑cement pain on biting → high occlusion or incomplete seating / open margin pressure until proven otherwise.
  • Days to weeks of food packing → open contact or open embrasure trap; papilla may be sore and swollen.
  • Months of chronic red bleeding margin with deep placement → bio width, excess cement, or chronic plaque ledge from overcontour.
  • Years later caries at margin → open margin, cement washout, poor OH, or root caries at crown edge in xerostomia/high risk.
  • Sudden chip while eating → occlusal trauma, foreign body bite, thin ceramic, or bruxism event.
  • Always match the timeline in the stem to the most likely mechanism — banks build the clock into the story on purpose.

G9. FPD / multi‑unit failure notes (brief)

  • Bridge abutments: if one retainer loosens, decay can destroy the abutment silently under a still‑attached pontic side — patient may only notice bad taste or mobility late.
  • Connector bulk of ceramic FPDs must be adequate or connectors fracture; metal frameworks handle different connector rules — know “connectors need bulk / design.”
  • Pontic design should be cleansable (modified ridge lap / ovate concepts appear in teaching); ridge lap plaque traps cause tissue inflammation under pontics.
  • Non‑rigid connectors and pier abutments are advanced — if a basic stem appears, focus on common path, ferrule of abutments, and hygiene under pontic.

STOP 60 sec: Say the one‑line table out loud twice. “Open contact food. High adjust. Open margin leak. Porcelain load. Retention form. Red = contour cement bio width. Timeline in the stem is a clue.”

H. Biologic width, impressions, retraction

H1. Biologic width (supracrestal tissue attachment)

  • Biologic width (modern: supracrestal tissue attachment): junctional epithelium + connective tissue attachment coronal to bone — classic teaching ~2 mm combined (variable by site and person).
  • There is also sulcus depth above that complex — total space from bone to margin matters clinically when placing restorations.
  • Biologic width violation: margin placed too deep toward bone → chronic inflammation, discomfort, unpredictable bleeding tissue, bone loss patterns around the restoration — not fixed by mouthwash alone.
  • Treat by moving the margin coronally (replace restoration) or by surgically creating room (crown lengthening) / orthodontic extrusion to re‑establish space for attachment + sulcus + restoration.
  • Deep caries forcing deep margins needs a plan for attachment space before the pretty final crown.

H2. Impressions for fixed — what must be captured

  • Impression for fixed: capture the entire finish line + some unprepared tooth apical to the line, in accurate dimensionally stable material, without tears, pulls, or voids at the margin.
  • Opposing arch and bite registration complete the story for occlusion.
  • If you cannot see the margin in the mouth after retraction, the impression will not magically invent it.

H3. PVS and friends

  • PVS (addition silicone): excellent dimensional stability, widely used for definitive fixed impressions; multiple pours often acceptable; wait full set before remove; use tray adhesive for many trays.
  • Historically hydrophobic — dry field and modern surfactants/hydrophilized materials help; blood and water still ruin margin detail.
  • Polyether appears as accurate and more hydrophilic/wettable but stiffer — undercut lock risk in some stories (you will see more on Day 3 materials).
  • Alginate is for prelims/study models/opposing sometimes — not the first choice when the stem wants precision final fixed margins.

H4. Retraction and hemostasis

  • Retraction: cord (single or double cord technique), paste systems — expose finish line without shredding tissue.
  • Double cord idea (classic teaching): thin cord stays in sulcus sometimes, thicker cord retracts and is removed before inject — know spirit: control tissue, see margin.
  • Hemostasis is critical; blood ruins detail for analog and digital. Ferric sulfate, aluminum chloride, electrocautery/laser carefully as indicated — control bleeding before final wash.
  • Moisture control + dry field for many adhesive steps later; impression tray rigid and adhesive‑coated for elastomers as indicated.

H5. Digital scan — same clinical rules

  • Digital intraoral scan does not forgive blood, saliva pools, or invisible margins.
  • Soft tissue management, dry clean margin, and full capture of finish line still decide success.
  • Powder or powder‑free systems vary — concept over gadget brand.
  • If tissue collapses over margin, retract or delay scan after tissue health improves.

H6. Tissue health before final impressions

  • Inflamed, bleeding gingiva from a bad provisional will sabotage impressions and scans — fix the provisional contours/margins first, allow tissue to calm, then impress.
  • Cord packing in severely inflamed tissue increases bleeding and trauma — sequence is tissue health → retraction → impression.
  • Elective crown lengthening needs healing time before final margins and impressions in classic teaching — do not impress the day of aggressive osseous surgery for a perfect final fit story.
  • If caries is subgingival and tissues are raw after excavation, provisional and tissue control may come before the “perfect final impression” appointment.

H7. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (bio width / impressions)

  • “Crown margin near bone, chronically red, bleeds, no overhang visible” → biologic width violation high on differential.
  • “Final impression missing buccal margin detail, blood present” → re‑retract, hemostasis, remake impression.
  • “Precision fixed final material” → PVS or polyether family, not alginate alone.
  • “Scan fails to show sub‑G margin” → soft tissue not managed — same as analog problem.
  • “Bloody provisional, swollen papilla, dentist takes final PVS same day” → poor timing; control tissue first.
  • “Pull voids at margin of PVS, rest of impression fine” → inadequate retraction, movement, or early removal — remake that segment/whole as needed.

STOP 60 sec: “Bio width ~2 mm attachment idea. Violation → chronic red deep margin → lengthen or move margin up. PVS final common. Retraction + hemostasis. See the margin or redo. Calm tissue before final impressions.”

I. Implants basics (high repeat)

I1. Spacing numbers you must recite cold

  • Implant → IAN (inferior alveolar nerve) safety ≈ 2 mm classic teaching distance to reduce nerve injury risk. Closer planning needs advanced imaging and caution — banks want ~2 mm.
  • Implant → adjacent tooth ≈ 1.5 mm classic teaching to preserve bone and periodontium of the natural tooth and avoid damage.
  • Implant → implant ≈ 3 mm classic teaching between platforms for inter‑implant bone and papilla considerations (systems vary; know the classic numbers).
  • Vertical bone and prosthetic space also matter clinically; exam often hammers the three horizontal/safety numbers above plus medical risk.
  • Memory chain: 2 · 1.5 · ~3 = IAN · tooth · implant–implant.

I2. Peri‑implant mucositis vs peri‑implantitis

  • Peri‑implant mucositis = reversible inflammation of soft tissue around implant, no progressive bone loss beyond initial remodeling — gingivitis analog.
  • Peri‑implantitis = inflammation + progressive loss of supporting bone — periodontitis analog around implant; harder to manage, can lead to implant loss.
  • Bleeding on probing, redness, swelling appear in both; bone loss progressive on serial radiographs separates implantitis.
  • Treatment spirit: mucositis → mechanical biofilm control, hygiene, risk control; peri‑implantitis → more complex debridement/surgical approaches, still hygiene‑dependent — exam mainly wants the definition split.

I3. Maintenance and prosthetic design

  • Maintenance, plaque control, and cleansable prosthetic design matter as much as surgical placement.
  • Overcontoured implant crowns, excess cement on cement‑retained implant crowns, and open contacts cause peri‑implant inflammation just like natural teeth — plus cement can be catastrophic subgingival on implants.
  • Screw‑retained vs cement‑retained: cement residue risk is a classic implant restorative trap.
  • Patient must clean; design must allow floss/interdental brushes. Uncleanable beauty is future peri‑implantitis.

I4. Medical and lifestyle risk (implants)

  • Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus → higher implant failure / delayed healing teaching → prefer safer removable options (RPD) until medical control improves; do not push elective implants in poorly controlled systemic disease.
  • Smoking is a major risk factor for implant complications (often tested alongside DM) — counsel reduction/cessation; higher failure and peri‑implant disease risk.
  • Other risk themes: poor OH, history of severe periodontitis, radiation, bisphosphonate/antiresorptive contexts (more oral surgery day topics) — for Day 2, DM + smoking + distances + mucositis/itis are the free points.
  • Controlled DM with good hygiene is a different conversation than uncontrolled — stems usually say “uncontrolled” or high HbA1c clues.

I5. Placement and restorative exam angles

  • Implant questions often sit at restorative/perio border — still Day‑2 free points if distances and mucositis/itis are automatic.
  • Need bone volume and keratinized tissue considerations for long‑term health; narrow ridges may need grafting stories (know existence; not full surgical atlas today).
  • Immediate vs delayed loading is advanced — if a stem is basic, stick to safety distances and disease definitions.
  • Single missing tooth with healthy neighbors and good bone: implant is often ideal when systemic factors OK; uncontrolled DM flips priority to control + removable meantime.

I6. Probing and radiographs around implants (definition support)

  • Probing around implants can show BOP in mucositis and implantitis; baseline probing depths after prosthetic delivery help future comparison.
  • Plastic or appropriate probes are often taught to avoid scratching the implant surface — concept may appear; do not lose the mucositis vs implantitis split over probe material details.
  • Radiographs: compare to baseline after remodeling. Progressive saucer or crater bone loss + inflammation = peri‑implantitis pattern banks love.
  • Initial physiologic remodeling after placement/loading is not automatically disease — progressive loss is the keyword.
  • Mobile implant is a late failure sign (loss of osseointegration) — different from slightly mobile screw‑retained crown that needs torque check.

I7. CLASSIC MCQ patterns (implants)

  • “Implant planned near IAN canal on radiograph” → respect ≈2 mm safety.
  • “Implant too close to natural tooth, bone loss on tooth side” → violated ~1.5 mm idea.
  • “Two implants almost touching, papilla/bone loss between” → violated ~3 mm idea.
  • “Red soft tissue around implant, radiographs stable vs baseline remodeling” → mucositis.
  • “Red tissue + progressive crater bone loss on serial films” → peri‑implantitis.
  • “Uncontrolled DM wants fixed implants now” → control disease; RPD safer meantime.
  • “Cemented implant crown, deep inflamed pocket, cement on radiograph/flap” → residual cement.
  • “Smoker, bleeding implants, progressive bone loss” → peri‑implantitis risk high; smoking is major modifier.
  • “Implant crown loose but implant not mobile” → screw loosening / abutment issue until proven otherwise — not always failed osseointegration.

STOP 60 sec: “IAN 2 mm · tooth 1.5 mm · implant–implant ~3 mm · mucositis soft only · peri‑implantitis + progressive bone · bad DM → removable safer · smoking hurts implants · residual cement is an implant killer.”

J. Medical free points + exam traps

J1. Medical free points that leak into restorative days

  • Asthma pain control → prefer paracetamol (acetaminophen); caution with NSAIDs (can exacerbate some asthmatics). Classic bank free point.
  • Gingival enlargement drugs (classic triad): nifedipine (and other CCBs), phenytoin, cyclosporine. Not every swollen gum is plaque only — check meds.
  • Uncontrolled DM: healing, infection, implant risk — already in section I; also poorer perio outcomes (Day 5 will deepen).
  • Bleeding disorders / anticoagulants matter for surgery more than crown prep, but deep cord packing and flap crown lengthening still need medical history awareness.

J2. Infection control order (mixed stems still appear)

  • Always‑comes IC order: Clean → Disinfect → Sterilize. Organic debris first; you cannot sterilize dirt well.
  • May appear in mixed restorative papers — free point if automatic from Day 1.
  • Critical instruments that penetrate tissue → sterilize; noncritical surfaces → disinfect pathways — know spirit.
