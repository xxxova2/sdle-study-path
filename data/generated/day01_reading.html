B. Caries science — disease, risk, excavation (Sturdevant + classic exam core)

B1. Disease process

  • Definition you can write: dental caries is net demineralization of tooth mineral when plaque biofilm acids drop local pH below critical levels often enough and long enough that remineralization cannot keep up.
  • Enamel critical pH ≈ 5.5. Sturdevant notes that after a sucrose challenge, pH can remain below critical for roughly 20–50 minutes. Dentin and exposed root surfaces are more vulnerable once cementum/dentin is open — root caries pathways appear constantly in banks.
  • Stephan-curve / frequency hinge: fermentable carb hit → plaque pH crash within minutes → saliva buffers toward neutral over tens of minutes. Frequent snacks restart the attack before recovery finishes — higher risk than one dessert with a long recovery window. Exam distractor: “only total sugar grams matter.”
  • Classic organisms: Streptococcus mutans / sobrinus (initiation, acidogenic + aciduric); Lactobacillus associated with progression in established lesions; root caries flora more mixed (Actinomyces and others). You do not need a micro atlas — you need mutans + acid + frequency.
  • Four pillars (Keyes-type model): susceptible tooth surface · cariogenic biofilm · fermentable carbohydrates · time. Remove or weaken any pillar and risk falls. Sealants attack the susceptible pit; fluoride and saliva attack demineralization chemistry; diet attacks substrate; OH attacks biofilm mass.
  • Why this is operative, not “just pedo”: adult secondary caries, root caries in xerostomia, and residual infected dentin decisions all reuse the same biology.

B2. Fluoride, saliva, risk-driven care

  • Fluoride mechanisms banks accept: (1) enhances remineralization when calcium/phosphate available, (2) forms a more acid-resistant surface chemistry, (3) at high local concentrations interferes with bacterial enzymes/metabolism (varnish, SDF, prescription pastes).
  • Multiple fluoride layers: Sturdevant links population caries decline to water fluoridation and expanded use of topical fluorides, mouthrinses, and professional applications — concept: more exposure pathways beat a single “I brush sometimes.”
  • Saliva is a free protective factor: sugar and acid clearance, bicarbonate buffering, calcium/phosphate for remineralization, antimicrobial components, lubrication. Xerostomia from meds, head/neck radiotherapy, Sjögren, or chronic mouth breathing = high caries until you change risk, not only drill.
  • Risk assessment mindset (CAMBRA-style thinking): low vs moderate vs high/extreme changes prevention intensity and recall interval. High risk: varnish, high-F toothpaste concepts, diet counseling, shorter recalls, sealants — not “watch and pray.”
  • Protective factors exam loves: saliva flow, fluoride exposure, sealants, effective OH, low sugar frequency, intact sealed restorations/margins.

B3. Clinical appearance, infected vs affected, excavation sequence

  • White spot (non-cavitated enamel): first-line is risk control + fluoride remineralization + OH/diet when lesion is cleansable and risk is manageable — not automatic MOD prep. Cavitation or non-cleansable breakdown → restorative consideration.
  • Active vs arrested clues: active often soft/leathery, matte, plaque-covered, light/yellow-brown; arrested often dark, hard, shiny, cleansable. Arrested cleansable lesions may be monitored (document). Do not restore color alone.
  • Infected dentin (outer carious dentin): soft, wet, bacteria-rich, poor remineralization capacity → remove.
  • Affected dentin (inner): demineralized but firmer collagen framework, fewer bacteria, can remineralize under a sealed restoration → may preserve selectively over pulp. Sturdevant teaching language: infected requires removal; affected may not — this is the selective/stepwise hinge.
  • Peripheral seal gold rule (write this on your wall): clean the DEJ and cavity margins thoroughly even when the deepest pulpal/axial floor is managed carefully. A leaky periphery causes secondary caries and pulp death. Never leave soft infected mush at the margin “to save the pulp.” Pulp lives under a seal, not under garbage at the edge.
  • Excavation sequence stems describe: access → outline toward sound enamel where possible → remove soft infected dentin from the periphery first → approach the pulp last with care. Near pulp + no irreversible symptoms → firm affected dentin + liner + sealed restoration may beat heroic complete excavation that guarantees exposure.
  • If exposure happens: reassess history (lingering thermal? spontaneous pain?) + hemorrhage control + rubber dam → decide direct pulp cap vs endodontic therapy. Banks often prefer MTA/bioceramic language for appropriate capping/repair scenarios.

B4. Diagnosis tools and secondary caries

  • Bitewing radiographs: best routine view for proximal posterior caries. Radiolucency suggests demineralization; radiograph often underestimates true clinical extent — do not ignore a dentin-depth BW because the occlusal “looks fine.”
  • Hidden occlusal caries: can sit under intact-looking enamel. Sharp explorer stabbing is outdated. Use visual inspection after drying, good light, and appropriate radiographs.
  • Activity + cavitation thinking (ICDAS-style): non-cavitated vs micro-cavitation vs frank cavitation drives whether prevention alone is enough.
  • Secondary caries: almost always at margins (microleakage, overhang, open contact, plaque stagnation, poor OH) — not magic failure in the bulk center of a perfect seal. Operative literature (including Kidd-type teaching cited in the field) centers margin integrity.
  • Special therapies: SDF arrests lesions and blackens permanently — consent. Sealants prevent pit/fissure disease; PRR conservatively treats discrete fissure caries then seals. ART uses hand excavation + high-viscosity GIC (chemical bond + fluoride) when resources or cooperation limit full restorative care.

C. Isolation — rubber dam as free points (Sturdevant clinical setup)

  • Why isolate: moisture control, visibility, soft-tissue retraction, reduced aspiration/swallow risk for instruments and burs, cleaner field for adhesive procedures. Sturdevant discusses isolating the oral environment with rubber dam or cotton rolls — for bonded composite and many pulp procedures, dam is the exam default when feasible.
  • Shade before dam: for tooth-colored work, determine shade before isolation and preparation. Dehydrated teeth lighten and mislead shade selection.
  • Hole spacing — highest-yield dam family: holes too far apart → excess dam between teeth → wrinkles/folds → saliva leak and bond contamination. Holes too close → dam stretched tight → tear, snap, or pull off clamps — also leaks.
  • Hole size: too small tears on stretch; too large leaks around the cervical. Match punch size to tooth (incisor vs molar concept).
  • Clamp discipline: stable four-point contact when possible; floss ligature through the clamp bow before seating — classic prevention against swallow/aspiration if the clamp flies.
  • Placement sequence ideas: clamp then stretch dam (or dam-first techniques) → floss dam through contacts → invert dam edge into the sulcus with air/instrument for seal → frame holds dam off the face.
  • Limitations without abandoning the principle: latex allergy → non-latex dam, not “skip isolation.” Partially erupted thirds, fixed ortho, severe gag — know alternatives (careful cotton + suction + retraction devices) but still aim for a dry field when bonding.
  • Dam is not anesthesia: isolation ≠ pain control. Cotton rolls alone are weak for Class II bonding and endo compared with dam in bank contrasts.
  • Bond failure after “cotton only” Class II: moisture contamination sits high on the differential — isolation failure is a stem, not a footnote.

STOP 45 sec: “Wrinkles = holes too far · tears = too close/small · floss the clamp · shade before dam · invert for seal · dam ≠ LA.”

D. Tooth preparation fundamentals — classes + forms (Sturdevant Ch. 6 logic)

  • Nomenclature: Sturdevant replaces older “cavity preparation” language with tooth preparation, still organized as a two-stage procedure: initial stages then final stages.
  • Class I — pits and fissures: occlusal of posteriors, buccal pits of molars, lingual pits of upper incisors.
  • Class II — proximal surfaces of posteriors (MO, DO, MOD).
  • Class III — proximal anteriors without involving the incisal edge.
  • Class IV — proximal anteriors plus the incisal edge (trauma classic).
  • Class V — gingival/cervical third of facial or lingual of any tooth (caries, abfraction, erosion, root surface).
  • Class VI — cusp tip or incisal edge only (wear/attrition).

D1. Form vocabulary — say these cold

  • Outline form: external shape of the preparation on the tooth surface. Modern teaching is disease-driven and conservative — not “extension for prevention” as an automatic rule for every composite.
  • Primary resistance form: features that keep the tooth and restoration from fracturing under load — adequate bulk of material, rounded internal stress risers, covering unsupported cusps when needed.
  • Primary retention form: features that keep the restoration from dislodging — slightly converging walls, grooves, slots for amalgam; micromechanical and chemical adhesion for bonded materials.
  • Convenience form: access so you can see, instrument, remove caries, and place material properly.
  • Final stages (concept stack): removal of remaining infected dentin, pulp protection as indicated, finishing of enamel walls, and cleaning of the preparation (“toilet of the cavity” in older language).
  • Unsupported enamel rods at the margin: remove them — they fracture under load and open the interface.
  • Axiopulpal line angle: round it in amalgam Class II thinking to reduce stress concentration and restoration fracture at the isthmus.
  • Isthmus width: too wide weakens remaining cusps. If a cusp is undermined, coverage strategies beat a huge MOD “hope.”

D2. Amalgam vs composite philosophy (high-yield contrast)

  • Amalgam: no reliable chemical bond to tooth structure → depends on mechanical retention and resistance; needs bulk; cavosurface often taught near a butt joint (~90°) so thin feather edges do not chip. Sturdevant notes that even when amalgam bonding agents are used, conventional resistance and retention features still matter — bonding is not a free pass to ignore form.
  • Composite: bonds via adhesive systems → more conservative outlines are possible; enamel bevels (especially anterior) increase enamel rod-end area and blend color over roughly a half-millimeter to millimeter transition in teaching illustrations. Do not copy amalgam outline “because Black said so.”
  • Class II matrix and wedge: create contact, contour, and gingival seal. Overhang = plaque trap. Open contact = food packing (same clinical pain as crown open contacts on Day 2).
  • Class III/IV: shade before dam; enamel bevel common; sometimes lingual access preserves facial enamel for esthetics.
  • Class V: often root surface — GIC/RMGI or adhesive composite depending on isolation, esthetics, and caries risk; isolation is hard near gingiva — retraction/cord/dam when possible.
  • Minimal intervention tools: selective caries removal, sealants, PRR, slot/tunnel where indicated — still respect peripheral seal always.

STOP 45 sec: “Outline · resistance · retention · convenience · amalgam = bulk + mechanical · composite = bond + conservative · round axiopulpal · clean unsupported enamel.”

E. Bonding science — smear layer and hybrid layer (Sturdevant histology/adhesion core)

  • Smear layer: when you cut dentin, a few-micrometers-thick layer of debris forms — denatured collagen, hydroxyapatite, and cutting debris. It forces material into tubules as smear plugs and reduces permeability. Attachment of smear to underlying dentin is relatively weak.
  • Why adhesives engage it: most etch-and-rinse and many self-etch strategies remove, solubilize, or modify the smear layer and partially demineralize intertubular dentin so resin can penetrate collagen.
  • Hybrid layer: the resin interdiffusion zone — resin infiltrating demineralized collagen. Sturdevant-linked teaching (including Pashley-type descriptions) stresses that the primary attachment mechanism to dentin is mechanical interlocking via this hybrid layer, not pure chemical “glue.”
  • Enamel etch: creates microretention for sealants and composites; rod orientation affects etch patterns. Enamel bonding is generally more predictable than deep dentin bonding.
  • Deep dentin challenge: more tubules, more water, more technique sensitivity. Over-drying after etch-and-rinse can collapse collagen and block resin infiltration (moist bonding concepts in many systems).
  • Microleakage: bacterial products and fluid movement through imperfect interfaces → sensitivity, pulp inflammation, secondary caries. Seal is pulp protection.
  • Eugenol warning: zinc oxide–eugenol pathways can inhibit resin polymerization. Do not leave eugenol materials under a final bonded composite without an appropriate barrier or replacement strategy.
  • Clinical checklist before light-cure: isolation confirmed · adhesive steps completed per system · no pooled water or saliva at margins · increments sized for cure depth.

STOP 45 sec: “Smear = cutting debris + plugs · hybrid = resin + collagen interlock · seal protects pulp · no eugenol under composite.”

F. Composite materials, polymerization, and C-factor

  • Composite structure: resin matrix + filler particles + silane coupling. Light-cured materials dominate modern exam scenarios. Filler load and particle size influence strength, wear, and polish.
  • Polymerization shrinkage: after the gel point, shrinkage creates strain on the bonded network and tooth. Stress can open margins, crack enamel rods, or leave the patient with sensitivity.
  • C-factor (configuration factor): ratio of bonded surfaces to unbonded surfaces. High C-factor configurations (deep Class I box with many walls bonded and little free surface) generate more stress risk. Lower C-factor (Class IV with free surfaces) is more favorable. Sturdevant discusses C-factor with polymerization stress management and incremental strategies.
  • Clinical counters: incremental placement, attention to cure, soft-start concepts where taught, correct adhesive protocol, avoid careless bulk-fill of deep high-C boxes, ensure the light actually reaches the material (distance, angle, debris on tip).
  • Indications hinge: posterior composite when isolation is achievable, occlusion is appropriate, and patient selection fits. Contraindication signal on banks: cannot control moisture.
  • Anterior bevel benefit: blends color transition across the margin and increases enamel surface for bonding — esthetic and mechanical teaching point.
  • Repair vs replace: modern operative includes repair of composite in selected defects — still diagnose why it failed (margin, occlusion, caries) before repeating the same error.

STOP 40 sec: “C-factor = bonded/unbonded · high C = more stress · increments + dam + full cure.”

G. Pulp protection, liners, bases, and exposure decisions

  • Seal is the real pulp protector: restorations must seal against microleakage. Bonded interfaces, careful caries removal, and occlusion control protect pulp better than theatrical thick bases alone.
  • Liners and bases: still indicated when thermal or mechanical protection is needed — classic under deep metallic restorations. Under modern bonded composites, thick conventional bases are not automatic; use when thermal/mechanical indication remains.
  • Deep caries, no irreversible symptoms: leave firm affected dentin + protective liner + sealed restoration (indirect pulp / selective removal) rather than guaranteed exposure for “completeness.”
  • Direct exposure pathway: history (lingering/spontaneous?) · hemorrhage control · rubber dam · material choice (MTA/bioceramic often bank-preferred for appropriate caps) vs endodontic therapy when pulp status demands it.
  • Post-op sensitivity differential: high occlusion · open margin · adhesive technique error · overheating without coolant · cracked tooth · reversible pulpitis after deep work — not always “RCT tomorrow.”
  • Carries-control mindset: when many teeth are active, stabilize with provisional control restorations, then definitive care — pulp protection is staged, not chaotic.

STOP 40 sec: “Seal > base theater · affected may stay · exposure → history + dam + MTA/endo logic · check occlusion.”

H. Materials board + IC free points that bleed into operative stems

  • Amalgam: durable posterior workhorse historically; needs bulk + mechanical form; tested for isthmus fracture, when composite is preferred, and still for moisture-tolerant posterior scenarios in some teaching contrasts.
  • Composite: esthetic, adhesive, isolation-dependent; dominant modern direct material when field is controlled.
  • Glass ionomer / resin-modified GIC: chemical bond to tooth, fluoride release; Class V, root caries, liners, ART, sandwich techniques; weaker wear than many hybrids under heavy load.
  • GIC Type I often framed as luting cement; know indication-by-type thinking when banks ask “which GIC.”
  • Sealants: etched enamel micromechanical retention; prevention in susceptible pits/fissures; part of PRR workflows.
  • Calcium hydroxide heritage → MTA/bioceramic modern language for pulp capping and perforation repair in many packs.
  • Instrument processing always-comes: Cleaning → Disinfection → Sterilization. Debris must be removed before sterilization can be effective. Light handles and cure tips need barriers or disinfection between patients. High-speed without coolant burns pulp — technique stem.
  • End-of-section write gate: list eight rules without looking: pH 5.5 · frequency · infected/affected · clean DEJ · dam wrinkles · hybrid layer · C-factor · no eugenol under composite.

STOP 30 sec: Name three Sturdevant ideas for every MCQ today: hybrid layer · C-factor · clean DEJ/seal.

  • Adhesive system families (exam map): etch-and-rinse (total-etch) vs self-etch vs universal systems. You do not memorize every brand. You know: follow the system’s wetness rules, do not mix steps from two kits, and isolation still decides success more than marketing claims.
  • Hybrid layer failure modes to name: incomplete resin infiltration (over-dry collagen collapse), moisture contamination, premature light exposure of adhesive pools, and leaving thick unpolymerized oxygen-inhibited mess without a plan. Banks phrase these as post-op sensitivity or white-line margins.
  • Enamel vs dentin priorities: maximize enamel margins when possible for more predictable bonding. On root dentin Class V, expect more technique sensitivity — GIC/RMGI often appears because of chemical adhesion and fluoride in high-risk root surfaces.
  • Smear plugs tradeoff: plugs reduce permeability (can temporarily decrease sensitivity) but block resin tags if not modified. Modern systems intentionally modify smear so hybrid layer forms — “leave smear forever untouched” is not the modern adhesive story for etch-and-rinse pathways.
  • Light-curing discipline: keep the tip close and clean, cure in increments appropriate to shade and depth, and do not assume a 1-second wave cures a deep box. Under-cured composite at the floor is a leak and sensitivity factory.
  • Incremental strategies linked to C-factor: horizontal, oblique, or cusp-building increments can leave free surfaces during cure to lower effective stress. Exact geometry matters less on the exam than the principle: do not lock a high-C box in one bulk shrink event when the stem screams bulk-fill failure.
  • Matrix systems for Class II: sectional matrix + wedge + ring often improve contact vs loose circumferential matrix for composite. Contact and contour failures become food-packing stems that look like “prostho” but start in operative.
  • Finishing and occlusion: after cure, remove flash, check occlusion in MI and excursions, polish margins. High restoration is the most common “mystery sensitivity” after an otherwise fine bond.
  • Liners that make sense: thin protective liners on deep dentin when indicated; RMGI liners in sandwich techniques; avoid stacking random bases that steal adhesive surface without a reason.
  • When pulp status overrides cosmetics: spontaneous pain, lingering thermal pain, or necrotic signs move the plan toward endodontic assessment — a pretty composite on a dying pulp is not a pass strategy.
  • Always-comes IC overlap: dirty instruments cannot be sterilized into cleanliness. Cleaning removes bioburden; disinfection reduces microbes on surfaces; sterilization kills spores on critical instruments. Operative trays live in that world every day.
  • Handpiece water coolant: inadequate coolant while cutting deep dentin is a thermal pulp injury stem. Speed without water is not “efficient”; it is iatrogenic.
  • Repair decision tree: marginal ditch with no active caries may be repaired; recurrent caries under a failing interface needs disease control first. Replace when the restoration cannot be sealed or the tooth’s resistance form is gone.
  • Patient communication hinges: explain blackening with SDF before application; explain that white-spot monitoring is treatment, not neglect; explain that rubber dam protects them, not only you.
  • Link forward to Day 2 without studying it: open contacts and poor emergence after crowns reuse today’s contact/contour logic. Ferrule and TOC wait until tomorrow — today ends at the filling and the seal.
  • Self-check before quizzes: can you draw C-factor as a fraction? Can you define hybrid layer in eight words? Can you state infected vs affected in one sentence? If no, re-read E–G once more, slowly, out loud.
  • Final Block B recap (say it): hybrid layer is resin in collagen · C-factor is bonded over unbonded · high C needs increments · seal protects pulp more than thick base theater · GIC gives chemical bond and fluoride on roots · cleaning before disinfection before sterilization · high occlusion after composite is a sensitivity stem · eugenol and composite do not share a bed without a barrier.
  • One more write line: “Moisture is the enemy of the hybrid layer.” Put that on your wrong-book header for the whole restorative week.
