B. Caries science (must know cold)

B1. Disease process

  • Caries = net demineralization of tooth mineral when plaque biofilm acids drop local pH below critical levels for long enough / often enough.
  • Enamel critical pH ≈ 5.5. Dentin / cementum critical pH is higher (~6.0–6.2) → root surfaces demineralize more easily once exposed (aging, recession, perio).
  • Stephan curve: fermentable carb challenge → plaque pH crash within minutes → saliva buffers pH back toward neutral over ~20–40 min (varies). Frequency of sugar hits keeps pH low more of the day → higher risk than one big sweet meal with long recovery windows.
  • Key organisms classically linked: S. mutans / sobrinus (initiation, acidogenic + aciduric), Lactobacillus (progression in established lesions), root caries flora more mixed (Actinomyces etc.). Exam still loves mutans + acid + sugar frequency.
  • Four classic factors (Keyes‑type): susceptible tooth · biofilm · fermentable carbs · time. Remove any pillar → risk falls.

B2. Fluoride, saliva, risk

  • Fluoride: (1) enhances remineralization, (2) forms more acid‑resistant surface (fluorapatite‑type resistance), (3) interferes with bacterial enzymes/metabolism at high local levels (varnish, SDF, Rx pastes).
  • Community water F + toothpaste = baseline. High risk → add varnish, Rx high‑F toothpaste (e.g. 5000 ppm concepts), diet counseling, shorter recalls — not “drill everything.”
  • Saliva: clearance of sugars/acids, bicarbonate buffer, Ca/PO₄ for remineralization, antimicrobials, lubrication. Xerostomia = high caries (meds, head/neck RT, Sjögren, mouth breathing patterns).
  • CAMBRA / risk assessment: low vs moderate vs high/extreme drives prevention intensity and recall interval — not one protocol for all.
  • Protective factors exam loves: saliva, F exposure, sealants, good OH, low sugar frequency, intact restorations/margins.

B3. Clinical appearance & activity

  • White spot (non‑cavitated enamel): first‑line = risk control + F remineralization + OH/diet — not automatic aggressive prep. Cavitation or non‑cleansable breakdown → restorative consideration.
  • Active lesion often: soft/leathery, matte, light/yellow‑brown, plaque‑covered, sensitive. Arrested often: dark, hard, shiny, cleansable — may not need restoration if truly arrested and cleansable (case‑by‑case; document).
  • Infected dentin (outer carious dentin): soft, wet, bacteria‑rich, no capacity to remineralize well → remove.
  • Affected dentin (inner): harder, demineralized, fewer bacteria, collagen framework can remineralize under a sealed restoration → may preserve selectively over pulp (modern selective caries removal / stepwise concepts).
  • Peripheral seal rule (exam gold): clean the DEJ and cavity margins thoroughly even when the deepest pulpal/axial floor is managed selectively. A leaky periphery = secondary caries and pulp death.
  • Never leave soft infected mush at the margin “to save pulp.” Pulp lives under a seal, not under garbage at the edge.

B4. Diagnosis tools

  • Bitewings: best routine view for proximal posterior caries. Radiolucency in enamel/dentin suggests demineralization — radiograph often underestimates true clinical extent.
  • Occlusal caries can hide under intact‑looking enamel (hidden caries) — sharp explorer stabbing is outdated; use visual + dry + good light ± BW/PA as indicated.
  • ICDAS‑style thinking: non‑cavitated vs micro‑cavitation vs frank cavitation drives whether prevention alone is enough.
  • Transillumination, temporary tooth separation, and careful floss/catch history help proximal decisions when BW is ambiguous.

B5. Special therapies banks love

  • SDF (silver diamine fluoride): arrests caries; blackens lesion permanently — always consent (esthetics). Huge for pedo, special needs, geriatric, and interim arrest when definitive care delayed.
  • Sealants: prevention on susceptible pits/fissures; also part of PRR (preventive resin restoration) when only discrete fissure caries is cleaned and sealed/composite‑restored conservatively.
  • ART: hand excavation + high‑viscosity GIC — field/public health / limited resources; still tests GIC chemical bond + F release logic.
  • Secondary caries = almost always at margins (microleakage, overhang, open contact, poor OH) — not “magic failure in the middle of a perfect seal.”

B6. Excavation sequence (how stems describe the visit)

  • Access → outline to sound enamel where possible → remove soft infected dentin from periphery first → approach pulp last with care.
  • If near pulp and tooth has no irreversible symptoms: consider leaving firm affected dentin + liner + sealed restoration (indirect pulp / selective removal) rather than heroic complete excavation that guarantees exposure.
  • If exposure happens: reassess history (lingering pain? spontaneous?) + bleeding control + dam → decide cap vs endo (section G).
  • After restore: check occlusion, contacts, margins; document material and pulp status plan.

B7. Exam stem patterns (caries)

  • “White chalky lesion after drying, no cavitation, low risk” → F + OH + diet, not MOD prep.
  • “Soft light dentin at DEJ, hard dark near pulp, asymptomatic” → clean DEJ; may leave hard affected deep + seal.
  • “Root surface lesion, high caries risk, poor isolation” → GIC/RMGIC or SDF arrest pathways often appear.
  • “Radiolucency into dentin on BW, clinically intact occlusal” → still may need investigation; BW underestimates; do not ignore.
  • “Child/elderly, many lesions, limited coop” → SDF consent blackening + prioritized definitive care.

C. Isolation — rubber dam (free points)

C1. Why dam is not optional on exam stems

  • Dam = moisture control + visibility + soft‑tissue retraction + aspiration/swallow protection for instruments/burs/files + infection control aerosol reduction.
  • Bonded composite, endo, and many pulp‑cap scenarios expect dry field. Cotton rolls alone are weak for Class II composite bonding and endo — banks contrast dam vs rolls.
  • Dam does not replace local anesthesia when pain control is needed. Isolation ≠ analgesia.

C2. Hole punch spacing (CLASSIC MCQ family)

  • Holes too far apart → excess dam between teeth → wrinkles / folds between teeth → poor seal, saliva leak, material contamination.
  • Holes too close → dam stretched tight between teeth → tear, snap, or pull off clamps · also leaks.
  • Correct spacing ≈ follows average interdental distance so dam sits flat without bagging or ripping.
  • Punch size: too small hole tears; too large hole leaks around neck of tooth. Match punch to tooth size (incisor vs molar charts exist — concept > memorizing every number).

C3. Clamp, invert, ligature

  • Clamp selection: stable four‑point contact on tooth when possible; winged vs wingless for placement style; retainers for partially erupted / odd morphology exist.
  • Floss ligature through clamp bow (or secure tether) before placing in mouth = prevent swallow/aspirate if clamp flies. Same logic as floss on matrices sometimes.
  • Place clamp → stretch dam over (or dam‑first techniques) → floss dam through contacts → invert dam edge into sulcus with air/plastic instrument for seal.
  • Frame holds dam off face; napkin under dam for comfort/absorbent layer.
  • If clamp traumatizes gingiva heavily or will not seat without crushing, reassess tooth morphology / use alternate isolation carefully — still aim for dry field for bonding.

C4. When stems push “no dam”

  • Some third molars, fixed ortho, extreme gag, latex allergy (use non‑latex dam), partially erupted teeth — know limitations and alternatives (Isolite‑type devices, careful cotton + suction + retraction) but default exam answer for bonding/endo is still dam when feasible.
  • Latex allergy → non‑latex dam, not “skip isolation.”

C5. Exam stem patterns (isolation)

  • “Dam wrinkles between the two teeth being restored” → holes too far apart.
  • “Dam tears as soon as stretched over clamp” → holes too small / too close / sharp clamp wings / dry dam — banks favor spacing or hole size logic.
  • “Clamp disappeared / patient coughing” → missing floss ligature on clamp (prevention question).
  • “Bond failed / white line / sensitivity after Class II composite, cotton only used” → isolation failure / moisture contamination high on differential.
  • “Patient latex allergy, need Class IV” → non‑latex dam + still isolate.

STOP 45 sec: “Wrinkles = holes too far. Tears/stretch = holes too close. Invert for seal. Floss the clamp. Dam ≠ LA.”

Teach‑back (30 sec): Explain to an imaginary junior: why dam hole spacing changes the look of the dam between teeth.

D. G.V. Black classes + prep principles

D1. Classification (flash until automatic)

  • Class I — pits & fissures: occlusal of posteriors, buccal pits of molars, lingual pits of upper incisors.
  • Class II — proximal surfaces of posteriors (MO, DO, MOD…).
  • Class III — proximal of anteriors without involving the incisal edge.
  • Class IV — proximal of anteriors plus incisal edge (trauma classic).
  • Class V — gingival/cervical third of facial or lingual (any tooth) — abfraction/erosion/root caries stories.
  • Class VI — cusp tip or incisal edge only (wear/attrition). Less common but still tested.

D2. Black’s steps / form vocabulary

  • Outline form — external shape of the prep on the tooth surface (follows pits/fissures/caries; modern = conservative, disease‑driven).
  • Resistance form — features that keep the tooth and restoration from fracturing under load (adequate bulk, rounded internal stress points, cusp coverage when undermined).
  • Retention form — features that keep the restoration from dislodging (converging walls for amalgam, micromechanical/chemical bond for adhesive materials, grooves/slots when needed).
  • Convenience form — access so you can see, instrument, and remove caries/place material properly.
  • Caries removal / finish of walls / toilet of cavity — clean, inspect, rinse; remove unsupported enamel.

D3. Amalgam vs composite prep philosophy (high yield contrast)

  • Amalgam: no chemical bond to tooth → needs mechanical retention (slightly converging walls, grooves/slots/pins historically). Needs bulk (adequate depth/isthmus) or it fractures. Cavosurface often ~90° (butt joint) — thin feather edges of amalgam or unsupported enamel chips.
  • Composite: bonds via adhesive system → can be more conservative. Enamel margins often beveled (especially anterior) to expose more rod ends + blend esthetics. Do not copy amalgam outline just because “Black said so.”
  • Round axiopulpal line angle in amalgam Class II → reduces stress concentration / restoration fracture.
  • Isthmus width: too wide → weakens remaining cusps. If cusp is undermined/unsupported → onlay or crown coverage, not a huge MOD “hope.”
  • Unsupported enamel rods at the margin → remove (will fracture later under load/occlusion).

D4. Class‑specific traps

  • Class II: need matrix + wedge for contact/contour and gingival seal; overhang = plaque trap; open contact = food packing (same logic as crowns Day 2).
  • Class III/IV: shade before dam; enamel bevel common; lingual access sometimes preferred to save facial enamel for esthetics when possible.
  • Class V: often root surface — GIC/RMGIC or adhesive composite depending on isolation, esthetics, caries risk; isolation hard near gingiva — retraction/cord/dam when possible.
  • Modern minimal intervention: selective caries removal, tunnel/slot where indicated, PRR, sealants — still respect peripheral seal.

D5. Depth, width, and “how deep is deep”

  • Ideal amalgam depth historically into dentin for bulk (~1.5–2 mm occlusal concepts appear in teaching) — exam cares more that amalgam needs bulk and that pulp is respected than a single magic number.
  • Composite can be thinner in enamel‑only preventive resins; still cure fully in increments for deeper boxes.
  • Axial depth in Class II follows caries; do not overcut toward pulp “to make a pretty box.”
  • Bevel enamel for composite where it helps rods/esthetics; do not bevel gingival cementum/dentin margins the same way (little enamel rod benefit; thin flash risk).

D6. Exam stem patterns (prep/class)

  • “Caries only in mesial of #46, intact occlusal” → Class II (proximal posterior), not Class I.
  • “Facial cervical of #11, soft” → Class V.
  • “Trauma chipped mesial‑incisal #21” → Class IV.
  • “Amalgam restoration fractured at isthmus” → inadequate bulk / sharp axiopulpal / heavy occlusion / too wide thin isthmus — resistance form failure.
  • “Composite Class III without bevel, open margin stain” → enamel prep/bond geometry issues common in stems.
  • “Huge MOD, both cusps undermined” → cusp coverage (onlay/crown), not endless direct fill hope.

E. Materials map (composite · amalgam · GIC)

E1. Composite types

  • Microfill: tiny fillers → best polish/esthetics, but weaker under heavy occlusal load. Classic failure: pretty Class IV/anterior that chips → replace stress areas with hybrid/nanohybrid.
  • Hybrid / nanohybrid / nanofill: strength + polish balance — daily workhorse for most Class I–II and many anteriors.
  • Flowable: lower filler % → lower viscosity, higher polymerization shrinkage stress risk — use as liners, small repairs, pit seal adjuncts — not bulk stress‑bearing MOD alone.
  • Packable (historical marketing): stiffer handling; still follow incremental curing rules.
  • C‑factor = bonded surfaces ÷ unbonded surfaces. High C‑factor (deep boxy Class I) → high shrinkage stress on walls → gap risk. Fight with increments (~2 mm classical), oblique layering, proper light cure, soft‑start/pulse concepts product‑dependent.
  • Oxygen inhibition layer: sticky air‑inhibited surface helps next increment bond chemically; final polish removes it. Glycerin gel + final cure can fully harden the outer surface.
  • Shade: take before rubber dam on moist tooth under natural/neutral light — dehydration whitens teeth and misleads shade.
  • Failures banks love: moisture contamination, undercure (deep increments), high occlusion, open margin, overhang, secondary caries at margin, wrong material (microfill in load zone).

E2. Bonding systems

  • Smear layer = cutting debris on dentin/enamel after prep.
  • Etch‑and‑rinse (total‑etch): phosphoric acid creates microretention; enamel loves it. On dentin use wet bonding — do not desiccate collagen into a collapsed mat that resin cannot infiltrate.
  • Self‑etch: acidic primers modify smear; fewer rinse steps; less postop sensitivity in some stories; enamel bond often stronger if enamel is still selectively etched.
  • Selective enamel etch: H₃PO₄ on enamel only + self‑etch/universal on dentin — very common modern “best of both” answer.
  • Universal adhesives: flexible mode (self‑etch / selective / total) — follow the product logic the stem implies; isolation still rules.
  • Hybrid layer = resin‑infiltrated demineralized dentin. Over‑etch dentin, overdry, or saliva on etched enamel → weak bond / nanoleakage → re‑isolate and re‑etch as needed.
  • 10‑MDP: chemical adhesion to hydroxyapatite / zirconia / many metals in primers — appears in prostho cement stems too.
  • Eugenol (ZOE, IRM): can inhibit free‑radical resin polymerization. Do not leave eugenol temp under planned composite or resin cement without thorough cleanup / non‑eugenol alternatives.

E3. Amalgam

  • Still valid for large load‑bearing restorations, moisture‑challenging fields, and durability when isolation/bonding is unreliable.
  • High‑copper alloys reduce the weak corrosive gamma‑2 (Sn‑Hg) phase → better corrosion resistance + lower creep.
  • Creep = slow deformation under load → marginal ditching risk over years.
  • Galvanism: dissimilar metals + saliva electrolyte → sharp electric pain when they contact.
  • Retention = mechanical undercut/form, not chemical bond like GIC. Converging walls, boxes, slots — pins less favored (perforation/pulp risk).
  • Condensation, carving anatomy/contacts, check occlusion. Polish often delayed until full set per material timing.
  • Mercury hygiene: scrap in closed containers, amalgam separators, never household vacuum on spills, no heating scrap.

E4. GIC / RMGIC

  • Conventional GIC: chemical (ionic) bond to tooth + fluoride release/recharge concepts. Weaker fracture toughness under heavy occlusal load than hybrid composite.
  • RMGIC: resin‑modified — better early strength/handling in many uses; still not a universal bruxist MOD hero alone.
  • Great indications: high caries risk, Class V/root, liner/base, sandwich under composite, ART, pediatric temporary/definitive in selected cases.
  • Protect young GIC from early water contamination or desiccation (crazing) per product (coat/varnish).
  • Open sandwich: GIC exposed at gingival margin under composite — F + seal when enamel missing at cervix. Closed sandwich: GIC fully covered by composite.

E5. Light cure + handling traps

  • Cure from accessible angles; deep boxes may need longer times / smaller increments — undercure = soft deep composite + leakage + toxicity risk myths on exam as “incomplete polymerization.”
  • Darker/more opaque shades need more attention to increment thickness and cure time.
  • Hold light steady and close; dirty light tip reduces output — barriers and clean tip every patient (links to IC).
  • Radiometer concepts: offices should verify light output periodically (stem: “recurrent failure, light never checked”).

E6. Exam stem patterns (materials)

  • “Beautiful polish, fractures on working cusp” → was microfill → hybrid.
  • “Postop sensitivity after total etch, dentin desiccated” → wet bonding / overdry failure.
  • “Temp IRM last month, now composite fails to set / soft” → eugenol inhibition.
  • “High caries risk Class V root” → GIC/RMGIC often preferred over pure esthetic composite stories.
  • “Deep Class I bulk filled in one shot, gap on BW” → C‑factor / shrinkage / no increments.

STOP 60 sec: “Microfill pretty/weak · C‑factor increments · wet bonding · selective etch · GIC = chemical+F · eugenol kills resin · high‑copper kills gamma‑2.”

F. Class II matrix game (exam loves this)

  • Matrix band restores missing wall so you can pack material to proper contour + contact.
  • Wedge: seals gingival margin (anti‑overhang), slightly separates teeth for tighter contact after removal, protects papilla.
  • Overhang → plaque trap → gingivitis, secondary caries, bone loss. Classic BW finding after bad Class II.
  • Open / light contact → food packing, pain, perio insult (same logic as open crown contact on Day 2).
  • Sectional matrix + separating ring often gives more anatomic posterior composite contacts than a flat Tofflemire circumferential band (still know Tofflemire for amalgam/many stems).
  • After place: floss check (shreds = flash/rough/overhang), articulating paper in MIP + excursions, consider BW if suspicious of overhang/void.
  • Burnish band to adjacent tooth contour before packing when technique calls for it; remove matrix carefully without breaking marginal ridge.

F2. Class II stem patterns

  • “BW shows ledge of radiopaque material at mesial #46 under bone level” → overhang → replace/reshape; perio risk.
  • “Patient packs food between new MO composite and premolar” → open/light contact → redo contact (matrix/ring/wedge technique).
  • “Floss shreds after Class II” → flash, rough margin, or overhang ledge — finish margins, not “just polish occlusal.”
  • “Gingival stain line at box after 1 year” → microleakage/contamination at gingival margin (isolation + matrix seal failure classic).
  • “Amalgam Class II with sharp internal axiopulpal, restoration cracks” → resistance form failure — round axiopulpal.

STOP 30 sec: “Matrix = contour/contact · wedge = gingival seal + separation · overhang and open contact both fail the patient.”

G. Pulp protection (very high yield)

  • RDT (remaining dentin thickness): thinner floor over pulp → higher thermal/chemical/bacterial risk. Deep preps need respect, not “just fill.”
  • Liner = thin protective layer (e.g. CaOH/MTA/resin liner concepts by indication). Base = thicker thermal insulation / blockout under large restorations.
  • Indirect pulp therapy / selective deep caries removal: leave deepest affected dentin to avoid exposure; place biocompatible protection; deliver an excellent coronal seal — seal is half the therapy.
  • Direct pulp cap indications classically: pinpoint mechanical/traumatic exposure, clean field, healthy pulp history, controllable hemorrhage, rubber dam. Material: MTA / bioceramic modern first‑line over old CaOH‑only thinking (CaOH still appears in older stems).
  • Uncontrollable bleeding from exposure, lingering spontaneous pain, or necrotic signs → not a simple cap — pulpotomy/endo path.
  • Reversible pulpitis: brief pain to cold that resolves quickly when stimulus removed → remove cause + restore; monitor.
  • Irreversible pulpitis: lingering thermal pain, spontaneous/night pain → endo, not “bigger composite.”
  • Full crown prep removes more tooth structure → higher pulp insult risk than small Class I (carry this into Day 2).
  • Post‑op: mild cold sensitivity improving + occlusion OK → often monitor. Worsening spontaneous pain / bite pain escalating → reassess endo/occlusion/high restoration.
  • High restoration after filling → adjust occlusion first before assuming “mystery pulp death” on day one.

G2. Pulp stem patterns

  • “Pinpoint pulp blush during caries removal, no lingering history, dam on, bleed stops with pressure” → direct cap MTA/bioceramic + sealed restoration.
  • “Exposure, pulp bleeds freely 10+ min despite pressure” → inflamed pulp → not simple cap → pulpotomy/endo path.
  • “Cold lingers 2 minutes, wakes patient at night” → irreversible → endo, not bigger filling.
  • “Brief cold, stops in seconds, caries into dentin” → reversible pattern → remove cause + restore.
  • “Deep caries, asymptomatic, hard dark dentin over pulp” → selective/indirect pulp + seal often preferred to heroic exposure.
  • “Day‑2 post‑op: tooth feels high, percussion tender” → adjust occlusion first.

STOP 45 sec: “Seal saves pulp. Pinpoint clean + dam → MTA. Uncontrollable bleed/lingering pain → endo path. High bite → adjust.”

H. Infection control free points (inside operative stories)

  • Order always: Cleaning → Disinfection → Sterilization. Never put visibly dirty instruments straight into an autoclave and call it done — soil blocks sterilant.
  • Spaulding: critical (enters sterile tissue/bloodstream) → sterilize; semi‑critical (mucous membrane) → sterilize or high‑level disinfect; noncritical (intact skin) → low/intermediate disinfect.
  • Curing light guides, light handles, air/water syringes: barrier + disinfect every patient.
  • Handpieces: follow reprocessing guidelines (clean, lubricate per IFU, sterilize). DUWL biofilm management; sterile water/irrigant for surgical sites.
  • Sharps injury: wash, report, occupational protocol. Hep B vaccination for DHCP.
  • Antibiotics for routine fillings? No — only specific cardiac prophylaxis situations per current guidelines, not “toothache composite.”
